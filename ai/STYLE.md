# Writing style guide

General style guide for long-form prose in this repo: essays, design docs, public writing, devlogs. Read once before writing; follow on every line.

## Voice

- **Calm, measured, factual.** No hype. Authority comes from evidence and from being unafraid of the counter-argument.
- **Warm and humble.** Lead with what a thing *is* and *does*, not what it isn't. Praise people by name. Stand on the shoulders of giants and say so.
- **Confident, not strident.** The argument wins because it is right, not because it is loud.
- **Universal, not tribal.** No us-against-them. Adversaries are mistaken, not enemies.
- **Persuasive, not manipulative.** Use simile, metaphor, and rhythm to make the reader feel the stakes. Earn every emotional beat with a fact.

Target a voice that reads as a person, not a polished document. Where prose feels too careful, loosen by a notch. Not by half.

## Sentences

- Short over long. Concrete over abstract. Active over passive.
- A sentence that needs a comma to breathe is fine; one that needs three is two sentences pretending.
- Vary length. A long sentence that establishes context, then a short one that lands the point.
- Read every paragraph aloud. If it stumbles, cut.

### Punctuation as voice

- **Period.** Closure. Land on the loaded word and stop.
- **Comma.** Breath, parenthetical, list. Overuse muddies; underuse rushes.
- **Semicolon.** Two complete thoughts in tight relation.
- **Colon.** The reveal. Names what is to come.
- **Ellipsis.** Sparingly; only where the felt pause matters more than the clean delivery.
- **Parentheses.** Cut or integrate where the main flow can carry it.
- **Question mark.** Real questions only. Rhetorical questions become declaratives.
- **Dash.** En dash for number ranges (1939–1945). No em dashes anywhere.
- **Exclamation mark.** Forbidden except as part of a proper noun.

A comma joining two independent clauses is a splice; upgrade to semicolon, period, or rewrite.

## Forbidden tics

- **No em dashes.** Use a colon, a semicolon, a comma, or a full stop.
- **No exclamation marks.**
- **No buzzwords**: "leverage", "synergy", "disrupt", "10x", "ecosystem" (unless literal), "game-changer", "paradigm shift". If a sentence reads like a LinkedIn post, rewrite it.
- **No filler**: "it is important to note that", "needless to say", "in today's world", "at the end of the day".
- **No second-person command voice.** Show; don't instruct.
- **No first-person plural unless earned.** "We" is reader and writer together, not the author and an imaginary team.
- **No hedging stack.** "It might possibly perhaps be the case that" is one word: "is", or cut.

## AI prose tells to cut

LLMs leave a residue. The pass is not about hiding AI assistance; it is about humanising the prose so the writing carries the voice of the person who wrote it rather than the average of the corpus. Aim for recognisability, not concealment.

**Vocabulary to cut on sight.** delve, delves, delving; tapestry; landscape (metaphorical); navigate (metaphorical); realm; underscore; pivotal; crucial; essential; robust; comprehensive; leverage; harness; foster; cultivate (metaphorical); embrace (metaphorical); myriad; plethora; intricate; nuanced; multifaceted; holistic; transformative; seamless; ever-evolving; meticulous; commendable; boast; primarily as filler; ultimately as connector.

**Generic colour-temperature adjectives standing in for a particular.** "Vibrant warmth", "bright register", "saturated mood": the failure mode is the adjective doing the work a concrete picture should do. The fix is the picture: what makes the warmth that warmth, what the bright thing actually looks like, where the saturation lives. The adjective alone is fine when it sits against a specific noun that earns it ("the vibrant world the protagonist built", "Construction's vibrant register"); it fails when stacked with another abstract ("vibrant warmth", "bright energy", "saturated atmosphere") to feign weight.

**Constructions to rewrite or cut.** "It is important to note that"; "It is worth noting that"; "Not just X, but Y"; "More than just X"; "X is a testament to Y"; "X speaks to Y"; "X lies at the intersection of Y"; "stands as", "serves as", "plays a role in"; "paints a picture"; "sets the stage for"; "the cornerstone of", "a hallmark of", "a beacon of"; "ushering in"; "stands the test of time"; "in essence"; the false-balance "Some would argue X. Others would argue Y."; the closing moral; the paragraph that restates its own thesis to finish.

**Structural and punctuation tells.** Tricolons stacked across consecutive paragraphs; adjective stacks where one modifier would carry the load; colon-as-header used four or more times in one section; single-word fragment ("Quietly.", "None.") used more than twice; em dashes anywhere; Oxford comma drifting mid-document; "however" used as a soft pivot more than once per section.

Sources: Berens lab, *Delving into LLM-assisted writing*, *Science Advances*, 2025; *Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing*; Reuters Institute, 2024.

## Structure of a section

- Open with a concrete particular: a person, a date, a number, a moment. Not a thesis statement.
- Build through cases. Two or three is usually enough.
- Close on the structural point the cases support. Do not summarise; the section is the summary.

## Citations and quotes

- Every empirical claim needs a citation. Cite primary sources where they exist, strongest secondary where they don't.
- Do not paraphrase a quote into a "fact". If it's a quote, quote it.
- Numbers: units, date, source. "US$57M paid to creators by January 2015" beats "a lot".

## Naming

- Real names, full first time, surname only after. Pronouns when known.
- Titles of works: italics for games, books, films, albums.
- Currency with symbol and unit. Dates spelled long-form on first use ("27 September 2013").
- Credit communities by name where the contribution came from a community, not a company.

## Craft techniques

### Hook

The first sentence makes a promise the reader wants kept. It is not the thesis. It is a particular: a number, a person, a moment, a contradiction.

Bad: "Open development improves discoverability."
Good: "In 2024 Steam shipped roughly fifty-one new games a day, and four-fifths of them went unplayed."

### Show, don't tell

The fact lands harder than the claim about the fact. Ground the reader in the concrete first; reach for higher meaning only after the case is set.

### Rhythm and end-stress

- Long sets up; short lands. A long-long-short pattern reads twice.
- The last word of a sentence is the most prominent. Land on the noun or verb you want the reader to keep.
- Anglo-Saxon words for force; Latinate for nuance. Plosive endings (/t/, /d/, /k/) stop the ear cleanly.
- Avoid prepositional closes (*for, with, of*), weak pronouns at the close (*it, this, that*), adverbial drift ("in the end"), and hedge stacks.
- Read every paragraph aloud.

### Paragraphs end on the loaded sentence

The last sentence is what the reader will carry. Engineer it. Transitions go in the first sentence of the next paragraph, never the last of this one.

### Trust the reader

If a metaphor lands, do not explain it. If a citation makes the point, do not paraphrase it. Every paragraph that explains the previous paragraph is one paragraph too many.

### No signposting

Avoid "first", "second", "in conclusion", "as we discussed earlier". The structure should be felt, not announced.

### Strong nouns and verbs

Adjectives are accomplices, not principals. "The studio shipped" beats "the small independent studio successfully released". Cut adverbs aggressively.

### Active voice

Passive is allowed when the actor genuinely does not matter, or when the receiver of the action is the point ("the file was leaked").

### Cut filler ruthlessly

Find and justify: *something, actually, really, very, quite, just, basically, essentially, somewhat, rather*. Most second drafts are 30% shorter than first drafts and lose nothing.

### One idea per paragraph

Two ideas in a paragraph split attention and blunt both. Break it.

### Throughline and circle-back

A long piece has a central image and a central claim. Both land early and return, in altered form, at the close. Do not announce the return; let the rhyme do the work.

## Narrative momentum

### The but/therefore rule

Between any two beats, the connector must be "but" (reversal) or "therefore" (consequence), never "and then" (summary). Three "and thens" in a row mean the structure needs rework.

### Curiosity gaps

Each paragraph poses a small question the next answers. The reader is pulled forward by the gap, not by the argument. A paragraph that resolves itself is a paragraph the reader can stop on.

### Scene, then reflect

Toggle anecdote and reflection. All anecdote is gossip; all reflection is a lecture.

### Name the dog

Specificity that no general writer would notice is what breaks through. The colour of the password, the brand of the coffee, the phrase from the primary source. The detail nobody else included is the detail the reader remembers.

### The reversal

At least once per part, set up the obvious answer and overturn it. Stage the reversal; do not bury it mid-paragraph.

## Comedy

- A joke must come from a real observation, not wordplay. Never more than one per section.
- Where humour fits, prefer British understatement: litotes, bathos, mock-formal politeness around damning observations, the held pause, the aside that turns out to be the point, self-deprecation as a stalking horse.
- Default is no joke.

## Editing checklist

Run before declaring a section done:

1. Opening sentence is a particular, not a thesis.
2. Closing sentence is the one the reader will remember.
3. Every paragraph passes the read-aloud test.
4. Every claim has a citation, a quote, or is plainly an opinion clearly marked.
5. Every adverb has been examined; most are gone.
6. Every "really", "actually", "very", "just" cut or justified.
7. No paragraph summarises the previous paragraph.
8. No transition word does work the structure should do silently.
9. The throughline is visible.
10. The section earns its place. If it could be cut without weakening the whole, cut it.

## Length

No minimum or maximum. Long enough to be airtight, short enough to be read. If a paragraph isn't pulling weight, it isn't there.

## What writing here must not become

- A manifesto.
- A sales pitch.
- A complaint.
- A celebration of the author.

Hold the line.
