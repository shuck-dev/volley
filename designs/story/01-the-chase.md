# The chase

The protagonist is up at five-forty. They make coffee, eat toast with marmite over the sink, and leave the house. The lane to the garden smells of rosemary and rain.

They go through the side-arch into the garden. The walls are the colour of seashells. The court at the far end has white lines drawn fresh that morning, though the protagonist did not draw them. Behind the counter, the friend looks up and says, "Nine hundred and twelve," and sets down the rag they were folding. The protagonist picks up the racquet they left leaning against the post the night before.

"Try for a thousand today," the friend says, to their back.

The protagonist walks to the court. A coach is across it, warming up against the wall; when the coach sees them, the coach steps to the line. The first ball is gentle. The third is harder. Each time the rally crosses the net, a chime sounds, and the count climbs by one. Somewhere in the second hundred, the friend at the counter says, "Nine fifty," and the protagonist nods once in the rally without missing a stroke. The count climbs past a thousand.

There is a gate at the back of the garden, behind the stall and behind the rally. It is locked. The protagonist's feet have a route through the garden that does not pass within twenty feet of it.

The friend at the counter keeps the count. They wipe the counter when there is nothing to wipe, look up when the rally lands, hand the protagonist water without being asked and a towel that has been in the sun. They have a name the protagonist remembers from somewhere.

---

The protagonist is between rallies, walking back across the court for water, when something at the corner of the court catches their eye. They turn their head. By the time their eyes find it, there is nothing there to find. They stand a second with the racquet against their leg. They drink the water. They go back to the rally.

Three sessions later, a coach holds the line on a return one beat too long. Their head is tilted toward the protagonist. Their mouth is open. Then the coach blinks and serves and the rally goes on. The protagonist returns the serve. After, walking home, the protagonist almost rings a number they still have. They stop with their thumb above the green button.

A week after that the friend at the counter says the protagonist's name out loud. They say it like they meant to say something else. The protagonist looks at the friend. The friend looks at the protagonist. Neither of them speaks. Then the friend says, "Eleven hundred today, do you think," and starts folding the rag again. The protagonist goes to the court. They miss the next return.

---

The bracket clears. The protagonist beats the qualifiers, the regional final, the national. The world final is the champ. The champ has never lost. The champ has never come to a rally the protagonist could see. The protagonist has wanted to become them for years.

The match is in a battle court with the lights up and the noise of a crowd that does not exist. Across the court, the champ moves cleanly. When the rally goes long, the champ holds the line. The protagonist is pulled along. They volley back. They do not miss. The point comes down to a clean cross-court they did not know they had been working toward. They make the shot. The score lands at four-eighty-seven to four-eighty-six.

The protagonist stands at the line.

They wait for it to land. The chime is on. The crowd that does not exist is roaring. The friend at the counter has stopped saying numbers. The protagonist can see them through the gap between the battle court and the garden, holding the rag in two hands. Nothing comes.

The champ is across the court, not moving, looking at the protagonist with a look the protagonist remembers from somewhere.

The protagonist puts down the racquet. They take a step. Their first step is on the battle court. Their second step is on the gravel of the garden side-arch.

Then they are walking out of the garden, and the streets they grew up on are around them.
