# The story

The story Volley tells. Setting, cast, and structural canon are in [`outline.md`](outline.md). World rendering and tone are in [`../art/bible.md`](../art/bible.md). The protagonist's interior is in [`../characters/protagonist.md`](../characters/protagonist.md).

---

The protagonist is up before the sun is good. They eat what they eat in the mornings, drink what they drink, and walk the path to the garden with their feet doing it without consulting the rest of them. The path smells of rain and citrus the way it has always smelled. The garden is at the end of it. They go through the wooden side-arch and stand for a second on the gravel inside, and the friend behind the counter at the stall against the wall looks up and says, "Nine hundred and twelve," like a small celebration, and sets down the rag they were folding. The protagonist lifts the racquet they left leaning against the post the night before. They take it by the grip that has worn to fit their hand and the grip is warm from the morning even though the morning is cool.

"Try for a thousand today," the friend says, to their back.

The protagonist does not turn around. They walk to the court at the far end of the garden, where the morning light hits first. A coach is across the court, warming up against the wall, and when the coach sees them, the coach steps to the line. The first ball is gentle. The protagonist returns it cleanly. The second is harder. The third is harder than that. The rally finds itself; the racquet finds the ball, sends it back, finds it again. The count climbs, one each time it crosses the net. Somewhere in the second hundred, the friend at the counter says, "Nine fifty," in the same small-celebration voice, and the protagonist nods once in the rally without missing a stroke. The count climbs through to a thousand and one and stops being a number that wants saying out loud.

There is a gate set into the wall at the back of the garden, behind the stall and behind the rally. It is a wooden gate, painted once a long time ago, with a heavy iron lock that has been there longer than the gate. The protagonist's eyes do not go to it. They have a route through the garden, in and out, that does not pass within twenty feet of it; they have had this route for years, and they have never noticed they have it. If you asked them about the gate they would say there is a gate. They would not say they look at it; they would not say they avoid it; they would not have the language for either. The gate is a fact in the periphery of their day, the way certain shapes in a room you have lived in for a decade are facts. The gate is locked. They have not had a reason to check.

After the rally, in the shade behind the court, the protagonist takes out their phone. The battery is at fifty-two per cent. They scroll past the lock screen and into the call log. Fifteen rows down, after every other call they have made and received in the last few months, there is a row of digits with no contact name. They stop the scroll there. Their thumb rests above the green button. The screen waits for them. They look at the digits the way you look at a thing that has been sitting on your desk for a year that you keep meaning to deal with. The phone screen times out and goes black. The protagonist puts the phone away. They have been doing this for years. They believe it is a habit. They do not look at it long enough to know it is a habit.

The friend at the counter is the one who keeps the count. The friend wipes the counter when there is nothing to wipe, looks up when the rally lands, says the round numbers, hands the protagonist water without being asked and a towel that has been in the sun. The friend is funny in a way that does not perform; the protagonist has heard them be funny for years. The protagonist has never asked the friend their name. The friend has the same name as someone the protagonist used to know, and the friend wears their hair the way that person wore their hair, and the friend laughs the way that person laughed, but the protagonist has not put it together. The friend is the friend at the counter. Construction does not tell them to put it together; they have not put it together.

---

Cracks come into Construction quietly enough that the first one is almost not a crack at all.

The protagonist is between rallies, walking back across the court for water, when they catch the light at the corner of the court doing something. They look. By the time their eyes are on it, the light is just light again, slant morning light on the line at the edge. The protagonist stands a second with the racquet against their leg. The light does not move; the light has been doing this all day. The protagonist drinks the water. They go back to the rally.

A few sessions later, a coach holds the line on a return one beat too long. Their head is tilted toward the protagonist and their face is the face of someone who has just heard something. Then the coach blinks and serves and the rally goes on. The protagonist returns the serve and does not say anything, because what would you say. After, walking home through the path that smells of rain and citrus, the protagonist almost rings the friend's sister's number, which they still have memorised for some reason, and stops with their thumb above the green button. The sister was the dead friend's sister. The protagonist has not spoken to her in four years.

Then the friend at the counter, on a Wednesday, says the protagonist's name out loud. They say it like they meant to say something else. They say it like a kettle clicking off into a kitchen that has been holding its breath. The protagonist looks at the friend. The friend looks at the protagonist. Neither of them speaks for a long count. Then the friend says, "Eleven hundred today, do you think," in their normal voice, and starts folding the rag again. The protagonist goes to the court. The rally is the rally. The light at the edge of the court is the colour of light it has always been, except that it is not.

The protagonist tells themselves none of these things are happening. They tell themselves several times.

---

The bracket clears. The protagonist beats the qualifiers, the regional final, the national, and the world final is the champ. The champ has never lost. The champ has never come to a rally the protagonist could see. The protagonist has been thinking about the champ for years now in the way a person thinks about somebody they want to become.

The match is in a battle court with the lights up and the noise of a crowd that does not exist. The protagonist plays well. Across the court, the champ moves the way the protagonist has wanted to move for a long time, the way the protagonist's body has been trying to move all those mornings against the post. When the rally goes long, the champ holds the line. The protagonist is pulled along by it. They volley back. They do not miss. The point comes down to a clean cross-court the protagonist has been working toward without knowing they have been working toward it; they make the shot. The score lands at four-eighty-seven to four-eighty-six.

The protagonist stands at the line and waits.

They wait for the inside of them to lift. They wait the way you wait for the body to catch up to something the body has just done. The chime is on. The crowd that does not exist is roaring. The friend at the counter has stopped saying numbers. The protagonist can see them through the gap between the battle court and the garden, holding the rag in two hands, looking at the protagonist as if they are about to ask whether the protagonist is alright. The lift does not come.

The light in the corner of the battle court goes blue. Nobody else sees it. The colour at the edge of the score is a colour the score does not have. The crowd thins without leaving. A coach across the court is looking at the protagonist the way a stage actor looks at someone who has wandered onto the stage by mistake.

The protagonist puts down the racquet. They take a step. They are still in the battle court when they take the first step, and they are not in the battle court when they take the second. The light around them changes its mind about being warm.

They are walking, and the walking is the walking they have been not doing for years.

---

The protagonist is in their actual hometown.

The streets are the streets they grew up on. The rain is colder here than it was anywhere they have been recently. There are gulls. There is the smell of the harbour and the smell of someone making coffee somewhere up the hill and the smell of the rain that has stopped half an hour ago drying off the painted terraces. The protagonist walks them the way you walk into a place you have been late to for a long time. They pass the newsagent where the cashier they used to be afraid of has been replaced by someone they vaguely remember from school. They pass the pub. They pass the corner where their mother used to wait for them after primary school in the green car the protagonist's mother no longer has. They are walking toward the shop, even though they have not decided to walk toward the shop. The body is doing it.

The shop is closed.

The protagonist stops on the step. The window is dark. The door has three slips of paper wedged into the frame, deliveries from a wine merchant the shop has used for twenty years; the slip on top is dated a week ago Tuesday. The protagonist stands and looks at the slips and does not move. After a long minute the door behind the shop opens, the side door into the family kitchen, and the sister is there.

The sister is the dead friend's sister. The protagonist has not seen her in four years.

She looks at them the way you look at a person you have been waiting for and have stopped expecting. "Come in," she says. The protagonist comes in. The kitchen smells of toast and something else and the kettle has just clicked off. There is an album on the table, half open, half full. The sister puts a cup in front of the protagonist and does not pour anything into it. She sits across the table.

"He's not here," she says. "He hasn't been here for a week."

The protagonist looks at her. The sister has been crying recently and is not now. She has the look of a person who has been waiting at a kitchen table for someone else to come back, and is not about to ask for anything from the person who has just come in instead.

"I don't know where he is," she says. "He's not picking up. I think you're the only person he might pick up for. If he picks up. I don't know."

The protagonist sits down.

---

The protagonist starts going to the garden again. They do not know why. The friend at the counter is at the counter, and the count is no longer on the wall. The board has gone dim. The friend says nothing about it. The friend says, "Hello, you," like it is the first sentence either of them has ever said.

The rally is different. The racquet finds the ball and sends it back and finds it again, but somewhere in the middle of a swing, the protagonist sees an elbow on a counter. They miss the next return. They serve again. The next time, they see a sleeve folded twice at the cuff. They miss again. The next time, they see a racquet handed back to them grip first, by the friend who died, who never let the protagonist carry the bag home.

The friend who died was called something the protagonist has not said out loud since the funeral. The friend was a year older. The friend had small wrists and a laugh that was too loud for the room and a habit of folding their sleeves twice instead of once. The friend was the kind of person who rang you when they said they were going to ring you, which is a thing about them the protagonist had not understood was rare until later. The friend held the racquet by the throat when they walked, never the grip, because they had read somewhere that holding it by the grip wore the leather, and they cared about things like that. The friend was the protagonist's best friend.

The friend went off the cliff the summer the protagonist had stopped going. The friend group jumped from the cliff, the way a friend group will when something has been dangerous for long enough that it has become routine. The protagonist had gone the first few summers and stopped going the year before the friend died. They had stopped because the protagonist had been afraid, and because the protagonist did not know how to be afraid of something a group of people they loved did every weekend, and because being afraid had made the protagonist mean for the first time in their life. The protagonist had said no to coming for some months. The last thing the protagonist said to the friend was something on the phone the morning of the day. The protagonist has tried to remember what it was. They cannot.

The shopkeeper was on the cliff that day. The shopkeeper had been jumping with the friend. The shopkeeper came down. The protagonist did not come for weeks. When the protagonist came, it was to the funeral, and the protagonist stood at the back of the church, and they left without finding the shopkeeper, who was at the front of the church, and was looking for them.

The protagonist had loved the shopkeeper for as long as the protagonist had been able to love anybody. Saying so to themselves on the day of the funeral would have asked them to do something they could not do.

The protagonist built a place to be in that did not run into any of it. They built the garden. They built the friend at the stall. They built the count and the coaches and the racquet that found their grip at the post each morning. They built a number to chase that did not have a name attached. They had been chasing the number for years.

---

The album fills, slow. Each photograph the protagonist puts in is a photograph of a thing the protagonist did not know they had been keeping. The shopkeeper has been keeping them. The sister, sitting at the kitchen table with the protagonist on the afternoons the protagonist comes round, slides photographs across the wood: him at fifteen with the racquet, him at twenty on the cliff in summer, him last year at a friend's wedding wearing the shirt the protagonist does not recognise. The album fills. After a long time, after the album is mostly full, the compartment at the back of the album opens of its own accord, the way a thing opens when the right amount of weight has been put into the part you can see. The sister does not look up when it opens. She slides the key across the table without looking up. The key is a small brass key that has been somewhere dark for a long time.

The protagonist takes the key.

"You know where the gate is," the sister says.

The protagonist says, "Yes."

---

The protagonist walks back to the garden, the long way, through the wooden side-arch they have been going through every morning for years. The friend at the counter is at the counter still. They look up as the protagonist comes through. The look has shifted, and the air the protagonist has been moving through is thinner, and on the friend's face is a thing that is almost grief except the friend is not a person who can hold grief because the friend was made for this place. The friend says, "Hello, you," again. The friend wipes the counter that does not need wiping. The friend says nothing else.

The protagonist does not pick up the racquet. They walk past the court, where a rally is happening, and they walk past the stall, and for the first time in years their feet take them along a path that does not go around. The grass is shorter at the back of the garden. The wall is closer than they remembered.

They put the key in the lock. The key fits the way a key fits when it was made for the lock and somebody has kept it oiled. They turn it. The bolt is heavy, and it does not catch, and it slides back. The gate opens.

---

On the other side, the path is short. It is grass and rosemary, and a low wall, and a bench against the wall. There are no other people. The path is twenty feet long. The wall is at the cliff. The bench has a dedication plaque on the back of it; the name on the plaque is the friend's name. The shopkeeper is on the bench, alive, smaller than the protagonist remembers.

The shopkeeper is wearing a shirt the protagonist does not recognise. They are holding a phone. They are not looking at the path. The wind is coming from the sea.

The protagonist stops a few feet from the bench. The protagonist takes out their own phone. The screen lights. The number is fifteen rows down in the call log, with no contact name. The protagonist's thumb has hovered above it for years. The protagonist presses it.

The phone in the shopkeeper's hand rings.

The shopkeeper does not jump. The shopkeeper looks at the screen. The screen says the unknown number it has been saying every time it rang for the past four years. The shopkeeper has been letting the unknown number ring. The shopkeeper could let this one ring. The shopkeeper could put the phone face down on the bench and let it be one more thing to be done with the air. The shopkeeper looks up.

The protagonist is standing on the path. Their phone is at their ear. The protagonist's hand is shaking but their eyes are not.

The shopkeeper looks at the protagonist for a long time.

The shopkeeper picks up.

---

The line opens. Neither of them speaks. The wind is the wind. The shopkeeper's hand is on the bench beside them, holding the phone. The protagonist's hand is at their ear. They are looking at each other across twelve feet of grass. The line stays open.

After what is not a measurable amount of time, the protagonist crosses the grass. They do not lower the phone. They sit down on the bench. The bench is the right size for two people who are friends. The shopkeeper does not move. The phone is between them, in two hands, the line open.

The protagonist hears their own breath through it. The shopkeeper hears their own breath through it. They sit like that.

After a while, the shopkeeper puts the phone down on the bench between them, screen up, the call still running. They look at the sea. The protagonist puts their phone down beside the shopkeeper's, also still running. They look at the sea too. The wind is the wind. The line is open.

---

The credits play over the two of them on the same court. The rally between them is uneven; neither of them has rallied like this in a long time. The shopkeeper is not as fast as they used to be, and the protagonist is rusty in a way the protagonist would not have admitted, and the rally goes long anyway because both of them are trying. The light on the court is the light the garden has had all this time, with something else in it now: the texture the actual hometown has worn into it over the months of going back and forth. The friend at the counter is at the counter, and the friend is glad to be there, and the friend does not need to call any names today.

The cliff is behind them, returnable. The bench is there. The number in the protagonist's phone has a contact name now.

The gate stays open.
