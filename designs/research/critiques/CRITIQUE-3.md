# Round-three hostile review: every field the essay touches

This review treats the essay as it would be received by named experts in each domain it claims to speak about. Each section opens with the critic and their work, then channels their objection in their own conceptual vocabulary. Rounds one and two already converted the obvious sceptics (pragmatic indie, AI executive, journalist; e/acc, Yu/Blow, DeVault). Round three is wider: scholars and theorists from outside the essay's comfort zone whose disciplinary frames cut against its assumptions.

## The fields the essay touches

1. **Free software politics (FSF / GPL tradition).** The essay puts Volley! under MIT and treats MIT as a member of the open family.
2. **Open-source sociology / hacker ethics.** Section on Linux/Raymond and the "lineage older than games".
3. **Open-source economics / sustainability.** Section 13: "It pays for Anuke. It pays for tobspr."
4. **Philosophy of craft.** Section 12 ("What only people can bring") and the closing section.
5. **Critical theory of late capitalism.** The essay's whole frame: individual maker can act under platform consolidation if they choose openness.
6. **Game studies / aesthetics.** Games are treated as products with discoverability problems; aesthetic and cultural questions are absent.
7. **Indie zinester / queer-games tradition.** Anthropy's lineage of games-as-personal-expression goes unmentioned in the cases the essay surfaces.
8. **AI safety research.** Section 11 names the Anthropic RSP, METR, AISI.
9. **AI alignment / philosophy of automation.** Section 11 and 12 frame AI as a tool that can stay in service.
10. **Apprenticeship / educational theory.** Section on the disappearing rung borrows the apprenticeship frame without naming Lave & Wenger.
11. **Disability studies.** Section 09 (Celeste Assist Mode, Xbox Adaptive Controller, Forza signers).
12. **Computing history / labour.** Section on the disappearing junior rung and the open-source on-ramp.
13. **Documentary filmmaking.** PsychOdyssey and *Free to Play* as build-in-public artefacts.
14. **Marketing data / discoverability research.** Zukowski, GameDiscoverCo numbers.
15. **Platform / industrial organisation.** Valve as "open at the edges" exemplar.
16. **Behavioural economics under precarity.** The "open looks risky; silence is riskier" framing.
17. **Political philosophy of the commons.** Benkler is cited; the deeper commons literature is not.
18. **Literary / non-fiction craft.** The essay's own form. Less an "attack vector" than a self-test.

## Per-field critique

### 1. Free software politics: Stallman / Bradley Kuhn

**The critic:** Richard Stallman, *Free Software, Free Society* (FSF, 2002, 2010); the GNU "Why Open Source Misses the Point" essay; Bradley Kuhn's writing on copyleft as defensive infrastructure.
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** the four freedoms; copyleft vs. permissive; "free as in freedom"; X Window System as cautionary tale; enclosure of the commons; the conflation of "open source" with "free software" as the founding mistake.
**The strongest objection:** The essay's title is "The Case for Open Development" (renamed from "The Case for Open-Source Game Development" in response to this critique) and its central commitment is an MIT licence. To Stallman that combination is incoherent on its own terms. MIT is the licence under which Microsoft, Apple, and every closed publisher in the industry can take Volley!'s code, fold it into a proprietary product, and ship it without crediting Josh, paying Josh, or returning a line of code to the commons. The X Window System is the canonical case Stallman has made for thirty years: permissive licensing is how the very enclosure the essay decries gets enabled. The essay invokes Spolsky's "commoditise your complement" approvingly in section 10 and never notices that under MIT, Josh is the complement being commoditised, not the one doing the commoditising. The closing claim, "the commons grows", is what copyleft was invented to make true; under MIT the commons can be drained at any time.
**Recommended response:** *Engage* with one sentence in section 10. Acknowledge the choice of MIT over GPL is a deliberate trade (lower barrier to contribution, including from contributors employed by closed studios) and that the GPL tradition's objection to that trade is real. Otherwise the essay claims the lineage without paying its dues.

### 2. Open-source sociology: Coleman

**The critic:** Gabriella Coleman, *Coding Freedom: The Ethics and Aesthetics of Hacking* (Princeton, 2012).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** liberal political tradition; productive freedom; the aesthetic of the well-made patch; meritocracy as ideology; F/OSS as "a liberal critique from within liberalism".
**The strongest objection:** Coleman's argument is that hacker culture's politics are recognisably liberal, with all the strengths and blind spots that tradition carries. The essay borrows the aesthetic (visible work, credited contribution, the well-made commit) without naming the politics underneath. The result reads as universal common sense when it is in fact a position inside a tradition. A reader from outside that tradition (a Marxist game studies scholar, a writer trained in the queer-games scene) will hear the essay's "everyone is welcome under their own name" as the same liberal-meritocratic claim Coleman shows hackers actually make and sometimes fail to honour.
**Recommended response:** *Leave alone.* This is a deepening, not a refutation. The essay is honestly inside the liberal tradition; naming the tradition would expand the surface for less gain.

### 3. Open-source economics: covered in round two (DeVault / DHH / Spolsky)

Round two channelled this. No new round-three writer would land harder than DeVault's "name the median" already does. The Stallman attack above (field 1) sharpens the same point on the licensing axis.

### 4. Philosophy of craft: Sennett

**The critic:** Richard Sennett, *The Craftsman* (Yale, 2008).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** the desire to do a job well for its own sake; skill as bodily practice; "joined skill in community"; the obsession with perfect rightness as a deformation; the social conditions craft requires.
**The strongest objection:** Sennett would actually agree with much of the essay; he praises Linux maintainers as public craftsmen in the book itself. His sharper objection is the one the essay does not earn: craft requires *time*, *protection from market urgency*, and a *community of judgement*, and the essay's prescription (build in public, accept the maintainer-tax, learn the craft on the public record) places the lone developer under exactly the market urgency Sennett names as the deformer. The Bay 12 case the essay celebrates is twenty years of near-poverty; Sennett would read that as the price the craft extracted from the brothers, not as the model. The essay would be stronger if it conceded that openness is a craft *practice*, but craft *survival* requires the social and material conditions (patronage, public funding, low cost of living, partner income) the essay never names.
**Recommended response:** *Concede honestly.* One line in section 12 acknowledging that the practice asks conditions of the practitioner that not every developer has access to. This also strengthens the essay's response to the precarity attack (field 16).

### 5. Critical theory: Adorno

**The critic:** Adorno and Horkheimer, *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, "The Culture Industry" (1944); Adorno, *Minima Moralia* (1951).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** the culture industry; pseudo-individuation; the standardisation of taste; amusement as the prolongation of work; the integration of the consumer from above; "the whole is the false".
**The strongest objection:** The essay's central agency claim ("the people who want to make things get to decide what the tools are for") is, in Adorno's idiom, a textbook instance of pseudo-individuation: the system grants the appearance of meaningful choice (open vs. closed, MIT vs. GPL, devlog vs. silence) while the structural conditions (Steam's 30%, the AI labs' training corpus, the 51-games-a-day attention economy, the platform's algorithmic gatekeeping) determine the outcome regardless of which choice the individual maker makes. Section 14's "kick against a wall" framing is exactly the kind of redemption-through-individual-craft move Adorno would call ideological. The essay names the structural conditions in section 01 (51 games a day, layoffs, self-funding) and then proceeds as if individual posture could escape them. Adorno would say the essay is the culture industry's ideal artefact: it takes the very precarity the industry produces and reframes it as an opportunity for authentic self-expression.
**Recommended response:** *Engage* lightly in section 12 or section 14. One sentence acknowledging that the practice does not undo the structural conditions; it offers one route through them, for the makers who can sustain the route. The essay's "structural, not guaranteed" qualifier already does most of this work; sharpening it against the Adorno reading would close the gap.

### 6. Game studies: Bogost / Aarseth

**The critic:** Ian Bogost, *How to Talk About Videogames* (Minnesota, 2015) and *Persuasive Games* (MIT, 2007); Espen Aarseth, *Cybertext* (1997).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** procedural rhetoric; games as cultural objects "part art and part appliance"; the "what is it for" question Silicon Valley refuses to ask; ergodic literature.
**The strongest objection:** The essay is entirely about how games get *found* and *funded*. It says nothing about what games *mean*, what they *argue*, or what makes one worth finding in the first place. Every case is selected on commercial-survival criteria (sales numbers, wishlist counts, units shipped) and the only aesthetic discussion is the *Celeste* Assist Mode wording, which is treated as an inclusion question rather than an aesthetic one. Bogost would point out that the essay's "open development" frame describes a *production discipline*, not an *artistic stance*, and conflates the two by assuming that visible craft produces meaningful work. *Hollow Knight* is great because of what it is, not because Team Cherry posted on Newgrounds. The essay's thesis would survive this attack but would be sharpened by admitting it is a thesis about *practice*, not *art*.
**Recommended response:** *Concede honestly.* One half-sentence in the nut graf or section 14 saying the essay is about how work gets seen, not about what makes work worth seeing; the latter is the harder question and outside this essay's scope.

### 7. Queer / zinester games: Anthropy

**The critic:** Anna Anthropy, *Rise of the Videogame Zinesters* (Seven Stories, 2012); merritt k; Porpentine.
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** the zine; games by "freaks, normals, amateurs, queers, housewives"; the corporate "elite"; tools that let non-programmers make games; games as personal expression rather than commercial product.
**The strongest objection:** The essay does not name Anthropy, merritt k, Porpentine, or the Twine scene whose work has made queer and personal authorship visible in games for over a decade. The cases the essay celebrates are all commercial successes that pass through Steam, Patreon, or Kickstarter; the zinester tradition Anthropy named is precisely the work that *refuses* those funnels and the discoverability problem the essay opens with. Anthropy would read the essay as a more polite version of the indie-orthodoxy she wrote against: still measuring success in units shipped, still treating Steam as the relevant venue, still privileging the games that scaled. The "open development" the essay describes is the open development of small studios who *want to be Wube*. The open development of someone making a Twine game about their own life on neocities is a different practice with different stakes.
**Recommended response:** *Engage* in section 14 or section 06. Name Anthropy and the zinester lineage as a parallel tradition the essay's frame does not capture; cede the ground rather than annex it.

### 8. AI safety research

Round two's Camp A (LeCun branch) and round one's AI executive critique already covered this. Round three's only addition: Russell himself, in *Human Compatible*, has explicitly advocated the enforcement architecture the essay's "only pressure from outside" line gestures at without naming. The essay should cite Russell's own proposal (manufacturers must prove safety; government defines the threshold; deployment blocked otherwise) rather than leaving the mechanism abstract. *Engage* with one clause in section 11.

### 9. Philosophy of automation: Dreyfus / Brian Cantwell Smith

**The critic:** Hubert Dreyfus, *What Computers Still Can't Do* (MIT, 1992); Brian Cantwell Smith, *The Promise of Artificial Intelligence: Reckoning and Judgment* (MIT, 2019).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** embodied skill; tacit knowledge; reckoning vs. judgement; the gap between competent calculation and accountable understanding.
**The strongest objection:** Smith's distinction between *reckoning* (what current systems do well) and *judgement* (what they cannot do, because judgement requires being accountable to the world the judgement is about) maps almost perfectly onto the essay's own line about audience-as-audit in section 11. The essay has the right intuition and reaches for the wrong language. Naming Smith would let the essay say in two sentences what it currently says in three paragraphs, and would protect the argument from the LeCun-style "you don't understand what these systems do" rejoinder. Dreyfus would push harder on the asset-creation paragraph: it is not that art is "the most identifiably human hand"; it is that *all* skilled practice is embodied, and the spell-checker / power saw / drum machine analogies undersell how different a generative model is from those tools. A power saw cuts where the carpenter places it; a generative model proposes the work itself.
**Recommended response:** *Leave alone* on Dreyfus (the analogies are doing rhetorical work the essay needs); *engage* with one citation to Smith's reckoning/judgement frame in section 11 if the essay wants stronger philosophical backing for the audience-as-audit move.

### 10. Apprenticeship / educational theory: Lave & Wenger

**The critic:** Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger, *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation* (Cambridge, 1991).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** legitimate peripheral participation; community of practice; newcomers and oldtimers; mastery as movement from periphery to centre; the social production of identity through practice.
**The strongest objection:** The "disappearing rung" section makes the apprenticeship case without the apprenticeship literature. Lave and Wenger's framework would *strengthen* the essay's claim: open source is one of the few remaining settings where legitimate peripheral participation actually works (a newcomer can land a small, low-risk, productive contribution and accumulate identity within the community). But the framework also exposes a gap. Asparouhova's caveat that the typical project is one or two maintainers means most open-source projects do not have the *community of practice* Lave and Wenger require for legitimate peripheral participation to function. The essay treats "open source" as a single mechanism; LPP would force the distinction between projects that are communities (where apprenticeship works) and projects that are solo public broadcasts (where it does not). Volley! today is the second. The essay's apprenticeship claim depends on Volley! becoming the first, and does not say so.
**Recommended response:** *Engage* with one sentence in the disappearing-rung section. Name Lave & Wenger. Acknowledge that the apprenticeship effect requires community, not just visibility.

### 11. Disability studies: McRuer

**The critic:** Robert McRuer, *Crip Theory: Cultural Signs of Queerness and Disability* (NYU, 2006); the essay "Compulsory Able-Bodiedness and Queer/Disabled Existence" (2002); Alison Kafer, *Feminist, Queer, Crip* (Indiana, 2013).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** compulsory able-bodiedness; crip; the neoliberal celebration of difference as appropriation and containment; access as a question for the majority, not a minority concession.
**The strongest objection:** The inclusivity section closes with Hamilton's "accessibility business case" numbers: 14% of *The Last Door*'s players used the dyslexia font; *Solara*'s highest spenders were blind. McRuer would call this exactly the neoliberal move he names: disabled experience appears in the argument as a *productivity input* (it improved the design) and as a *revenue input* (it expanded the market). The disabled speedrunner who corrected the *Celeste* line is credited as an asset to the studio, not as a person whose objection mattered on its own terms. The essay's frame instrumentalises disability for the case for openness. A crip-theory reading would insist that access is the end, not the means; framing it as the means is precisely how compulsory able-bodiedness reproduces itself under a friendlier face. The essay's cases are real and the credits are real; the rhetorical structure that uses them to argue for a *production discipline* is the structure McRuer would name.
**Recommended response:** *Engage* with one sentence in the inclusivity section. Acknowledge that the cases stand on their own terms first, and that the openness-as-multiplier framing is a secondary reading that does not exhaust them. The fix is small and the gain is real, because this is the single attack a hostile reader could most damage the essay's reception with.

### 12. Computing history / hidden labour: Hicks

**The critic:** Mar Hicks, *Programmed Inequality: How Britain Discarded Women Technologists* (MIT, 2017).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** hidden labour; gendered de-skilling; the structural consequences of who counts as a technologist; the long history of the field forgetting who built it.
**The strongest objection:** The essay's open-source roll call (Torvalds, Raymond, Stallman implicitly, Fogel, Asparouhova once) reproduces the same male-coded, English-fluent canon Hicks's work spent a career complicating. Octoverse contributor numbers from India, Nigeria, Brazil, the Philippines appear once in the inclusivity section as a growth statistic; they are not present in the lineage section, where Linus stands alone as the founding figure. The "lineage older than games" is told as if it began in 1991 in Helsinki rather than in the multiply-attributed labour of decades of women, hobbyists, and overseas contributors who built the infrastructure Torvalds put a kernel on top of. A historian would not call the essay wrong; she would call it canonical in the way the field's canons always have been.
**Recommended response:** *Leave alone* unless the essay is willing to restructure the lineage section. The fix is larger than a sentence and the essay's argument does not depend on it. Note as a known limitation.

### 13. Documentary filmmaking

The essay treats *PsychOdyssey* and *Free to Play* as build-in-public artefacts. A documentary critic (Errol Morris, Patricia Aufderheide's *Documentary Film: A Very Short Introduction*) would point out that *PsychOdyssey* is also a 33-episode work of editorial selection by 2 Player Productions: the documentary discipline is not the same as raw transparency. The essay flattens the two. *Leave alone*; the distinction is real but does not damage the argument.

### 16. Behavioural economics under precarity

**The critic:** Sendhil Mullainathan and Eldar Shafir, *Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much* (Times Books, 2013); the literature on bandwidth tax under poverty.
**The strongest objection:** The essay frames open vs. closed as a *choice* the developer makes. Under the precarity conditions section 01 documents (one in three US developers laid off in two years, 82% self-funding), the choice is not free in the sense the essay implies. A developer with eighteen months of runway and a partner subsidising rent can practise the cadence; a developer with three months and a child cannot. The maintainer-tax round two named falls hardest on exactly the developers the essay says it wants to help. The essay's "openness is the practice with a chance" line is true on average and may be false for the median person it is addressed to.
**Recommended response:** *Engage* in section 13 or section 14. One sentence acknowledging that the practice has a cost the developer must be able to pay, and that the conditions under which payment is possible are themselves the structural problem the essay does not solve.

### 17. Political philosophy of the commons: Ostrom

**The critic:** Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge, 1990).
**Their conceptual vocabulary:** common-pool resources; the eight design principles; monitoring and graduated sanctions; the failure modes of unmanaged commons.
**The strongest objection:** Benkler is cited; Ostrom is not. Ostrom's design principles for sustainable commons (clear boundaries, congruence between rules and conditions, collective-choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms, recognition of self-governance, nested enterprises) are the actual blueprint for what makes a commons survive enclosure. Most of the essay's open-source cases pass some of these principles and fail others. Naming Ostrom would let the essay distinguish *commons that hold* from *commons that get drained*. *Leave alone* unless the essay wants to take on the longer commons-governance argument; this is a deepening, not a refutation.

### 18. Literary / non-fiction craft (self-test)

The STYLE.md is rigorous and the essay largely meets it. One observation a literary critic would make: the essay has many strong individual lines and a slightly weaker overall arc, because Part II runs eight cases and they begin to rhyme. Round one and two already noted the survivorship problem; the literary version is that the *form* of repeated success-story makes the argument feel inductive in a way the prose explicitly does not claim. *Leave alone*; the structural decision is defensible.

## Cross-cutting attacks

Three objections recur across critics from very different traditions:

1. **The structural-conditions gap.** Adorno (field 5), Sennett (field 4), Mullainathan and Shafir (field 16), and McRuer (field 11) all converge on the same point from different angles: the essay names structural conditions in section 01 and proceeds as if individual practice could route around them. The "structural, not guaranteed" qualifier in section 12 is a single line carrying the weight of every one of these objections. It needs to do more work, or the essay needs to admit it cannot.

2. **Instrumentalisation of marginalised experience.** McRuer (field 11), Anthropy (field 7), and Hicks (field 12) all read the essay as taking the experience of disabled, queer, and globally-distributed contributors and converting it into evidence for a production discipline run, in practice, by developers who look like the essay's canonical figures (Torvalds, Schafer, Barone, Tarn, Anuke, tobspr, Antti). The cases are real. The use the essay makes of them is the move these critics would name.

3. **The MIT problem.** Stallman (field 1) names the licensing version. Spolsky (round two, Camp C) names the strategic version: under MIT, Volley!'s code is the complement that gets commoditised, not the one doing the commoditising. The essay's title says "open source" and its commitment is the most permissive possible reading of that phrase. Either it should defend the choice in those terms or change the title.

## What the essay does well across all fields

- The Asparouhova caveat, *Airscape*, and the Valve closing line continue to do disproportionate work; round-three critics from disciplines that would otherwise pile on (disability studies, commons theory, open-source sociology) all register these moves and adjust their attack accordingly.
- The "structural, not guaranteed" qualifier in section 12 is the single most important sentence in the essay against round-three critics; every cross-cutting attack lands less hard because of it.
- Section 11's audience-as-audit move is independently arrived-at versions of Brian Cantwell Smith's reckoning/judgement distinction. The intuition is good even where the citation is missing.
- Naming the *Celeste* exchange in two places (section 06 and section 12) and crediting Halfcoordinated by name does some of the work McRuer would demand, even though the surrounding rhetoric undercuts it.
- The closing image (the developer alone with an empty repository, the audience that arrives invisibly) survives every disciplinary lens, because it is concrete and it does not over-claim.

## The single sharpest line in the essay

After three rounds: **"Open at every layer that touches its players, closed at the layers that touch its money. The strategy scales. The openness has limits, and those limits are what the company keeps."** (Section 10 close.)

This survives every disciplinary lens. Stallman would nod (the limits *are* what gets kept). Spolsky would smile (the strategy is exactly his). Bogost would respect the refusal to over-claim. Adorno would find less to attack here than anywhere else in the essay because the line names structural reality rather than working around it. The line is short, plosive, and end-stressed; it does the structural work the rest of the essay sometimes asks individual rhetoric to carry.

The line that holds up worst: **"This is unacceptable. If it were unacceptable to them, they would have stopped."** Round one flagged it for hand-waving. Round two showed it gets weaker after the named-safety-initiatives paragraph, because the contradiction the line names becomes a richer one the line cannot carry. Round three: a McRuer / Adorno reading hears the same rhetorical move (individual moral declaration substituting for structural account) the essay critiques in others. The line is the most quotable sentence in the essay and the one that least survives the disciplines the essay borrows authority from.
