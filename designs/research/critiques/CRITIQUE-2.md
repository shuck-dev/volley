# Round-two hostile review: in the voice of the opposition

This review is harder than the first. It uses the actual rhetoric of the camps that disagree with the essay's positions. Each camp's section opens by establishing their idiom, then attacks the essay in their voice. Round one's "engage" recommendations have largely been applied: section 02 now contains a definitional anchor, section 11 names Anthropic's RSP, METR, and the UK AISI, section 13 finally names *Airscape* as a failure case, section 10 closes on "open at every layer that touches its players, closed at the layers that touch its money", and section 04 distinguishes presence from open source. Round two assumes those fixes are present and pushes on what they did not solve.

## Camp A: The accelerationist / anti-pause critique

**Idiom and worldview.** Declarative verities, binary framings (lies vs. truth, growth vs. death), repeated anaphora ("We believe", 113 times in Andreessen), a vocabulary of "zombie ideas", "mass demoralisation", "the doomer's delusion". Appeals to caution are framed as status, fear, or regulatory capture. Beff Jezos / Verdon cites thermodynamics ("dissipative adaptation", "the will of the universe"); Andreessen cites biology ("societies, like sharks, grow or die"); both push policy preferences out of debate and into physical law. LeCun is colder and credentialed: existential risk is "complete bullshit", equating intelligence with dominance is "the main fallacy", "AI is not some sort of natural phenomenon that will just emerge".

**Sources studied.** Andreessen, *The Techno-Optimist Manifesto* (a16z, October 2023). Yann LeCun, X/Twitter posts 2023-2025 (Fortune, TechCrunch coverage). Beff Jezos / Guillaume Verdon, "Notes on e/acc principles and tenets" (Substack); Lex Fridman Podcast #407. Sam Altman, "Moore's Law for Everything" (March 2021).

**The essay through their eyes.** Section 11's "I support AI safety research and the slowdown of frontier capability work" reads, in the e/acc idiom, as the "nihilistic wish for fewer people, less energy, and more suffering" Andreessen names. The Future of Life letter the essay defends is, to this camp, the canonical example of pause-advocate signalling that "did not get its pause" because the world correctly ignored it. The new RSP-and-METR-and-AISI paragraph is, for Camp A, an own-goal: it concedes the labs *have* built real safety machinery, then declares them "unacceptable" for not stopping. LeCun would reply, "So the safety regime works, and the demand is still that they shut it down. This is what motivated reasoning looks like." Hinton's resignation, used as moral weight, reads to Camp A as the move of a researcher who lost the technical argument and reached for the credentialed-elder card.

The "smaller failure mode" passage would be turned around: this camp would call it the *only* failure mode, then ask why the essay needed the existential framing at all.

**Specific phrases they would single out.** "This is unacceptable. If it were unacceptable to them, they would have stopped." Andreessen: "We are not victims, we are conquerors." Beff: "decel mind virus". The essay's line is exactly the rhetorical posture e/acc has spent two years training itself to mock: an outside party declaring an industry's choices unacceptable while having no skin in the technical game.

**Their strongest argument the essay does not address.** Selection. If the safety-conscious labs slow and the unsafe ones do not, the world's frontier model is built by the actor least disposed to caution. The essay's response, "only pressure from outside the labs might", is not a response, it is a wish. Camp A would point out that the essay names zero specific external-pressure mechanisms with a credible track record, against a list of internal-safety mechanisms (RSP, METR, AISI, Apollo) that demonstrably exist.

**Where the engage-fixes made things worse from this angle.** The named-safety-initiatives paragraph improved the essay's defence against the AI-researcher critique (Camp A.1, the LeCun branch) and worsened its defence against the accelerationist critique (Camp A.2, the e/acc branch). It now reads as "the safety apparatus is real, large, and still inadequate", which is a much harder position to hold than "the safety apparatus does not exist".

## Camp B: The pragmatist-against-public-development critique

**Idiom and worldview.** Dry, transactional, allergic to romance. Derek Yu: prefers to "put out games when I feel that they're ready". Jonathan Blow: the indie scene has "changed into something in which he does not belong"; "progress has not been as large as people assume". Chris Zukowski, data-driven: "wishlists aren't milk", demos are the post-2020 marketing meta, 10,000 wishlists is the threshold, devlogs are one channel among many. Adam Saltsman: sustainability is taking a smaller cut and giving developer autonomy, not working in public. The tone is "show me the spreadsheet".

**Sources studied.** Derek Yu, *Spelunky* (Boss Fight Books, 2016) and his "Make Games With Derek" essays. Jonathan Blow, the *Newsweek* "Indie Game Blowback" interview and GDC talks. Chris Zukowski, *howtomarketagame.com*, the PreMortem Games "Wishlists Aren't Milk" interview. Adam Saltsman, *Game Developer* "Profitable Indie Game Development" interview and the Finji publishing model.

**The essay through their eyes.** The Zukowski citation in section 02 (the 0.10–0.15 wishlist conversion) is *Zukowski's number used against Zukowski's actual position*. His framework treats devlogs as one channel among many and is far more interested in Steam page conversion, capsule art, demo timing, and festival placement. The essay borrows his statistic to motivate "marketing is wrong, the answer is open development", a conclusion he has never drawn.

Derek Yu would read sections 04 and 06 and shrug. His practice (prototypes "never made it out there", "that's probably the case for most developers") contradicts the prescription. He is the canon indie text and the essay does not name him once.

Jonathan Blow would call the essay the orthodoxy. He has spent fifteen years arguing the indie scene's received wisdom is wrong, and the essay is fluent received wisdom. *The Witness* shipped from a near-closed studio after seven years. The essay's framework cannot account for this without explaining it away.

The new *Airscape* paragraph in section 13 cuts the wrong way for Camp B: West did everything the essay prescribes. Devlog, forums, Reddit, PAX three times. He sold 150 copies. The essay's "openness is a practice, not a guarantee" response invites the push: if the practice does not predict the outcome, what is it doing in the argument? The honest answer, that it is necessary but not sufficient, is not the answer the essay wants to land, because the rhetoric is prescriptive throughout.

**Specific phrases they would single out.** "The risk of silence is higher" (section 13). Camp B's response: silence preserves the option to pivot, to rename, to ship a different game. The audience the essay celebrates is also the audience whose expectations harden around the prototype. Silksong, still unmentioned, is the elephant. "Polish is cheap; credibility is not" (section 13) reads to this camp as a slogan from someone who has not shipped. Polish is not cheap. Polish is what separates *Hollow Knight* from the 17,900 games on Steam that did not break through.

**Their strongest argument the essay does not address.** Asymmetric labour cost. Working in public adds a permanent overhead: comments to triage, expectations to manage, devlogs to write, a Discord to moderate, a parasocial relationship that cannot be turned off. Derek Yu and Jonathan Blow's implicit position is that this overhead is the difference between shipping and not shipping. The essay treats the overhead as a feature ("the audience asks the questions the model does not"). Camp B treats it as a tax that scales with the audience, paid out of the only finite resource a solo developer has.

**Where the engage-fixes helped or hurt.** The *Airscape* addition helps with intellectual honesty and hurts with rhetorical force. The "spectrum, not a binary" definitional anchor in section 02 helps with definitional slippage and hurts because it now lets a Camp B reader say, "fine, then your prescription reduces to 'be visible somehow', which is uncontroversial, and the rest of the essay is overstated".

## Camp C: The open-source-economics realist critique

**Idiom and worldview.** Tired, structural, allergic to vibes. DHH argues open source can thrive *without* market sustainability by rejecting market logic entirely. DeVault is the brutal version: "the open source donation model does not provide a sustainable source of income"; "you can make money in open source, but not by accident; you need a monetization plan". Spolsky's "Strategy Letter V" is the cynical lens: open-source the complement to drive demand for the proprietary layer you sell. Cantrill catalogues the corporate anti-patterns. Romantic open-source rhetoric is the failure mode that produces burnout.

**Sources studied.** DHH, "The Perils of Mixing Open Source and Money" (2013) and "Open Source Beyond the Market" (RailsConf 2019). Drew DeVault, "The path to sustainably working on FOSS full-time" (February 2018). Joel Spolsky, "Strategy Letter V" (June 2002). Bryan Cantrill, "Corporate Open Source Anti-Patterns" (FISL 2012, P99 2023).

**The essay through their eyes.** Section 13's "Open source does not pay. It pays for Anuke. It pays for tobspr" is the line DeVault would screenshot and reply to with one word: "median". His own essay's central claim is that the donation model fails as a structural matter, and the success cases built monetisation plans first and called them open source second. Wube sells Factorio without sales: closed-product, open-process. Bay 12 took twenty years on Patreon at near-poverty. Tobspr's *Shapez* is sold on Steam with a free open-source version as marketing complement. The list reads as Spolsky's strategy in disguise: every named case has a proprietary commercial layer the open layer commoditises. The essay never names this dynamic.

DHH would call the essay confused about its claim. If the argument is "open source is sustainable", his RailsConf talk *rejects* market sustainability as the relevant frame. If the argument is "open source plus a commercial layer is sustainable", that is Spolsky, not romance. The essay sits between and inherits the weakness of both. The MIT licensing of Volley! lands on Camp C as either naive (DeVault would warn of the commercial fork coming) or strategic (Spolsky would smile: code is the complement, the brand and assets are the moat).

**Specific phrases they would single out.** "It pays for Anuke. It pays for tobspr." (Camp C: name the median.) "The MIT-licensed repository" (the round-two essay does not specify when this is mentioned but the essay's commitment is on record). DeVault: "If you want to get your rent paid and put food on the table you need to be able to rely on something consistent". The essay does not engage with what consistency looks like for a developer one year in, ten thousand wishlists short.

**Their strongest argument the essay does not address.** The asymmetry between visible and invisible work. Maintainers in Cantrill's idiom are crushed by the visible part of the job (issues, PRs, drama) and run out of time for the invisible part (the actual code). The essay treats visibility as pure upside. Camp C has spent a decade naming it as a tax that disproportionately falls on the maintainer.

**Where the engage-fixes made things worse from this angle.** The new "I am learning the same cadence on Volley!" framing in section 04 and the section-14 doubling-down on Shuck-as-studio-built-around-this-practice now read, to this camp, as the author signing up for the maintainer-tax on day one without a hedge. DeVault would write back in two years asking how the inbox is going.

## Cross-camp: what all three would notice

- All three treat *visibility* as monotonically good and never name the cost function. Camp A: visibility of safety-research detail accelerates capability work at competitor labs. Camp B: visibility taxes the developer's only finite resource. Camp C: the maintainer's inbox burns the maintainer out. Low-friction participation is symmetric with low-friction extraction.
- The essay's three rhetorical homes (cases, principles, prescription) sit at different confidence levels; the prescription rides the principles' confidence and the cases' specificity while inheriting both weaknesses.
- "This is unacceptable", "the risk of silence is higher", and "polish is cheap; credibility is not" are the most quotable lines and the easiest to strip from context.

## Recommended responses

- **Camp A (selection problem and pause-incoherence):** *Engage* in section 11. One paragraph naming the actual selection mechanism: which lab the world wants at the frontier if any lab is going to be there, and why that is not a defence of the choice the named labs made. Without this, the "this is unacceptable" line continues to read as an outsider's incantation.
- **Camp A (Andreessen rhetorical reversal):** *Leave alone.* The essay is not in conversation with e/acc and does not need to be. Engaging would expand the surface for less gain than the cost.
- **Camp B (Zukowski misappropriation):** *Concede honestly.* One half-sentence in section 02 acknowledging that the wishlist-conversion citation motivates "marketing alone is hard for the under-resourced", not "open development is the answer". Zukowski deserves the credit for the narrower claim.
- **Camp B (silence as option-preservation):** *Engage* in section 13. The "silence is higher risk" line needs a sentence explaining what option silence preserves and why open development still beats it on expectation.
- **Camp B (Blow / Yu absence):** *Leave alone.* Naming them opens fronts the essay does not need.
- **Camp C (median, not top-of-distribution):** *Engage* in section 13. One sentence: the named cases are the top of the distribution; the median open-source-plus-game project does not pay; the argument is that the practice expands the right tail, not that it raises the median.
- **Camp C (visibility-as-tax):** *Concede honestly.* One line in section 12 or section 14 acknowledging the maintainer-tax as the genuine cost of the practice.

## What the essay still does well after round two

- The Asparouhova caveat remains the model: the strongest objection is named inside the section that makes the claim, with the source the objection would otherwise be raised from. Camp B's instinct is to look for this kind of move and respect it when it appears.
- The *Airscape* paragraph added in round one buys real ground against survivorship bias and was the highest-leverage edit of the round.
- The Valve closing line ("open at every layer that touches its players, closed at the layers that touch its money") now lands with the seam acknowledged. Camp C in particular reads this as the essay catching up to Spolsky.
- Section 12's "structural, not guaranteed" qualifier continues to do real work and is the essay's main inoculation against all three camps' base-rate objections.
- Section 11's named-safety-initiatives paragraph (RSP, METR, AISI), even though it creates a new vulnerability for Camp A, defangs the AI-researcher attack from round one. The trade is worth it.

## The single sharpest line in the essay

"The devlog is not content alongside the work. It is the work, shown." (Section 04 close.)

- **Camp A** would strip "the work, shown" and reply: yes, and the safety apparatus is also "the work, shown", and the essay refused to credit it. The line becomes a rhetorical concession.
- **Camp B** would strip the line and ask: shown to whom, at what hourly rate, with what conversion? The line reads as romantic without the case behind it.
- **Camp C** would strip the line and reply: the work shown is the work taxed. The maintainer's inbox is also the work, shown. The line, on its own, does not survive the question of who pays for the showing.
