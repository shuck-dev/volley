# Counter-point review of "The Case for Open-source Game Development"

A hostile-reader review focused on weakest seams, not on tone or copy. Each persona is read in turn.

## 1. The pragmatic indie developer's critique

- **Strongest objection:** The essay is survivorship bias dressed as principle. Every named case (Hollow Knight, Stardew Valley, Factorio, Celeste, Dwarf Fortress, Caves of Qud, Mindustry, Shapez) is a winner. There is no denominator. How many indie devs ran public devlogs in the same period and shipped to twelve wishlists? The 18,900-games-a-year stat in the opening section is used to motivate the problem, then quietly dropped: the essay never asks how many of those 18,900 *were* developed openly. If the answer is "thousands", the cases prove nothing about the practice.
- **Where the essay fails to engage:** Section 13 ("Open source does not pay") claims it pays for "Anuke", "tobspr", "Bay 12", "Wube", "Freehold". Each of those took five-to-twenty years of unpaid or near-unpaid work first. The essay never quantifies the median time-to-livable-income for an open developer. It also never names a single case where open development *failed* visibly. Where is the indie who livestreamed for three years, shipped, sold 200 copies, and burnt out?
- **Cases that look like cherry-picking:** Stardew Valley as an "open development" case is a stretch. Barone posted on a blog and a forum; he did not run a Friday Facts cadence, did not open the source, and the famous "no strategy" quote argues *against* the essay's prescriptive framing. *Hollow Knight*'s 2,158 backers came in part because Team Cherry already had Australian indie scene contacts and a Newgrounds presence: that is "having an audience", not "open development" in the section 04 sense. The essay conflates "be visible" with "publish source", and both with "build in public", and uses any case from any of the three to support all three.
- **Specific paragraphs at risk:** The Volley! mention in section 06 ("I am learning the same cadence on Volley!, one Friday at a time") and the closing section ("Shuck is a studio built around this practice") are exactly the move a sceptic will seize on as the author pre-claiming an outcome the cases have not yet earned for him. The "1.7 million / 41 million / 15 million" sales numbers stacked across sections 03–05 read as a highlight reel. The "the risk of silence is higher" line in section 13 is asserted, not argued: silence at least preserves the option to pivot.

## 2. The AI executive or researcher's critique

- **Strongest objection:** Section 11's central indictment ("This is unacceptable. If it were unacceptable to them, they would have stopped") is a debating-society move that ignores game theory. The essay grants the "someone will, and the safest version is built by the people paying attention" position, names it, and then dismisses it with one sentence. That sentence is the entire load-bearing rebuttal of the lab leadership's actual argument. A serious engagement would address race dynamics, the counterfactual of an unsafe Chinese frontier model, or RSP commitments; the essay engages none.
- **Where the essay fails to engage:** Anthropic's RSP, OpenAI's Preparedness Framework, DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework, the UK AISI and US AISI evaluations, METR's autonomy evals, Apollo Research's deception evals, and the substantial public interpretability work coming out of Anthropic and DeepMind all go unmentioned. The essay says "Open evaluation, open weights at the scales where openness is responsible, and open research into alignment are the levers a public can actually pull" without naming a single existing initiative doing exactly that. The reader cannot tell whether the author is unaware or intentionally omitting.
- **Cases that look like cherry-picking:** The roll call of signatories (Hinton, Bengio, Altman, Hassabis, Amodei, Sutskever, Russell) treats as a single bloc people whose actual positions diverge sharply: Hinton resigned, Bengio chairs an international report, Russell wrote a book, Amodei runs Anthropic, Altman runs OpenAI. Lumping them together and then accusing "them" collectively of contradiction obscures the real argument.
- **Specific paragraphs at risk:** "Only pressure from outside the labs might" is hand-waving. What pressure, applied by whom, in what venue, with what mechanism? The essay has spent ten thousand words on concrete cases and at the climactic moment offers an abstract noun. The "10% likelihood" framing also strips Anthropic's published context, which a researcher would notice immediately.

## 3. The games journalist's critique

- **Strongest objection:** The essay romanticises labour. Bay 12 is twenty years of two brothers living near-poverty before the Steam release; Tarn's hand and back issues drove the Steam release more than community generosity did. *Caves of Qud*'s nine years of Early Access is framed as a "positive lifestyle" choice; a journalist would ask what the team's hourly rate worked out to. The Hollow Knight "Silksong" delay (announced 2019, still a saga in 2026) is the obvious counter to the framing of Team Cherry as a model: the open-development community that the essay celebrates is the same community that has spent years in escalating frustration with the studio's silence. The essay does not name Silksong once.
- **Where the essay fails to engage:** Wube's actual labour conditions inside the Prague office; the toxic side of the *Factorio* community around mod creators; the fact that "Friday Facts has readers who do not play the game" cuts both ways (parasocial audiences can also become hostile audiences). The Davey Wreden / *Wanderstop* citation in section 13 uses a burnout disclosure as a positive case for openness without acknowledging that the burnout came partly *from* the openness of *The Stanley Parable* / *The Beginner's Guide* reception. *Cuphead*'s seven-year development under a remortgaged-house regime is not in the essay; it is the strongest "open development plus near-ruinous personal cost" case the essay could honestly cite, and its absence is conspicuous.
- **Cases that look like cherry-picking:** Valve in section 10 is the biggest one. The essay concedes "Steam itself is proprietary. Valve takes a 30% cut. Source 2 is not open. Half-Life 3 is silent" in a single tucked-in caveat paragraph, then closes with "Open development is not a constraint of scale. It is a strategy that scales." The caveats undercut the conclusion harder than the essay admits. Valve is the largest closed-shop in PC gaming with an open-coloured veneer at the edges; calling its posture "closer to open development than its peers" is true and trivial, since its peers are Epic, EA, and Ubisoft.
- **Specific paragraphs at risk:** The Double Fine PsychOdyssey paragraph treats the documentary as proof the discipline survived Microsoft acquisition, when in fact Double Fine's output cadence post-acquisition has been the slowest of its history and the studio has had layoffs that the documentary did not, in the end, prevent or even fully cover. The Buffer open-salaries citation is from 2013–2014: the journalist will note the essay does not say whether Buffer still does this in 2026, or what the long-term effects on retention and pay equity actually were.

## Cross-cutting weaknesses

- **No failure cases.** The essay names zero open-development projects that publicly failed. A reader trained to look for falsifiability sees this immediately.
- **Definitional slippage.** "Open development" oscillates between (a) public devlog, (b) community feedback loop, (c) open source code, (d) open documentation, (e) community-co-design. Cases prove different ones, and the essay inherits the strength of whichever subset the reader is most sympathetic to.
- **No base rates.** Sales figures are absolute, never relative to the cohort that tried the same practice and failed.
- **The author's own project is the unstated stake.** Volley! is mentioned three times. A hostile reader concludes the essay is partly a justification of choices already made.
- **The AI section's pivot is too fast.** Section 11 swings from "I used Claude to write this" to "the labs are unacceptable" to "open development helps here too" in five paragraphs. Each move is defensible; the sequence reads as motivated reasoning.

## Recommended responses

- **No failure cases:** *Engage.* Add one paragraph in the closing section or in section 13 acknowledging at least one named open-development project that did not find an audience. The essay's argument is *strengthened*, not weakened, by naming one. Without it the survivorship objection lands clean.
- **Definitional slippage:** *Engage.* Add a one-paragraph definitional anchor in section 02 (the nut graf seat) clarifying that "open development" is a family of practices on a spectrum, not a single binary.
- **No base rates:** *Concede honestly.* One line in section 02 or section 14: the cases are not a controlled study; they are existence proofs that the practice can work, against a default that is failing.
- **AI labs counter-argument under-engaged:** *Engage.* Section 11 should name at least three concrete safety initiatives (Anthropic RSP, UK AISI evaluations, METR autonomy evals, or interpretability research) before the "this is unacceptable" line. The line lands harder when the reader sees the author has read the steelman.
- **Valve caveats undercut conclusion:** *Engage.* The closing line of section 10 should acknowledge what is genuinely closed at Valve, not just nod at it. A sharper close: "Open at every layer that touches its players; closed at the layers that touch its money. The strategy scales; the openness has limits."
- **Stardew/Hollow Knight conflation:** *Concede honestly.* Add a half-sentence in sections 03 and 04 distinguishing "having a public presence" from "open source": both are useful, both belong in the family, the cases prove different members of it.
- **Silksong / Cuphead absences:** *Leave alone.* The essay's argument does not require resolving the Silksong delay or Cuphead's labour conditions; engaging would open new fronts for the same gain a one-line caveat already buys.
- **Volley! mentions as motivated reasoning:** *Leave alone.* The preface already declares the speaker. A reader who reads it as self-justification was never going to be persuaded.

## What the essay does well against hostile reading

- The Asparouhova caveat in the inclusivity section is genuinely the model: it pre-empts the strongest objection inside the section that makes the claim, with the source the objection would otherwise be raised from.
- Section 11's opening admission ("This essay was written with AI") disarms the most obvious bad-faith attack.
- The Valve caveats paragraph at the end of section 10, while undercutting more than the section admits, is the right *kind* of move.
- The "structural, not guaranteed" qualifier in section 12 is doing real work and deflects a lot of the survivorship attack at low cost.
- Naming the "smaller failure mode" in section 11 (a culture that stops practising the craft) is sharper than the standard AI-doom framing and harder to dismiss.
- The fears section (13) is short, calm, and concedes the objections before answering them. Hostile readers respect this even when they disagree with the answers.

## Lines specifically vulnerable to misreading or out-of-context excerpting

- "This is unacceptable. If it were unacceptable to them, they would have stopped." (section 11) — quotable as combative; loses the surrounding qualification when excerpted.
- "Polish is cheap; credibility is not." (section 13) — reads like a slogan; will be screenshotted and stripped from the cases that earn it.
- "Open development is not a constraint of scale. It is a strategy that scales." (section 10) — directly contradicted by the caveat paragraph immediately above it; an excerpt would expose the seam.
- "The risk of silence is higher." (section 13) — assertion without grounding; will be quoted by people the essay did not intend to enable.
- "Asset creation is the part of game-making most identifiably a human hand." (section 11) — invites a "what about programmers / designers / writers?" pile-on the essay does not need.
- "Almost every Valve franchise except Half-Life itself originated outside Valve." (section 10) — true but easily rebutted by anyone who points out the franchises were *acquired*, not co-developed openly.
- "It pays for Anuke. It pays for tobspr." (section 13) — invites the reply "name the median, not the top of the distribution".
