# Facet 3: Is mind a quantity you can add?

**Facet pairing:** Hard problem of consciousness (Chalmers, Nagel) + scaling laws and the "more compute closes the gap" claim.

**Fusion verdict: REAL, but framing matters.** The engineering posture of "more scale closes the gap" embeds a metaphysical commitment: that mind (proxied as general intelligence / benchmark capability) is a continuous quantity on the same axis as compute and data. That is exactly the claim the hard problem challenges. The fusion holds if the essay states the commitment is *implicit and unacknowledged*. Saying the labs explicitly claim to solve the hard problem is a strawman; their stated target is capability, not phenomenal experience.

---

## Ancient form: the hard problem and the explanatory gap

**David Chalmers, "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness," *Journal of Consciousness Studies* 2(3), 1995; expanded in *The Conscious Mind* (Oxford University Press, 1996).**

Chalmers distinguishes two classes of problem:

- **Easy problems:** explaining cognitive functions (attention, discrimination, integration of information, memory, learning, behavioural control). "Easy" does not mean trivial; it means they are tractable by the standard methods of science (mechanisms, functions, processing).
- **Hard problem:** explaining why *any* of those physical/functional processes give rise to subjective experience at all. "Even after we have explained the performance of all the cognitive and behavioural functions in the vicinity of experience... there may still remain a further question: why is the performance of these functions accompanied by experience?"

The **explanatory gap** (a term coined by Joseph Levine, 1983, adopted by Chalmers) names the structural discontinuity: a complete functional account leaves unanswered why those functions *feel* like anything. Chalmers states: "There is an explanatory gap between the functions and experience, and we need an explanatory bridge to cross it."

**Zombie argument (Chalmers):** A physical duplicate of a human, functionally identical in every respect, is conceivable without consciousness ("philosophical zombie"). If coherent, phenomenal experience is not a functional property. Therefore, functional completeness (more capability, more scale) cannot by itself guarantee phenomenal closure.

Source: [https://consc.net/papers/facing.pdf](https://consc.net/papers/facing.pdf)

**Thomas Nagel, "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?", *The Philosophical Review*, October 1974.**

Central claim (verbatim): "An organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism -- something it is like *for* the organism."

Nagel calls this the *subjective character of experience* (also: qualia, phenomenal experience, raw feels). His bat thought experiment: no accumulation of objective, third-person description of bat echolocation tells us what it is *like* to perceive via echolocation. "Imagining one has webbing on one's arms, poor vision, and perceives through high-frequency sound signals would only indicate what it would be like *for us* to behave as a bat behaves."

The point is structural: subjective character resists translation into objective description not because we lack data but because the gap is *between description and experience*, not between thin and rich description. More description (more compute, more data, richer representations) does not close a gap that is categorical.

Nagel argued that reductionist theories of mind, which analyse it in physical terms, "can never succeed" because the essential subjectivity of conscious experience cannot be captured in third-person terms.

Source: [https://philosophy.uconn.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/365/2020/03/Nagel-What-is-it-like-to-be-a-bat.pdf](https://philosophy.uconn.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/365/2020/03/Nagel-What-is-it-like-to-be-a-bat.pdf)

---

**Joseph Levine, "Materialism and Qualia: The Explanatory Gap," *Pacific Philosophical Quarterly*, 1983.**

Coined "explanatory gap" -- the formal name for the discontinuity between physical/functional accounts and phenomenal experience. Cited in Chalmers as the terminological anchor for the hard problem's central difficulty.

## Present form: scaling laws and the plateau

**Kaplan et al. (OpenAI), "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models," 2020.**

Foundational empirical paper showing predictable power-law improvements in loss with model size, data volume, and compute budget. This became the empirical backbone of the claim that "more scale closes the gap." Established the template for interpreting capability gains as a smooth, continuous function of resource input (mind as a quantity you can dial up).

Not fetched directly; background consensus confirmed across multiple retrieved sources. URL: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361)

---

**Ilya Sutskever, NeurIPS 2024 talk, December 2024.**

Verbatim (reported): "Pre-training as we know it will unquestionably end... because we have but one internet." And: "We've achieved peak data." Sutskever compared data to "the fossil fuel of AI." He described data exhaustion as the ceiling for Transformer-based pretraining and called the current moment a return to "the age of wonder and discovery" after the age of scaling.

This is the scaling-law inflection stated explicitly by a central architect of the scaling era. The claim is not that capability is stuck, but that the specific mechanism (pretraining on internet-scale data) has hit its limit.

Source: Techmeme / The Verge, [https://www.techmeme.com/241213/p33](https://www.techmeme.com/241213/p33)

---

**2025 survey of 475 AI researchers.**

76% responded "unlikely" or "very unlikely" that simply scaling current LLMs would reach AGI.

**Observed capability plateau evidence (2024-2025).**

- Knowledge tasks (e.g., MMLU) show diminishing returns beyond ~30B parameters; reasoning tasks (e.g., GSM8K) plateau around 70B+ parameters.
- Falcon 180B (2023) outperformed by Llama 3 8B (2024): smaller models closing the gap via better training, not more scale.
- Industry has pivoted: test-time compute, Mixture of Experts, and synthetic data are the new scaling dimensions.

Source: [https://shaunyap01.github.io/articles/generative-ai-and-the-diminishing-returns-of-scale-are-we-nearing-the-plateau/](https://shaunyap01.github.io/articles/generative-ai-and-the-diminishing-returns-of-scale-are-we-nearing-the-plateau/); [https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02156v3](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02156v3)

---

**David Chalmers on AI consciousness (2023 public remarks).**

Chalmers is open to the possibility of AI consciousness, not flatly dismissive. He applies the hard problem equally to artificial systems: the mystery is equally profound for biological and artificial minds. He has explored "quasi-beliefs" and "quasi-desires" as useful predictive attributions to LLMs without requiring human-like consciousness.

Note: Chalmers cannot be invoked as ruling out machine experience. His position actually *sharpens* the fusion: the hard problem applies to AI too, so the labs building toward machine intelligence face the same unanswered question, whether they know it or not.

Source: [https://officechai.com/ai/im-open-to-the-possibility-of-ai-consciousness-david-chalmers/](https://officechai.com/ai/im-open-to-the-possibility-of-ai-consciousness-david-chalmers/)

## Load-bearing claims

1. The hard problem distinguishes easy problems (cognitive functions) from the genuinely hard one: why any physical process gives rise to subjective experience. The easy/hard distinction is Chalmers 1995.
2. Nagel's bat shows that subjective character is structurally inaccessible to objective third-person methods. No accumulation of objective information closes the gap, because the gap is categorical (between description and experience), not merely quantitative (thin vs. rich description).
3. Scaling laws (Kaplan et al. 2020) showed empirical power-law gains in loss with compute, data, and parameters. This is the empirical spine of "more scale closes the gap."
4. By late 2024/2025 the scaling consensus fractured: Sutskever announced the end of the pretraining era; 76% of surveyed AI researchers doubted scale alone reaches AGI.
5. The engineering posture of "scale closes the gap" treats mind as a continuous quantity on the same axis as compute/data. The hard problem is the direct philosophical challenge to that assumption -- making the fusion structurally real, not merely analogical.
6. Zombie argument: functional completeness cannot guarantee phenomenal closure, because phenomenal experience is not a functional property.

---

## Overclaim risks

1. **Strawman risk (high).** "The labs claim to solve the hard problem" is false. Their stated target is capability/benchmark performance, not phenomenal consciousness. The essay must state the metaphysical commitment is *implicit and unacknowledged*.
2. **Survey number (medium).** The 76% figure needs primary-source verification. Do not cite as a number until confirmed.
3. **Chalmers' position (low).** He is open to AI consciousness; invoke him as applying the hard problem to AI, not as ruling out machine experience.
4. **Plateau overstated (medium).** Diminishing returns on pretraining is real; it is not proof that AGI-level capability is unreachable. The essay's hedge ("the plateau might come long after it matters") is correct and must stay.
5. **Explanatory gap entailment (low).** The gap does not entail scale *cannot* produce consciousness; it entails we cannot confirm it from the outside. That nuance serves the essay's epistemic honesty.
