# Angle 8 — Accessibility, Inclusivity, and Compounding Audience Advantage

Reportorial notes. Direct quotes verbatim where possible. Primary sources favored. Honest about limits.

## 1. Celeste — Assist Mode and Madeline-as-trans

**Assist Mode ships at launch (25 Jan 2018).** Per Wikipedia and the Celeste Wiki, Assist Mode was added late in development ("a couple of day's work" per Noel Berry) and shipped in the base game. The decision was triggered by the Cuphead difficulty discourse of 2017. It was originally called "Cheat Mode"; Thorson renamed it because "Cheat" felt judgmental. Sources: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celeste_(video_game)>, <https://celeste.ink/wiki/Assist_Mode>.

**Thorson's public framing.** Widely quoted statement of intent (in-game Assist Mode description and press coverage, e.g. Vice 2018):

> "Celeste is intended to be a challenging and rewarding experience. If the default game proves inaccessible to you, we hope that Assist Mode will allow you to still enjoy it in a way that suits your style of play."

After speedrunner Clint "Halfcoordinated" Lexa publicly flagged the word "intended" as othering, Thorson agreed on Twitter and the description was rewritten with narrative designer Kathy Jones to: *"If the default game does not prove to be challenging yet rewarding due to inaccessibility, we hope that Assist mode will still give you that experience."* Sources: <https://www.vice.com/en/article/celeste-assist-mode-change-and-accessibility/>, Thorson tweet thread <https://twitter.com/maddythorson/status/1352404112260775936>.

Thorson in the Vice interview on design intent: *"The goal is a fluid experience where players are safe to float around between loosely-defined difficulty levels as suits them ... Hopefully players feel safe enough to experiment, connect with the story, and express themselves through the game systems."* (<https://www.vice.com/en/article/celeste-difficulty-assist-mode/>)

**Reception.** Broadly celebrated in press as a model for difficulty-as-accessibility (Vice, PC Gamer, Eurogamer). Clint Lexa's public critique and Thorson's public correction are themselves the cleanest evidence of the feedback loop: a disabled speedrunner surfaced a framing bug a neurotypical, non-disabled lead hadn't seen, and the text shipped better because of it.

**Madeline-as-trans, 6 Nov 2020.** Post: "Is Madeline Canonically Trans?" by Maddy Thorson on Medium, <https://maddythorson.medium.com/is-madeline-canonically-trans-4277ece02e40>. Thorson explicitly rejects the retcon framing and casts the disclosure as design intent that was present but unconscious:

> "During Celeste's development, I did not know that Madeline or myself were trans."
> "I wasn't fully conscious of them, and their metaphorical connection to the gameplay."
> "that was all unknowingly written from a trans perspective."
> "I would argue that Madeline's transness *is* meaningfully intertwined with her story, but the base game was subtle about Madeline's identity."
> "It's wrapped up in my personal gender identity and it's messy! I simply didn't know the words until now."

Reception: trans players had been reading Madeline as trans for two years; the post largely confirmed a community reading. Press coverage: PC Gamer (<https://www.pcgamer.com/celeste-creator-confirms-that-yes-madeline-is-trans/>), TheGamer, Happy Mag. The epistemic point: the trans reading emerged from the audience first; Thorson credits that community reading in the process of arriving at self-understanding. Broader audience surfaces meaning the author didn't consciously author.

## 2. Accessibility in indie and AAA — institutional infrastructure

**Game Accessibility Guidelines (gameaccessibilityguidelines.com).** Live since 2012. Coordinated by Ian Hamilton as a collaboration of studios, specialists, academics. Organized into Basic / Intermediate / Advanced tiers. Widely cited as a developer-first reference. Sources: <https://gameaccessibilityguidelines.com/>, <https://ian-hamilton.com/project/game-accessibility-guidelines/>, Game Developer history piece <https://www.gamedeveloper.com/audio/a-history-of-game-accessibility-guidelines>.

**AbleGamers APX (Accessible Player Experiences).** Design-pattern framework built from research by Paul Cairns and Chris Power (University of York) with Mark Barlet and Greg Haynes (AbleGamers). Two-axis model: Access (foundational) and Challenge. AbleGamers is now embedding APX Practitioners into AAA studios. Sources: <https://ablegamers.org/apx/>, <https://mcvuk.com/business-news/ablegamers-to-launch-a-new-scheme-that-champions-accessible-player-experience-in-aaa-studios/>.

**Ian Hamilton on accessibility-as-advantage.** Hamilton tracks concrete adoption numbers as the business case: The Last Door reported 14% of its first 150,000 players used the dyslexia-friendly font (shipped for ~2 days of work); Solara's highest-value IAP spenders were blind; MUDRammer had a 12% blind player base. Source: <https://ian-hamilton.com/> and <https://abilitynet.org.uk/news-blogs/accessibility-and-games-design-interview-ian-hamilton-video-game-accessibility-specialist>. The argument: accessibility is not philanthropy, it is audience expansion with measurable conversion.

**The Last of Us Part II (Jun 2020).** Naughty Dog's own detailed public breakdown: <https://www.naughtydog.com/blog/the_last_of_us_part_ii_accessibility_features_detailed>. 60+ settings; first AAA title playable start-to-finish by a fully blind player. Accessibility consultants credited publicly (<https://caniplaythat.com/2020/06/23/the-last-of-us-2-accessibility-consultants-advancing-the-industry/>). Ian Hamilton's aggregation of player response: <https://ian-hamilton.com/gamer-response-to-tlou2s-accessibility/>.

**Forza Horizon 5 ASL/BSL (1 Mar 2022).** Picture-in-picture interpreters for cutscenes. Playground Games hired a deaf-led casting company and native signers, not a single hearing interpreter. Sources: Microsoft Source <https://news.microsoft.com/source/features/work-life/forza-horizon-5-introduces-sign-language-support-throughout-in-game-scenes/>, Kotaku, Can I Play That interview. Honest limit: ambient/gameplay dialogue is not signed; Access-Ability flagged the inconsistency (<https://access-ability.uk/2022/04/25/the-flawed-inclusion-of-sign-languages-in-forza-horizon-5/>). Good data point on "partial access is progress; decorative inclusion is the risk."

**Xbox Adaptive Controller (2018).** Designed *with*, not *for*: AbleGamers, SpecialEffect, Cerebral Palsy Foundation, Warfighter Engaged, and individual disabled gamers iterated the hardware and the packaging (loops, hinged lid, unobstructed slide-out). Source: <https://news.microsoft.com/features/how-gamers-with-disabilities-helped-design-the-new-xbox-adaptive-controllers-elegantly-accessible-packaging/>, GameSpot <https://www.gamespot.com/articles/microsofts-xbox-adaptive-controller-went-through-a-unique-evolution-during-the-design-process/1100-6484917/>. Microsoft: "the Xbox Adaptive Controller looks absolutely nothing like the first prototype." Clean case of external input reshaping a product internal teams had already signed off.

**Hades God Mode (early access, Dec 2019).** Supergiant's framing: not enemy-nerfing but a self-calibrating damage-resistance scale (20% → +2% per death → cap 80%). Greg Kasavin: God Mode is not portable, "the way to approach difficulty settings may need to be proprietary to the game." Source: <https://caniplaythat.com/2021/08/11/hades-god-mode-explained-by-supergiant-games/>, Inverse interview <https://www.inverse.com/gaming/hades-god-mode-interview>. Early access IS public-facing development; God Mode was refined with visible player response.

## 3. Open-source community diversity — honest

**Octoverse 2024.** Geographic diversification is the strongest signal. India projected to overtake the US by 2028; Nigeria +28% YoY, Brazil +27%, Indonesia +23%, Philippines +29%. GitHub does not publish gender/racial breakdowns of contributors at scale. Source: <https://github.blog/news-insights/octoverse/octoverse-a-new-developer-joins-github-every-second-as-ai-leads-typescript-to-1/>.

**Outreachy.** Started 2006 (GNOME, women only); expanded 2015 to US residents who are Black, Hispanic/Latino, Indigenous, Native Hawaiian, Pacific Islander; now open globally to anyone facing underrepresentation. Paid three-month FOSS internships. 1,000+ interns by 2023 across 40+ communities. Sources: <https://www.outreachy.org/>, <https://sfconservancy.org/news/2015/sep/18/outreachy-expands/>. GSoC and Hacktoberfest are the volume-oriented counterparts; Outreachy is the explicitly demographic-targeted one.

**Caveats (do not erase).** Nadia Asparouhova's *Working in Public* (2020) — available at <https://nadia.xyz/open-source/> — argues that the "community" framing of OSS is overstated: most projects are maintained by one or two people, and low-quality / high-volume contributions are a maintainer burden, not a gift. Harassment of maintainers is real and underreported. OSS demographics skew male and skew toward countries with stable connectivity and English fluency. The structural claim ("broader pool") does not mean OSS has achieved breadth — it means the mechanism exists where closed development has none.

## 4. Community feedback catching what internal teams miss

- **Celeste.** Clint Lexa publicly catches the "intended gameplay" framing; Thorson rewrites it. Madeline-as-trans reading surfaces from the audience before the author consciously arrives at it.
- **TLOU2.** Naughty Dog publicly credits external disabled consultants for features the internal team would not have specced alone; the blind-playable result could not have been designed in-house without that input.
- **Xbox Adaptive Controller.** The shipped device is unrecognizable from the internal prototype. The delta is entirely external input.
- **Forza Horizon 5.** Hiring a deaf-led casting company rather than a hearing interpreter was an insight the deaf community provided; the alternative default (one interpreter) would have been worse and obvious only in retrospect.

The epistemic point across all four: the insight that improved the product was *not available* to the internal team at any staffing level. It was only available from outside. This is the structural claim the essay wants — not that open teams are nicer, but that they have access to information closed teams do not.

## 5. Compounding-interest framing — existing writing

I did not find a citable piece that frames accessibility as literal compounding returns in the financial sense. The closest adjacent arguments:

- **Ian Hamilton** consistently frames accessibility as audience-expansion with measurable conversion (dyslexia font 14%, blind IAP spenders, etc.). This is the business-case version. Source: <https://ian-hamilton.com/>.
- **Cherry Thompson** (cherryrae.com, Ubisoft, GDC) frames consultancy work as surfacing design wins internal teams cannot reach alone, but I did not locate a specific essay using compounding language. Source: <https://www.cherryrae.com/bio>, <https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/gdc-speaker-q-a-cherry-thompson-explores-disabled-representation-in-games>.
- **AbleGamers APX** documentation implicitly argues the loop: player data → design patterns → more players → more data. Not named as compounding but structurally identical.

**The essay can land the compounding framing from first principles without overclaiming prior art.** Recommended line: broader audience yields insights; insights expand accessibility; expanded accessibility widens the audience; the loop is positive-feedback. Closed development has a narrower input pool and therefore converges on local optima slower. Hamilton and APX supply the numeric half; Celeste, TLOU2, Forza, and XAC supply the anecdotal half; OSS via Outreachy/Octoverse supplies the structural analogue, with Asparouhova as the honest caveat.
