# Notes for a Book-Length Extension

The essay makes the case for open development as one practice. A book would break the practice into its constituent disciplines and argue each independently. Each discipline is a distinct production decision a studio makes, with its own evidence base, its own counter-arguments, and its own people. The essay treats them as one bundled case because that is what an essay can do. A book has the room to give each discipline its own chapter (or short part) and prove it on its own merits, so a reader convinced of one but not another still has somewhere to land.

## The disciplines, broken out

Each of the below is a candidate chapter. Order is a sketch, not a commitment.

1. **Open code.** The repository itself: licence choice (MIT / copyleft / source-available), public issue tracker, contributor workflow. Cases: *Celeste*'s player code on GitHub, Mindustry, *Cataclysm: Dark Days Ahead*, the Godot foundation. Counter-argument: piracy and clones. Answer: lineage, taste, audience.

2. **Open design.** Design docs in public, Friday Facts, public roadmaps, decision logs. Cases: Wube's twelve years of devlog, Bay 12's monthly notes, *Dwarf Fortress*'s bug confessionals. Counter-argument: the design changes weekly, so why publish. Answer: the change is the work.

3. **Open art.** The hardest discipline. Public pipelines, contributor credit, the AI line. Cases: *Cuphead*'s frame-by-frame discipline shown in production diaries; *Slay the Princess*'s Howard team on technique; the line the essay's author drew (no AI generation in art). Counter-argument: art is competitive advantage. Answer: the hand is the advantage.

4. **Open community.** Devlogs as a relationship, not a marketing channel. Cases: Team Cherry's *Hungry Knight* lineage to *Hollow Knight*; Davey Wreden's *Wanderstop* burnout video; Maddy Thorson's "Is Madeline Canonically Trans?" post. Counter-argument: parasocial risk and harassment. Answer: name it; mitigate it; do not abandon the practice.

5. **Open accessibility.** Lived experience as the input no internal team can supply. Cases: Halfcoordinated's correction to the *Celeste* Assist Mode framing; Naughty Dog's TLOU2 consultants; the Xbox Adaptive Controller co-designers; Forza's deaf-led signing casting. Counter-argument: tokenism. Answer: information, not virtue.

6. **Open business.** Open finances, transparent pricing, public Kickstarters, no-discount commitments. Cases: Wube's no-sales policy; Re-Logic's donation flows; Buffer's open salaries (transferable from outside games); the Hollow Knight Kickstarter's 2,158 backers. Counter-argument: competitors will undercut you. Answer: the audience stays.

7. **Open process.** The working day visible. Public retros, post-mortems, the third revision. Cases: Friday Facts #229 and #250 reversals; Klabnik's open notebook; the GDC "What Went Wrong" tradition. Counter-argument: failures look bad. Answer: hidden failures look worse when they leak.

8. **Open lineage.** Honest provenance. The mod that became the franchise. Cases: *Counter-Strike* from the Half-Life mod; *Portal* from *Narbacular Drop*; *Garry's Mod*; *Dota 2*; *Hollow Knight* from *Hungry Knight*. Counter-argument: it makes you look small. Answer: it makes you look honest, and honest is the larger thing.

9. **Open infrastructure.** Open source as the foundation under the commercial layer. Cases: Valve's Proton and SteamOS, the Source SDK, Blender's foundation, Godot. Counter-argument: open infrastructure is unfunded. Answer: the funding models exist; the essay names them.

10. **Open succession.** The next person in. Handoffs, contributors becoming maintainers, junior training that the industry has stopped doing. Cases: Outreachy, Octoverse, the Brynjolfsson / Chandar / Chen paper, the contributor pipelines of long-running open projects. Counter-argument: this is altruism, not strategy. Answer: it is both, and the strategy half compounds.

11. **Open failure.** How to ship a post-mortem. The discipline of writing what went wrong while it is still fresh, in public, with names on it. Cases: Daniel West's *Airscape* post-mortem; Friday Facts #250 walking back a redesign; Davey Wreden's burnout video for *Wanderstop*; the GDC "What Went Wrong" tradition. Counter-argument: airing failure invites pile-on. Answer: failures hidden are failures repeated; failures named are training material.

12. **Open critique.** Letting the audience interrogate the work in public, and answering them. Not feedback forms; published exchange. Cases: Halfcoordinated to Maddy Thorson on the *Celeste* Assist Mode wording; the Friday Facts comment threads that have been a peer-review surface for twelve years; Nadia Asparouhova's *Working in Public* read as a critique the open-development community absorbed rather than dismissed. Counter-argument: this is the slowest review process in software. Answer: the slowness is what produces the trust.

13. **Open prototyping.** Public, regular, time-boxed exploration outside the main project. Cases: Double Fine's Amnesia Fortnight (the model); the global game jam culture (Ludum Dare, GMTK, Global Game Jam, GitHub Game Off); Shuck Month as the studio's own version, run by participating in an existing jam. Counter-argument: the prototypes never become anything. Answer: most do not, and that is the point; the practice is what compounds, not the prototypes.

14. **Open lineage of tools.** The compound interest of one open tool enabling another. Cases: Linux under Blender under Godot under most of the indie pipeline; FFmpeg under almost everything that touches video; SDL under most of the cross-platform game runtime; Wine under Proton under the Steam Deck. Counter-argument: the open layers are unfunded substrates. Answer: the funding models exist; this chapter shows them, with numbers.

15. **Open money.** The funding stack for a small studio in 2026. Cases: Patreon (Bay 12, Freehold, hundreds of others); Kickstarter (Hollow Knight, Double Fine Adventure, the lineage); Fig (Psychonauts 2); itch.io (the indie storefront with no curation gate); Ko-fi; GitHub Sponsors; OpenCollective. Counter-argument: most projects raise nothing. Answer: most projects in any model raise nothing; the open model is the one where what *is* raised gets paid to a named person and shows on the receipts.

## Cross-cutting chapters (not disciplines, but threads worth their own chapter)

- **Closed development still produces hits.** Honest treatment of the cases that do not need open development to win: Balatro, Clair Obscur: Expedition 33, the AAA hits made in private. What they teach about talent ceilings, marketing leverage, and the conditions in which the closed model still works. The book's argument is comparative, not absolute.
- **The history of game documentaries.** *Indie Game: The Movie* (Swirsky and Pajot, 2012); Valve's *Free to Play* (2014); 2 Player Productions across Double Fine Adventure and PsychOdyssey; the broader documentary-as-devlog tradition. Different visibility surfaces for the same underlying practice.
- **The free software fork in the road.** Stallman / FSF / GPL versus OSI / MIT / pragmatist. Why the licence question is downstream of a deeper political question about who the work belongs to. The book takes a position; the essay only gestured.
- **The AI question, longer.** The essay's section 11 compresses a much larger argument into one tonally restrained chunk. A book chapter has room to engage Anthropic's RSP, METR, the AISIs, the international report, and the labs' contradictions at the length the question warrants.
- **The Spolsky frame.** "Commoditise your complement" as the unifying economic logic underneath the nine practices. Wube, Bay 12, tobspr, Anuke, Valve, the tools — every successful case has the structure Spolsky named. The book makes this explicit; the essay only nods.
- **The structural-conditions chapter.** The honest reckoning that everything in the book assumes a maker with the time, security, community and runway to take up the practice. Sennett, Mullainathan and Shafir, the labour-economics treatment the essay only hinted at.
- **Scrum, agile, and methodology imported from software.** Games are software, and the games industry has a complicated relationship with the agile and scrum practices software development settled on after 2001. Some AAA studios (Bungie, Insomniac, Ubisoft, Blizzard at various points) adopted scrum-flavoured process; others rejected it as ceremony overhead that kills creative iteration. The chapter takes a position: scrum's *cadence* (sprints, retros, demos, definition of done) is the discipline that pairs naturally with open development, because the sprint review *is* the devlog and the burndown *is* the visible record. What scrum gets wrong for solo dev (heavy ceremony for one person) is separable from what it gets right (rhythm, demos, visible done). Cases: Wube's Friday cadence as a sprint review the public attends; the Linear-ticketed open backlog as the public version of a private Jira; Shuck's own attempt to integrate scrum fully into open game development (the author writes from inside this practice — four years of scrum in finance applied to a first commercial game). Counter-arguments: methodology is downstream of culture; games are not software in the relevant sense; agile is corporate. Answers: methodology is *also* upstream of culture, the parts that pair with open development are the ones that turn ceremony into shared record; the rest can stay where it came from.

## What the book should NOT try to be

- A how-to manual. The disciplines are arguments, not instructions. Each chapter ends pointing at where the working developer can learn the practice; the book itself does not teach Godot or Patreon setup.
- A comprehensive history of indie games. The book uses cases; it is not a survey.
- An academic monograph. The voice stays the essay's voice — measured, plain, evidence-led. Footnotes belong in an appendix, not in the body.
- A jeremiad against AAA. The book's tone toward the closed model is the essay's: that is the system most people inherited, the book invites readers out of it, not against the people inside it.

## What the book can do that the essay cannot

- **Independent evaluation.** A reader can be persuaded that open code makes sense without being persuaded that open art does, or vice versa. The essay bundles; the book unbundles. Reader who argues with one chapter still has nine chapters to live with.
- **Specialist depth.** Each chapter can name conventions, contracts, failure modes, and tooling at a level the essay only gestures at. The art chapter can talk about pipeline software, contributor agreements, and the practical mechanics of paid commission flow. The business chapter can show actual P&Ls where studios have published them.
- **Counter-arguments treated seriously.** The essay's section 13 (fears) handles the bundled fears in one go. The book gives each discipline its own counter-argument chapter or sub-chapter, which is where most readers' resistance actually lives.
- **People, drawn fully.** Tarn Adams, Maddy Thorson, Ari Gibson, William Pellen, the Wube collective, Garry Newman, Halfcoordinated, the AbleGamers team, IceFrog, Chris Bratt's Valve sources, Clint Lexa, Davey Wreden. The essay names them; the book has room for the profile each deserves.
- **Geographic and cultural breadth.** The essay is anchored in English-language Western indie, with three named non-Western developers added late (Toge Productions in Indonesia, Polychroma Games in the Philippines, Rasheed Abueideh in Palestine). A book has room for the Korean indie scene's open-development lineage, the Japanese doujin tradition, the Latin American studio cooperatives (especially Brazil, Argentina, Mexico), the African mobile-first open communities (Maliyo Games in Nigeria, Kucha Studios in Kenya, the broader Lagos-Nairobi scene), the Eastern European demoscene roots, and the MENA indie wave the Abueideh case opens onto. The Octoverse data already points at this; the book can do the reporting.
- **A working appendix.** Tooling guides, contract templates, licence comparison tables, devlog cadence playbooks. Things a developer reading the book at midnight before opening a repository can use the next morning.

## Likely working title

*Open: Ten Disciplines for Making Things in Public* — or a title that does not lean on "open" since the word does too much work in the essay already. Better titles will surface during the chapter drafting.

## How the essay relates to the book

The essay is the lead chapter, or the prologue, or the proof-of-argument that the book then unbundles. If the essay finds its readers, the book is the next thing those readers might want. If the essay does not, the book is the next attempt at the same case in a longer form for a more patient audience.

## What the book actually costs

Less than the romantic figure of "a five-year solo manuscript" implies. The essay itself was drafted in a single intense stretch with the AI assist the essay openly names, and the cases, citations, and argument were assembled at speed because the practice the book describes (visible work, public research notes, contributors who carry pieces) is the same practice that lowers the cost of writing about it.

The book is real overhead. It is not a closed-door commitment. The realistic shape:

- **Order.** Volley! v1.0 ships first. Then the book becomes possible.
- **Form.** Drafted in the open, with notes and chapter outlines public from the start, the way the essay was drafted.
- **Help.** Collaborators, contributors, paid commissions for research and reporting where the geographic-and-cultural-breadth chapters need on-the-ground work the author cannot do.
- **Tools.** AI as a research and editing assistant on the same line the essay drew (boilerplate yes, thinking no), with the line stated openly in the book itself.
- **Time.** Months to a year of focused work after the prior commitments allow it. Not five years.

The book is the option the essay leaves on the table. Whether and when to take it is a decision for after the essay has found its readers and Volley! has found its audience. The cost is honest overhead, not a vow.
