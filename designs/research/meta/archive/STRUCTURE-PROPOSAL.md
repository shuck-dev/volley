# Structural proposal for the open-development essay

Internal working document. Not part of the essay. Companion to `STYLE.md`. The essay currently runs sixteen section files (00 through 15) and around 10,800 words. This proposal organises those sections into a three-part book structure, places a nut graf, recommends an epigraph, and asks whether a brief preface is warranted.

## Part structure

Three parts, named in running text rather than numbered. Part titles appear as page-break pages between section clusters; sections inside each part keep their existing titles and stay untouched.

### Part one — The mark

What it proves: that the discoverability problem is real, that recognition is a relationship, and that the relationship cannot be manufactured by a launch campaign. This is the promise and the problem.

Sections:

- 01 — The mark
- 02 — What recognition is

Word budget: ~650 words. Light, fast, and front-loaded with the central image (the developer alone with the empty repository) and the central reframe (recognition is made by people, not algorithms).

### Part two — The practice

What it proves: that open development is the mechanism that builds the relationship — across funding, devlogs, transparency, source, lineage, apprenticeship, inclusivity, and the largest existence-proof on the field. This is the proof.

Sections:

- 03 — Kickstarter
- 04 — Devlog
- 05 — Transparency
- 06 — Open source extreme
- 07 — Lineage
- 08 — Apprenticeship
- 09 — Inclusivity
- 10 — Valve

Word budget: ~5,600 words. The long middle. Order is deliberate: lightest case first (Kickstarter as the hand-off from problem to practice), heaviest case last (Valve as the existence-proof a sceptic cannot dismiss).

### Part three — The stakes

What it proves: that the same mechanism is the durable answer to the wider questions — AI displacement, the humanity of the work, the fears that keep makers closed — and that the close is earned by the middle. This is the payoff.

Sections:

- 11 — AI
- 12 — Humanity
- 13 — Fears
- 14 — Close

Word budget: ~3,200 words. Widens from craft to consequence and rings back to the opening image.

Section 15 (sources) is back matter and sits outside the part structure.

## Where the nut graf lands

The nut graf belongs at the hinge between part one and part two: the closing paragraph of section 02. Section 01 hooks on the particular (the empty repository, the Steam shipping rate). Section 02 reframes recognition. The last paragraph of section 02 already does most of this work ("Success is the distance between strangers and the people who already know who you are"). Tighten that paragraph into an explicit one- or two-sentence statement of the essay's claim — open development is the most reliable mechanism for an indie to be seen, and the same principle is the durable answer to the wider questions of how creative work survives — then let part two go to work without further preamble. The current closing line of section 02 is doing the job intuitively; the proposal is to make it deliberate.

## Epigraph

Three candidates, all drawn from material already in the essay.

1. Eleanor Roosevelt, *You Learn by Living* (1960), quoted in section 12: "Freedom makes a huge requirement of every human being. With freedom comes responsibility. For the person who is unwilling to grow up, the person who does not want to carry his own weight, this is a frightening prospect."

2. Gabe Newell at LinuxCon, September 2013, quoted in section 10: "What we became convinced of is that Linux really is the future of gaming."

3. Maddy Thorson, paraphrased from the Assist Mode renaming in section 05: the word "Cheat" felt judgmental, so it became "Assist". (Not a clean quotation; would need a sourced line.)

**Recommendation: Roosevelt.** It carries the moral weight the essay is reaching for, names the price of the freedom the essay is arguing for, and rings forward to section 12 where it appears in full. Newell's Linux line is too parochial for a title-page epigraph and lands harder where it currently sits, inside the Valve section. The Halfcoordinated exchange is one of the essay's most memorable beats but does not have a single quotable sentence that could carry a title page.

Suggested form on the page:

> "Freedom makes a huge requirement of every human being. With freedom comes responsibility."
>
> — Eleanor Roosevelt, *You Learn by Living* (1960)

Trim to the first two sentences for the epigraph; the third sentence stays in section 12 where it earns its place.

## Recommended movements, merges, splits

The current section order does not need re-ordering. The proposal is additive: insert two part-title pages (between 02 and 03, and between 10 and 11) and a preface (before 01).

Two smaller observations:

- Section 05 (transparency) is the longest in part two at 732 words and carries both *Factorio* and *Celeste*. It does not need splitting. It does need a clean transition into section 06 so the reader feels the move from "open about the work" to "open the source itself".
- Section 13 (fears) is the shortest in part three at 389 words. It carries the rebuttal element from Toulmin's model and earns its short length, but check that it actually answers the strongest counter-arguments part two has raised. If it does not, it is the section most likely to need a fourth or fifth paragraph.

No section should move, merge, or split on the basis of part structure alone.

## Preface

**Recommendation: yes, a brief preface.** One to two short paragraphs at the very front, before section 01, signed by the author. It exists to set expectations: this is an essay, not a manifesto; the author is a software engineer four years into a career building a first game; the framing is game development and the implications are larger; the case studies are real and named, the citations are in the back, and the reader is welcome to disagree.

Draft:

> A short note before the essay begins.
>
> This is a piece of writing about how creative work gets seen, framed through games because that is the world the writer is working in. It is not a manifesto, not a sales pitch, and not a complaint about the industry. The case studies are real, the people are named, and the citations are at the end for any reader who wants to check the work. The argument is offered in the hope it is useful. The writer is a software engineer four years into a career, building a first game in public, and is grateful to every contributor and every reader who comes along for the walk.

Set in italic on its own page, no title other than "A note before the essay" or similar. Keep it shorter than the shortest section; the preface earns nothing by being long.
