# Final-line candidates for "The Case for Open Development"

## What the line has to do
- Land the inclusio: return the reader to the developer alone with the empty repository who commits anyway. The image opens Section 14 ("Somewhere right now, a developer is sitting alone with an empty repository") and echoes the lone-developer-at-midnight image that opens Section 1 ("Somewhere tonight, a developer is finishing a Steam page no one will read").
- Carry the structural claim of the essay (visibility creates the audience that closed work cannot find) without restating it.
- Read as one line of human voice, not a model gloss. No abstractions in the last beat.
- Short. The current seven words is a good upper bound.
- No self-reference, no manifesto sign-off, no exclamation.

## What to study (research summary)

**The kicker, defined.** Magazine usage: the kicker is the last line, ideally a "single-sentence image or ironic twist" that closes the piece. Louisa Thomas (New Yorker staff writer) on why it matters: kickers are "really important, especially in a short piece," because at the end of a long piece "you trust it can be magical and can totally reframe the whole thing for you." (Longreads, "Sticking the Landing: On Kickers," 26 Nov 2024.)

**Know the ending before you write.** John McPhee, *Draft No. 4*: settle the ending before going back to the beginning, and when revision shows the planned ending isn't working, "your best ending is somewhere earlier in the text" — you were finished before you thought you were. Structure should be invisible, "about as visible as someone's bones."

**Inclusio (return to the opening).** Joan Didion, "Goodbye to All That," resolves with the older narrator naming what the younger one couldn't: "all I mean is that I was very young in New York, and that at some point the golden rhythm was broken, and I am not that young any more." The kicker drops from the city-scale meditation back to the single body in time. The technique: end on the same image you opened with, transformed by everything in between.

**The single short sentence after the longer paragraph.** George Orwell closes "Politics and the English Language" by collapsing his whole programme into one declarative line: "One can at least change one's own habits, and from time to time one can even, if one jeers loudly enough, send some worn-out and useless phrase ... into the dustbin where it belongs." The shape: a long argumentative paragraph, then a short verb-led sentence that lands.

**Lift to the universal, or drop to the particular.** Ursula K. Le Guin, *Steering the Craft*, treats endings as a question of what to leave out: the ending should feel inevitable but unforced, the structure invisible. The two reliable moves are the lift (from the specific case to the larger claim it implies) and the drop (from the abstract argument back to one body, one room, one act).

**The unanswered question.** Used sparingly in long-form because it can feel evasive, but a question that names the reader as the agent of the answer ends an essay on the reader's pulse rather than the writer's. The risk is rhetorical bloat; the payoff is that the reader supplies the verb.

## The current line

> "They are watching, if you let them."

What it does well:
- Lands the inclusio against Section 14's "no one will be watching" voice in the developer's head.
- Declarative, present tense, second-person addressed.
- Names the audience as already there, which is the essay's structural claim.
- Seven words; clean rhythm (4 + 3, comma break).

Where it could be stronger:
- "They" is a pronoun whose antecedent is "people who showed up to watch the work being done" — clear, but slightly diffuse after a long paragraph.
- The conditional clause ("if you let them") puts the conditional after the verb, which softens the kick. The pivot lands on permission rather than action.
- It does not return to the repository image. The inclusio fires on "watching" but not on the empty repo / first commit, which is the more specific image the essay has been building.

## Five candidate final lines

### Option 1: Return to the opening image (inclusio, image-bearing land)
> "The repository is not empty. It never was."

- Rhythm: 5 + 4. Two sentences, second one shorter.
- What it lands: Picks up the literal object from Section 14's first sentence ("a developer is sitting alone with an empty repository") and reverses it. The audience-already-there claim becomes a claim about the repository itself. The reader supplies the inference: visibility means others were already there before you noticed.
- Risk: Slightly riddling. A reader skimming could miss the move. But the essay has earned the riddle by Section 14, and the second sentence ("It never was") drops back to the particular and steadies the line.

### Option 2: Declarative kicker (Orwell shape)
> "Open the door. Someone is already on the other side."

- Rhythm: imperative, then declarative. 3 + 7. Verb-led, both halves.
- What it lands: Calls back to the closing of Section 13 ("none of it is possible at all behind a closed door") and answers it. Names the reader's action and the consequence in one beat. The "someone" is single, which is closer to the essay's lived scale than "they."
- Risk: Two sentences instead of one; loses the single-line discipline. Imperative voice is also slightly more sermonic than the rest of the essay.

### Option 3: The unanswered question (reader as agent)
> "Who is going to watch, if you do not let yourself be seen?"

- Rhythm: 12 words, conditional. Question form.
- What it lands: Inverts the current line. Puts the burden on the reader to answer rather than on the writer to assert. The "let yourself be seen" phrasing also shifts the agency from the audience (watching) to the maker (being visible), which is closer to what the essay actually argues.
- Risk: Questions can feel like the writer ducking the close. Twelve words is also above the seven-word ceiling. Probably the weakest of the five on pure rhythm; included because the brief asked for one.

### Option 4: Single short sentence under five words (the button)
> "Commit anyway."

- Rhythm: two words, imperative. As short as a kicker gets.
- What it lands: Lifts the verb directly from Section 14's third sentence ("They make the commit anyway") and puts it in the reader's hand. It is also the literal first action of open development — the git verb. The double meaning (commit to the repo, commit to the practice) is the whole essay compressed.
- Risk: Verges on slogan. Lands hardest if the paragraph above it stays exactly as written, so the reader hears the echo. Without that echo it could read as a Nike line.

### Option 5: Drop from the universal to the particular (Didion shape)
> "Somewhere right now, the first commit is being made."

- Rhythm: 9 words, present continuous. Mirrors the essay's two opening "Somewhere" lines (Section 1: "Somewhere tonight, a developer is finishing a Steam page no one will read"; Section 14: "Somewhere right now, a developer is sitting alone with an empty repository").
- What it lands: Closes the triptych of "Somewhere" openings. The essay has used the construction twice to name silence and isolation; the third use names the action that breaks them. The reader is placed back in the same room as the developer in Section 14, but a beat later — the commit has happened.
- Risk: Slightly long. Loses the second-person address that the current line has. Trades the punch of "you" for the pull of the recurring image.

## Recommendation

**Option 5** is the strongest single replacement, and Option 1 is the strongest if the author wants to keep the second-sentence rhythm. Both beat the current line on the inclusio test, because both return to the repository image rather than to the verb "watching."

Option 5 wins on three counts. It closes the "Somewhere" triptych the essay has been quietly setting up since the first sentence — a structural rhyme the current line does not collect. It keeps the present-continuous voice the essay has used throughout to mean "this is happening to someone right now, including possibly you." And it lands on action (the commit being made) rather than on permission (the audience being allowed to watch), which is the truer version of the essay's argument: the maker acts first, the audience follows.

If the author wants the kick of an imperative on the very last beat, Option 4 ("Commit anyway") is the cleanest button in the set and the most quotable. It works only if the paragraph above it is left exactly as written, so the echo from "They make the commit anyway" is audible.

If none of the five clearly outperforms "They are watching, if you let them" on a read-aloud test, keep the current line. It is already doing most of the work a kicker is asked to do, and the bar for replacing a working close is high. McPhee's rule applies: if the planned ending still works, the search for a better one ends there.
