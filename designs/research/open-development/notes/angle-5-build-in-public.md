# Angle 5: Build-in-Public and the Open-Source Lineage

Context for the argument that open development is a durable cross-discipline pattern, not a games-specific fad.

## Linus Torvalds and Linux

Torvalds posted to `comp.os.minix` on **25 Aug 1991, 20:57:08 GMT**, subject "What would you like to see most in minix?". The canonical passage:

> "Hello everybody out there using minix - I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be big and professional like gnu) for 386(486) AT clones. This has been brewing since april, and is starting to get ready. I'd like any feedback on things people like/dislike in minix..."

He signed off noting he had ported bash(1.08) and gcc(1.40). The post is archived at Google Groups and mirrored widely ([Learn Linux mirror](https://www.learnlinux.ie/content/linus-torvalds-original-announcement-usenet)).

Trajectory: from that hobby post Linux became the dominant kernel on internet servers, all top-500 supercomputers, Android (~70% of global smartphones), and virtually all embedded networked devices.

**Attribution clarification:** "Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow" is **Eric S. Raymond's** formulation (dubbed "Linus's Law") in *The Cathedral and the Bazaar*, first presented at Linux Kongress 27 May 1997 and published as a book in 1999 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cathedral_and_the_Bazaar)). Torvalds did not coin it.

## Pieter Levels (levels.io)

- **12 Startups in 12 Months** (started 2014): public challenge to ship a new product each month; Nomad List and Remote OK emerged from it ([levels.io/startups](https://levels.io/startups/)).
- **Open Startup dashboard** at [levels.io/open](https://levels.io/open) publishes live revenue and traffic for Nomad List.
- Publicly reported Nomad List revenue trajectory (per Latka, cross-referenced with Levels' dashboard): ~$120K (2017), $310K (2020), $505K (2021), $700K (2022), $704K (2023), ~$5.3M (2024) ([getlatka.com/companies/nomad-list](https://getlatka.com/companies/nomad-list)).
- Remote OK ~$2M ARR; combined portfolio ~$3M/yr as a solo operator.
- Stated reasoning (levels.io posts and Twitter): public metrics force honesty, attract early users, and give the indie-hacker community a benchmark.

## Buffer

- **Open Salaries**: "Introducing Open Salaries at Buffer: Our Transparent Formula and All Individual Salaries", published **19 December 2013** by CEO Joel Gascoigne ([buffer.com/resources/introducing-open-salaries-at-buffer](https://buffer.com/resources/introducing-open-salaries-at-buffer-including-our-transparent-formula-and-all-individual-salaries/)).
- Disclosed: every employee's name, role, salary, and the formula `Salary = job type x seniority x experience + location (+ $10K if salary choice)` with location tiers A ($22K) through D ($0K).
- Commercial effect in the public record: in the 30 days after the post Buffer received 2,886 applicants, up from 1,263 in the prior 30 days ([Quartz](https://qz.com/169147/applications-have-doubled-to-the-company-that-discloses-its-salaries)).
- Open-salary system has run continuously for 10+ years; [2024 retrospective](https://buffer.com/resources/salary-system/).

## Indie Hackers (Courtland Allen)

- Allen (MIT CS, YC W11 founder of Syphir) launched Indie Hackers in **2016** as a site for interviews with profitable bootstrappers that always asked the question other outlets skipped: *how much money are you making?*
- Premise: public revenue disclosure from indie devs, searchable product directory, community forum.
- By ~March 2017 (8 months in) Indie Hackers itself was at ~$7K/mo, ~90% sponsorships ([Software Sessions interview](https://www.softwaresessions.com/episodes/building-indie-hackers-with-courtland-allen/)).
- Acquired by **Stripe in 2017**; spun back out independent in April 2023.
- Notable founders who grew audiences through IH interviews: Levels (Nomad List), Jon Yongfook (Bannerbear), Damon Chen (Testimonial.to), among many.

## Theoretical framing

- **Eric S. Raymond, *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* (1999):** "Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow." Argues the bazaar model (open, parallel, early-release) out-debugs the cathedral (closed, staged) for software ([full text, First Monday](https://firstmonday.org/ojs/index.php/fm/article/download/578/499?inline=1)).
- **Yochai Benkler, *The Wealth of Networks* (Yale UP, 3 April 2006):** defines commons-based peer production as "radically decentralized, collaborative, and nonproprietary; based on sharing resources and outputs among widely distributed, loosely connected individuals who cooperate with each other without relying on either market signals or managerial commands" ([full PDF](http://www.benkler.org/Benkler_Wealth_Of_Networks.pdf)).
- **Clay Shirky, *Cognitive Surplus* (2010):** "The wiring of humanity lets us treat free time as a shared global resource, and lets us design new kinds of participation and sharing that take advantage of that resource" ([Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Clay_Shirky)).
- **Cory Doctorow, "The Memex Method" (2021, Medium):** frames public writing as a commonplace book that compounds: the act of publishing notes in the open is what makes them retrievable and generative years later ([doctorow.medium.com/the-memex-method](https://doctorow.medium.com/the-memex-method-238c71f2fb46)).

## Takeaway for the essay

Open development is a 35-year lineage across operating systems (Linux), startups (Buffer, Levels, Indie Hackers community), and theory (Raymond, Benkler, Shirky, Doctorow). Each case shows the same pattern: transparency converts spectators into contributors, forces internal honesty, and compounds audience over time. Games are late to the pattern, not early.
