# World and Narrative

## The premise (what the player thinks)

A plucky little paddle has a dream: beat the world volley record. It trains, recruits friends, gets better. Cozy, wholesome, idle. Nothing is wrong.

---

## The truth (what is actually happening)

Something is wrong.

The game is a fiction inside a fiction. The "world" of Volley Vendetta — the arena, the partners, the record — is a constructed reality. It is too warm, too safe, too perfectly framed around one very specific goal. Real worlds don't work like that.

The player is not playing a game about a paddle. They are inhabiting a delusion belonging to someone else — or possibly themselves. The paddle is a stand-in. The world record is a displacement. The partners are not who they appear to be.

**The exact nature of the truth is left deliberately ambiguous** — world building should plant the question, not answer it. Different players will land on different readings. All of them should feel valid.

Possible readings the clues should support:
- The player themselves is the paddle, projecting an abandoned athletic dream into a safe fictional container
- The game is being played obsessively by someone who has retreated from the real world, and we are watching them from inside their imagination
- The "world record" is a real number — a personal record, a date, a distance — that means something specific and painful to whoever set the goal
- The partners are imagined versions of real people who once believed in someone, and were lost

None of these need to be confirmed. The clues should make the player feel that *something* is underneath without telling them what.

---

## The clue ladder

Clues escalate across three stages. Early game: a feeling, nothing you could point to. Mid game: moments you can't quite explain away. Late game: the surface starts cracking.

### Stage 1 — Something is slightly off (early prototype through first partner unlock)

These feel like flavour. Players won't register them as clues on first play.

- The world record number is oddly specific — not a round number, not a Guinness-style record, feels like something someone actually measured once
- Partner names are ordinary human names, not sports/game nicknames — feel like people you could know
- The game occasionally uses second-person in unusual ways ("you remember what it felt like to be fast")
- A loading tip or idle line from the paddle almost references something outside the game, then doesn't ("I used to —" / "Back when we —")
- The arena has no audience, no judges, no formal structure — it's oddly private for a world record attempt

### Stage 2 — Something is definitely wrong (mid-game, mid-progression)

These are noticeable on replay but easy to rationalise first time.

- A partner says something that couldn't be in-game knowledge: a reference to time passing in the real world ("you've been at this desk a while"), a name that isn't their own, a memory that predates the game
- The personal best display shows a number that already existed before the current session — as if someone played before, or as if the record is being tracked somewhere else
- One partner's dialogue occasionally shifts register — warmer, more direct, less "game character" — as if a real person is briefly bleeding through
- An upgrade description uses language that doesn't fit its mechanical effect ("stamina — because you never knew when to stop")
- A milestone message congratulates the player on a streak that, if converted to time, is suspiciously close to a real duration (a year of sessions, a number of days since something)

### Stage 3 — The surface is cracking (late game, approaching the record)

These are hard to ignore. The cozy framing starts straining.

- Partner dialogue becomes more urgent and less characteristic — "please" where they'd normally be breezy, "not yet" on a milestone that should be celebrated
- The ball occasionally moves wrong in a way that can't be attributed to physics — too slow, too deliberate, as if something is resisting
- Visual artifacts: a single frame where the art style is different. Not a bug. When the player looks again it's gone
- The world record gets very close and the game's tone doesn't match — the music is still cozy, the UI is still warm, but something underneath is pulling in the opposite direction
- The paddle's idle quips shift from scrappy underdog energy to something quieter: "I just want to know if I can do it"

## The snap

When the world record is beaten, the expected celebration doesn't arrive immediately.

There is a pause. The ball stops. The partners stop. Everything holds for longer than it should.

Then the art style changes.

### What the art style shift communicates

The main game runs in clean, warm pixel art — deliberate, constructed, safe. A world that was designed to be okay.

The snap introduces a second visual language. It should feel like the constructed layer pulling back to show something underneath. Not ugly, not scary — just *different*. More raw. Less curated.

**Possible directions (to be decided in art pass):**
- The pixel art holds, but the background shifts — something outside the arena becomes visible, rendered in a different style (sketched, muted, photo-textured)
- The paddle and partners remain pixel, but a single new element enters the frame rendered realistically — a hand, a desk, a reflection
- Everything desaturates slightly and the linework becomes rougher, as if the dream is losing resolution
- The arena cracks to show a blank, overexposed white — not darkness, but too much light, like waking up

The shift should last exactly long enough for the player to register that it happened. Then the game continues — or offers to continue. The prestige loop begins. But the player now knows something the paddle doesn't.

### What is revealed

Something small and specific. A detail that recontextualises what came before without explaining it.

Possibilities:
- A date in the corner of the screen that means something (anniversary, elapsed time, something it couldn't know)
- A name — not the paddle's, not a partner's — written somewhere in the changed frame
- The arena, from outside, is a monitor. The monitor is on a desk. The desk has things on it.
- One partner, in a moment of clarity, looks directly at the player — not the paddle — and says something that was clearly meant for a person

The exact reveal is TBD. It should be one thing only. The urge to explain will be strong — resist it.

## Characters as clue vehicles

Partners are the primary delivery mechanism for narrative clues. Each one can carry a thread.

### Design principles for partner dialogue

- Each partner has a "surface layer" voice (their game persona) and a "signal layer" voice (what bleeds through occasionally)
- Signal layer lines should be written first and the surface layer constructed to nearly — but not quite — bury them
- No partner should have more than 2-3 signal lines total. Scarcity makes them land.
- Signal lines should work as surface-layer dialogue on first read and mean something different on second

### The partner who knew them

One partner should feel like they knew the player (or whoever is being projected) before the game. Their warmth is too specific. Their encouragement references things they couldn't know. They're not cheerful in a game-character way — they're cheerful in the way someone is cheerful when they're worried about you.

Signal lines might reference: the time before this game existed, something the player gave up, the fact that they're still here.

### The partner who is also confused

One partner should occasionally act as if the rules of the game are strange to them too. Not in a breaking-the-fourth-wall way — more like someone who expected something different and is quietly adjusting. They make the best of the situation. Their signal lines aren't about the player — they're about themselves, figuring out where they are.

### The rival who knows

If a rival partner exists: they've figured it out before the player has. Their aggression isn't competitive — it's frustrated. They want the snap to happen because they want out. Their late-game dialogue should feel like impatience.

---

## Tone boundaries

The twist should never be:
- Scary or distressing
- Explained directly in dialogue
- Played for sadness as a goal in itself
- Used to undermine the genuine warmth of the game

The cozy idle game and the unreliable fiction underneath it should coexist. Players who never notice the clues should finish the game feeling good. Players who do notice should feel like they discovered something — not that the game was lying to them, but that it trusted them with something true.

The snap is not a bad ending. It is a true one.

---

## Open questions for world building expansion

- What is the world record number? (Should be chosen deliberately once the broader narrative is clearer)
- What happened? (The real event that motivated the game-world fiction — to be decided before writing partner signal lines)
- What does the player do after the snap? (The prestige loop needs a new framing once the player knows what they know)
- Does the paddle ever understand? Or does it keep going, record beaten, dream achieved, unaware?
