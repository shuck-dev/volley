# Persuasive Reach-Out

A note on the craft of persuading by putting the reader inside an experience, rather than arguing at them. Companion to [`STYLE.md`](STYLE.md).

## The principle

A persuasive essay does not argue the reader into a position. It stands the reader inside the situation that produces the position, until they reach it themselves. The mechanism is **identification** (Kenneth Burke, *A Rhetoric of Motives*, 1950): persuasion is not proof but the audience coming to share substance, situation, or values with the writer, what Burke calls consubstantiality. Burke: "Identification is affirmed with earnestness precisely because there is division. If men were not apart from one another, there would be no need for the rhetorician to proclaim their unity" (https://www.kbjournal.org/clark-kbirc).

The empirical form is **narrative transportation** (Green & Brock, "The role of transportation in the persuasiveness of public narratives," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 79(5):701, 2000, https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1207/s15326950dp3802_5). A reader transported into a narrative adopts its embedded beliefs with reduced counter-arguing. Transportation requires attention, emotional engagement, and mental imagery together; any one alone is insufficient. John Gardner's "vivid and continuous fictional dream" (*The Art of Fiction*, 1984) is the craft name for the state; the dream breaks the moment something returns the reader to themselves as a reader.

## Techniques

- **Situational second person, sparingly.** "You" works when it is situational ("you are standing at the machine, the screen is bright") not evaluative ("you tend to avoid..."). One crisp situational address to establish presence, then first or third person for the argument. Sustained "you" becomes instruction-manual prose and provokes resistance.
- **Situational identification.** Place the reader in a moment both writer and reader recognise, so the distance to the writer's position dissolves before the argument is stated.
- **Show as human experience, not sensory detail.** A vivid image with no person in it is still abstract: no actor, no empathy channel. The same scene with a person living it is concrete, because the reader has a surrogate. Transportation needs imagery *and* empathy; a scene without a character offers nothing to understand. Always anchor sensory detail inside a person's moment, with that person's stakes visible.
- **The "you know this moment" open.** Describe a situation precisely enough that the reader recalls their own version. Trigger the reader's memory, not the writer's autobiography. Begin in the middle of an active moment. Pick the specificity that is vivid enough to be real but universal enough to be borrowed.
- **Guard psychic distance.** Do not zoom from an inhabited moment to abstract summary before the identification is secured; it breaks the dream and returns the reader as a sceptic.

## Failure modes

- Accusatory second person ("you tend to...") attributes a flaw and invites denial.
- Sustained second person reads as instruction manual and provokes resistance.
- Sensory scene with no person: imagery without an empathy channel; transportation fails.
- Psychic-distance violation: abstraction mid-moment breaks the dream.
- Biographical specificity that excludes readers who lack that life.
- **False identification:** placing the reader in a situation that misrepresents the facts. This is the manipulation line. The honest version never hides the facts; it delivers them from inside a felt situation rather than from a distance.
