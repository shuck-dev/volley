# Persuasion Audit: *The Case for Open Development*

A read of the essay against the canon of argumentation, rhetoric, and long-form essay craft. Line numbers refer to `designs/research/the-case-for-open-development.md` as it stands. Section names match the essay's running heads. Style guide constraints (`STYLE.md`) are treated as the boundary; nothing proposed here pushes against them. Where a fix would technically work but cuts against voice (manifesto sign-off, second-person command, signposting), it is not proposed.

## Techniques surveyed

A compact reference list of the moves the essay can be measured against.

**Aristotle, *Rhetoric* (esp. I.2, II.20–22, III.8).**
- *Ethos, pathos, logos.* The speaker's standing, the listener's feeling, the argument's logic. All three are required; one alone collapses.
- *Enthymeme.* A syllogism with a premise left for the audience to supply. Persuasion runs through what the reader completes, not what the writer states.
- *Common topoi (II.23).* Stock argument shapes: from contraries, from greater/lesser, from cause and effect, from precedent. Reaching for one before reasoning forward gives the prose its logical handle.
- *Prose rhythm (III.8).* The close of the period carries the weight; rhythm without metre.

**Cicero, *Orator* and *De Oratore* (on *numerus*, *clausula*).**
- *Clausula.* Engineer the cadence at the end of every clause; the reader's ear remembers it.
- *Arrangement (dispositio).* Lead with the strongest case the audience will accept; bury the weakest in the middle; close on the second strongest.

**Stephen Toulmin, *The Uses of Argument* (1958).**
- *Six elements.* Claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal. An essay missing any one feels off; an essay that names them in order feels schematic. The discipline is to carry all six and let the reader feel them.
- *The warrant is the move readers most often miss.* Naming the warrant (the inferential bridge from grounds to claim) is what converts a list of facts into an argument.

**Robert Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. 2021).**
- *Social proof.* Many credible others doing the thing reduces the perceived risk of doing it.
- *Authority.* Cited expertise transfers credibility; named expertise transfers more than abstract expertise.
- *Commitment and consistency.* A reader who agrees with a small claim early is more likely to accept the larger claim later. Stack assents from small to large.
- *Reciprocity.* A piece that *gives* the reader something (a fact they can use, a frame they can borrow) earns the right to ask for agreement.

**Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011).**
- *Loss aversion.* Losses are felt roughly twice as hard as equivalent gains. An argument framed as preventing loss lands harder than the same argument framed as producing gain.
- *Availability.* The example the reader can picture wins over the statistic the reader cannot. Specifics beat aggregates.
- *Anchoring.* The first number a reader sees sets their reference point. Pick the first number deliberately.

**George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" (1946).**
- *Six rules:* never use a metaphor seen in print; never use a long word where a short one will do; if it is possible to cut a word, cut it; never use the passive where you can use the active; never use a foreign phrase, scientific word, or jargon if there is an everyday English equivalent; break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.
- *Concrete language is moral.* Vague phrasing hides the actor; precise phrasing names them.

**William Zinsser, *On Writing Well* (1976; 7th ed. 2006).**
- *Clutter is the disease.* Cut every word doing no new work.
- *The lead.* The opening must seize the reader's collar, with a particular, not a thesis.
- *The ending.* Stop the moment the case is made; one paragraph too many is the most common fault.

**Roy Peter Clark, *Writing Tools* (2006).**
- *Tool 14: get the name of the dog.* The detail no general writer would notice is the detail that makes the case real.
- *Climb the ladder of abstraction.* Ground in the concrete, then reach for the broader meaning. Never the reverse.
- *Subject-verb-object, close together.* Move the reader through the sentence by getting the doer, the doing, and the done quickly.

**Verlyn Klinkenborg, *Several Short Sentences About Writing* (2012).**
- *The sentence is the unit.* Build the essay sentence by sentence; the paragraph emerges from sentences that earn each other.
- *Pay attention to rhythm, first and last.* The sentence has a sound; the sentence next to it has a different sound.
- *Most transitions should be deleted.* The shape of the thought is the transition.

**Trey Parker and Matt Stone (NYU 2011, "but/therefore" rule).**
- Between any two beats the connector must be *but* or *therefore*. *And then* is summary; readers stop on it.

**Ira Glass (storytelling videos, 2009).**
- *Anecdote then reflection.* The piece toggles between the moving thing and the line that says why it matters. All anecdote is gossip; all reflection is a lecture.

**Robert Boynton, *The New New Journalism* (2005).**
- *Curiosity gaps.* Each paragraph closes a small question and opens a smaller one. The reader is pulled by the gap, not the argument.

**Malcolm Gladwell ("Structuring Narrative: The Imperfect Puzzle", MasterClass 2017).**
- *The reversal.* Articulate the assumption the reader is bringing, show where it fails, replace it. Once per major part, staged not buried.

**Christopher Hitchens, *Letters to a Young Contrarian* (2001).**
- *Irony as the gymnasium of dissent.* The lightly-handled aside that quietly damns is often more persuasive than the direct charge.
- *Take the strongest version of the opponent's case.* A thin counter destroys the writer's credibility for the rest of the piece.

**Susan Sontag, *On Photography* and essays in *Against Interpretation*.**
- *The discipline of the close.* Look at the particular thing for longer than is comfortable; the argument arises from sustained attention, not from the assertion you came in with.

**Joan Didion, "The White Album" and *Slouching Towards Bethlehem*.**
- *The discipline of the lead.* The first sentence is the contract. Engineer it for sound, image, and tension.
- *Sentences as pacing instruments.* A long sentence runs the reader uphill; a short sentence catches them at the top.

**Rapoport's rules / steelmanning (Daniel Dennett, *Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking*, 2013, ch. 3).**
- Rapoport's four rules: re-express the opponent's position so well they say "I wish I'd thought of putting it that way"; list points of agreement; mention what you've learned from them; only then rebut. The discipline forces honest opposition and earns the reader's trust before the critique lands.

## Where the essay lands the technique

Five places where the prose does the persuasion craft well.

1. **Hook (line 10, "Fifty-One Games a Day").** Particular, image, contradiction, all in two sentences. "Somewhere tonight, a developer is finishing a Steam page no one will read. They will refresh the wishlist count… twelve." Concrete particular per Clark; tension per Zinsser's lead; availability per Kahneman. The number 51 is the anchor for the rest of the section. This is a textbook *Didion* opening, and the strongest argumentative move in the essay.

2. **The reversal in the Pope section (lines 76, 82–84, "The Author's Apology").** The November 2017 dithering reversal is staged exactly as Gladwell prescribes: assumption (open development is a vow), case (six years of devlogs), break (the April 2026 podcast), restatement (open development is a posture, not a vow). The reversal is structural, not buried, and it earns the section title. This is the single best Toulmin move in the essay: the qualifier ("not a vow") is dramatised, not asserted.

3. **End-stress on the closing line (line 284).** "The hardest thing for a new indie to do is be recognised. Open development is how a person's work gets found. Closed development is how it gets lost." Aristotle/Cicero on clausula; Klinkenborg on rhythm; Anglo-Saxon ending words ("found", "lost") that hit hard. This is the essay's strongest cadenced close. The triple is restrained and the final monosyllable does the work.

4. **Steelman of the closed-development case (line 266, "Closed development still produces hits").** Yu, LocalThunk, Sandfall named by counter-example. This is Rapoport in miniature: the writer concedes the case, names three real wins for the other side, and only then narrows the claim ("the most reliable path for makers without Yu's authorial standing, LocalThunk's talent ceiling, or Sandfall's bench"). The qualifier is the persuasion; the concession is what makes it land.

5. **The Spolsky frame inversion in "The Source as the Product" (line 101).** "Naming the mechanism does not weaken the case; it sharpens it." The writer pre-empts the cynical reading (this is just commoditising-your-complement, not generosity) by naming it first and absorbing it into the argument. Cialdini's commitment-and-consistency working in the writer's favour: the reader who agreed with Spolsky's frame in line 101 is now committed to the open layer / closed layer distinction the rest of the section relies on.

## Missed opportunities

Concrete, surgical adds or rewrites. None of these contradict STYLE.md.

### 1. The nut graf (lines 17–25, "Known Already") buries its own warrant.

The essay's plain-language thesis is folded into a long final paragraph that opens with "The practice that builds the relationship has a name: open development." Toulmin's warrant (visible work compounds into recognition) is implied but never stated as a single sentence the reader can carry through 280 lines. STYLE.md (line 320) calls for the nut graf at the close of section 02.

*Fix.* Add a single short paragraph after line 24, before the "practice" paragraph, that names the warrant directly in one breath: "Recognition compounds because work seen by strangers can be remembered, recommended, and returned to. Hidden work cannot." Two sentences, no signposting, no "this essay will argue". The nut sits inside the prose, not on top of it.

### 2. The Team Cherry section (lines 28–40) does not use its own anchor.

The opening is strong (three people, a one-star Newgrounds entry, some drawings). The number that would land — *2,158 people* — arrives on line 32 inside a longer sentence and is then repeated on line 40. Kahneman's anchoring says the first number sets the reference; Cicero's clausula says land on the loaded word.

*Fix.* Move "2,158" to the section's first paragraph and end the opening on it. Something like: "A one-star Newgrounds entry, some drawings, and, a year later, 2,158 people who showed up." The reader anchors on the human number, not the dollar figure, for the rest of the section.

### 3. "The Long Cadence" (lines 41–69) carries four cases in a row with no reversal.

Stardew, Dwarf Fortress, Terraria, Double Fine, Caves of Qud: five cases, all confirming. Parker/Stone's test fails between cases 2 and 3 and again 3 and 4 — each new case is *and-then*, not *but* or *therefore*. The reader's pace flags around the Terraria paragraph (line 47) because nothing has been at risk for three paragraphs.

*Fix.* Convert the Terraria-Stadia moment (line 49) from a closing aside into the section's deliberate *but*. Restage: "Stardew, Dwarf Fortress, Caves of Qud all built audience by showing the work. *But* showing the work in public also commits the maker to a posture they may have to defend." Then the Stadia paragraph is the consequence, not a coda. The same facts; new connective tissue; the section earns the next paragraph.

### 4. The Valve section (lines 173–191) misses the most powerful Cialdini move available to it.

The leak opening is excellent (blank password, a German hacker, the worst case). But the section then moves immediately into a confirming list (Counter-Strike, Team Fortress, Portal, Garry's Mod, Dota). This is social proof, but stacked too tight. Five mod origins in one paragraph reads as enumeration; one dramatised origin would land harder.

*Fix.* Either pick *one* of the five mod origins (Counter-Strike or Garry's Mod) and tell it slowly across two paragraphs — Minh Le's first beta in June 1999, the acquisition, the moment Newell hired both creators — *then* list the others in a single tight sentence. Or front-load the section with the *Garry's Mod* "$50% revenue share, full Source code in exchange" deal, because that fact is the most transferable evidence of the practice and currently sits buried mid-list.

### 5. "The Rung That Disappeared" (lines 127–141) does not use loss aversion where it would be devastating.

The section is full of declines (13% drop, 71% fall, 25% cut). These are losses, but they are presented as data. Kahneman says losses land twice as hard as gains *when the reader is asked to feel them*. The current prose lets the reader scan past them.

*Fix.* End one of the data paragraphs (after line 131) on a single human sentence that lets the loss land. Something like: "A computer-science graduate in 2025 is more likely to be unemployed than an English major." The fact is already in the cited tracker. End-stress on "English major" is a Hitchens-style aside that quietly damns the assumption the reader walked in with.

### 6. "Outside the Firm" (lines 142–170) opens with reflection where scene would pull harder.

The first paragraph (line 144) is structural reflection ("A playtest specialist can run a checklist…"). The Glass rule is anecdote-then-reflection. The Celeste exchange is the strongest scene in the essay and currently sits as an aside *inside* the third paragraph (line 150). Per Clark's "name the dog", that scene should open the section.

*Fix.* Move the Halfcoordinated/Thorson exchange to the top of the section. Open on "Clint Lexa, who has limited use of one hand, posted on Twitter that the word 'intended' othered players who needed Assist Mode." Let the scene play. Then the structural paragraph ("A playtest specialist can run a checklist") arrives as the reflection on it, doing twice the work it does at the top.

### 7. The fears section (lines 252–268) breaks Rapoport on the strongest objection.

Six fears are listed; five are answered well; the sixth ("The risk is too high for a solo developer") is answered too quickly, in a single paragraph, with the line "The risk of silence is higher." This is not a steelman. The full version of the fear — that openness front-loads emotional cost on a person already carrying the work — is the strongest objection to the entire essay, and the Wreden/Cuphead paragraph that follows (line 264) is the better answer but sits underneath the dismissal.

*Fix.* Lead the fear with the Wreden line, not after it. "Davey Wreden's burnout from *The Stanley Parable* came partly from the openness of its reception, not in spite of it." Then concede the fear at length. Then the narrower closing claim ("the alternative costs more in expectation"). Per Rapoport, list points of agreement first; only then rebut. The current order is rebut first, then concede; reversing it doubles the trust.

### 8. The AI section (lines 196–226) misses the enthymeme it has set up.

The argument that "the community is not [agreeable]" (line 222) is doing the heaviest persuasive lifting in the section, but it arrives on the second-to-last paragraph and is not given its own beat. Aristotle's enthymeme says: leave the conclusion for the reader to complete. The reader is asked to complete: "an LLM gives me what I want; a community tells me what I need." The essay never lets that completion happen.

*Fix.* Break the long paragraph at line 222 after "The community asks the questions the model does not." Let the next short paragraph be three sentences: "Why this trade-off. Why this credit. Why this cut." (already there). End the paragraph on a single line: "The model agrees. The community does not." The enthymeme finishes itself in the reader's head. Currently the prose says it for them.

### 9. "What Only People Can Bring" (lines 228–251) over-explains its strongest image.

The "pale blue dot" line (line 239) is followed by four sentences of explanation. Klinkenborg: most transitions should be deleted. Zinsser: stop the moment the case is made. The image carries; the next four sentences gild.

*Fix.* End the paragraph on "the experience of having been a person on this pale blue dot we call Earth." Move the feedstock-thinning point to its own paragraph or cut. The prose is currently doing the reader's work for them and losing the image to the gloss.

### 10. The author's note (line 271) interrupts the close.

The note "Shuck is currently one person, Josh Hartley…" sits between "The Fears" and "The Audience Already There", breaking the rhythm Mary Douglas's *Thinking in Circles* requires. The inclusio (open on the lone developer at the empty repository, close on the lone developer at the empty repository) is interrupted by a meta-paragraph. STYLE.md treats the close as a held register; a credit note in the middle of it cools the reader before the final beat.

*Fix.* Move the author's note to a true endnote position after "The Audience Already There", or convert it into a single-line preface at the top of the document (which STYLE.md line 333 explicitly recommends). The close then runs uninterrupted from "The Fears" into the lone-developer image, and the ring closes cleanly.

## Active breaks

Where the essay weakens its own argument by missing a craft move it elsewhere uses well.

### A. End-stress lost in section 02 (line 24).

"Recognition is made by people, not algorithms, and people become a community by being given the chance to watch." The sentence ends on the verb *watch*, which is fine — but the prior clause ends on *algorithms*, a five-syllable Latinate that drifts. The loaded word the section has been building toward is *people*; the sentence buries it in the middle. Per Cicero/Zinsser, restructure so the period closes on a plosive Anglo-Saxon noun. Suggested: "Algorithms do not make recognition. People do, and people become a community by being given the chance to watch." Same content; the kicker now hits.

### B. "And-then" between Bay 12 and Terraria (lines 45 to 47).

The transition from Bay 12 to Andrew Spinks is pure summary: a new paragraph that introduces a new case with no connective. Parker/Stone test fails. The reader's momentum drops at the paragraph break. A single transition word in the first sentence of line 47 ("Spinks held a longer line still", or "The Adams brothers' twenty years are not the longest cadence on record") would convert *and-then* into *therefore* without adding a sentence. The cases compound; the prose currently doesn't.

### C. The section names its own thesis (line 24, line 91, line 127, line 172).

Several section closes summarise rather than land. "The hardest thing for a new indie to do is be recognised" (line 16) is good, because it is image. But: "Open at every layer that touches its players, closed at the layers that touch its money. The strategy scales. The openness has limits, and those limits are what the company keeps" (line 191) — this is three sentences of summary where the section's loaded image (the blank-password leak, line 173) should have been recalled. The section opens on a great particular and closes on abstraction. Reverse the inclusio: end the Valve section on "a build account whose password, after careful consideration, was blank" or on the Steam Hardware Survey climbing from 1.0% to 5.33% — a number, not a frame.

### D. Passive close at line 268.

"Openness is a practice, not a guarantee. The practice still beats the alternative." The first sentence is good; the second sentence is generic. The loaded word is *alternative* — a Latinate that drifts and a noun the reader has nothing concrete to picture. The section's strongest sentence sits one paragraph above ("West wrote the post-mortem himself, plainly: 'I've actually never heard of a game released on Steam (with some level of marketing) that has sold less copies than *Airscape*'"). Engineer the section to end on Airscape's plain English and let the structural claim go unsaid.

### E. The opening of the close (line 274) does not echo the opening of the essay.

The essay opens (line 10) on a developer alone at midnight refreshing a wishlist count. The close opens (line 274) on a developer alone with an empty repository. Same image, but altered just enough that the reader does not feel the rhyme. Mary Douglas's inclusio rule (cited in STYLE.md line 150) requires conspicuous key words from the exposition. Currently the opening uses *Steam page*, *wishlist*, *twelve*; the close uses *repository*, *commit*. The two scenes feel parallel but do not rhyme. One concrete word repeated — *the laptop closes*, *twelve* — would cinch the ring. Suggested: end the close's first paragraph on the same gesture the opening ended on. Open: "They will close the laptop." Close: "They open the laptop and make the commit anyway."

### F. The "Outside the Firm" section breaks its own rule about scene-then-reflection (line 158).

"The insight that improved the product was not available to the internal team at any staffing level…" This is a thesis statement at the *end* of a section that should have ended on the strongest scene. The Forza Horizon 5 paragraph (line 156) is the loaded one; the structural sentence should have come earlier as setup, not at the close. Currently the section ends on reflection where it should end on the deaf-led casting company.

## Top three highest-impact changes

If only three edits ship, these three.

**1. Move the Halfcoordinated/Thorson exchange to the top of "Outside the Firm" (lines 144 → 150).**
The strongest scene in the essay is currently sitting as an aside in the third paragraph of the section it was made for. Opening on it converts the section from a frame-then-cases structure to a case-that-becomes-the-frame structure (Glass's rule, Clark's name-the-dog, Boynton's curiosity gap, all at once). Estimated lift: this is the single change most likely to be remembered an hour after a first read.

**2. Re-order the fears section to lead with the strongest objection's strongest concession (lines 252–268).**
Currently the section answers six fears in order of difficulty; the hardest one (the personal cost to the maker) gets the shortest answer and the steelman arrives below the dismissal. Reverse the order inside that single fear: lead with Wreden's burnout, concede at length, then narrow. This is the Rapoport move the essay uses well in the Yu/LocalThunk/Sandfall paragraph but breaks here. Estimated lift: removes the essay's biggest unanswered objection in the reader's mind without conceding the argument.

**3. Tighten the inclusio between the opening hook and the closing scene (line 10 ↔ line 274).**
The essay already has a ring; it just isn't rhymed. One repeated concrete word (the laptop closing, the wishlist number, the midnight) converts a parallel into an echo. Mary Douglas's structural minimum is met; the reader's body is not yet asked to feel it. Estimated lift: the close lands as the close, not as a coda. This is the cheapest of the three changes and the one most likely to make the essay feel finished.
