# Marketing Strategy: Volley!

Early-stage marketing strategy informed by competitive research into Pong, Breakout, and clone culture (see `research-early-clone-games.md`).

---

## Positioning

### What Volley! Is Not

Volley! is not a Pong game. It is not a volleyball game. It is not a nostalgia revival. Positioning it as any of these limits discovery to audiences that are either too small or too niche to sustain a commercial release.

The research shows the challenge of building visibility within a dormant genre label:
- "Pong" as a Steam tag has almost no search traffic
- "Volleyball" has 34 games on Steam, with even the best performer (The Spike) seeing modest PC engagement
- PONG Quest, Atari's own creative reimagining of the concept, found a small audience
- Windjammers 2, a well-crafted competitive disc game with real publisher backing and strong reviews, reached 50-100K owners

These are quality games that found real audiences. The lesson is not that they failed; it is that genre framing shaped their ceiling. Discoverability is harder when the category itself is not something players actively browse for.

### What Volley! Is

A **cozy idle game with hidden narrative depth**. The Pong foundation makes it instantly readable; the progression, partners, and story are what make it worth playing and worth talking about. A desktop companion mode exists as a secondary way to play, but the primary experience is fullscreen.

The positioning statement: **"A cozy idle game with more going on than it lets on."**

### The Genre Frame

The game should live in the intersection of three proven, growing categories:

1. **Cozy idle game** (Cookie Clicker, Rusty's Retirement: proven, growing audience)
2. **Simple mechanics + deep progression** (Balatro, Vampire Survivors, Ball x Pit: all multi-million sellers)
3. **Narrative indie with emotional depth** (Omori, Celeste, A Short Hike: strong press and community engagement)

Volley! does not need to compete with any of these directly. It needs to be discoverable by the same people who enjoy them.

### Tags and Discovery

**Primary Steam tags** (what drives browse traffic):
- Casual
- Idle
- Relaxing
- Singleplayer
- Indie

**Secondary tags** (what refines the audience):
- Emotional
- Story Rich
- Atmospheric
- Hand-Drawn
- Sports

**Avoid leading with**: Pong, Volleyball, Retro, Arcade, Classic. These can exist as tags but should not be the first impression.

## Audience

### Tier 1: The Glancers

People who want something pleasant and low-pressure to dip into. They care about:

- Low friction (pick up and put down without penalty)
- Satisfying feedback (numbers go up, streaks feel good)
- Visual charm (hand-drawn art stands out in a pixel-saturated market)
- Minimal commitment ($7.99, no time pressure, no daily obligations)
- The option to run it in the background on their desktop while they work (desktop companion mode)

**Where they are**: Cozy game communities, r/incremental_games, idle game forums, Steam discovery queue.

**What converts them**: A short clip showing the core loop: a rally building, a streak climbing, a partner saying something warm. The art style and tone do the work.

### Tier 2: The Noticers

Players who pay attention and discover the narrative layer. They enjoy the idle loop but start to wonder why the partners say what they say, why the world record matters so much, why the shopkeeper is the way they are. They care about:

- Emotional resonance (grief, guilt, reconnection)
- Narrative craft (discovery, not exposition)
- Feeling respected (the game trusts them to figure it out)
- Something to talk about (The Break is a moment people will want to discuss)

**Where they are**: Narrative-focused indie communities, emotional game discourse (Omori, Celeste, Spiritfarer audiences), YouTube/Twitch story-focused creators.

**What converts them**: A review or video that says "this game is not what I thought it was." Word of mouth from Tier 1 players who became Tier 2 players.

## The Narrative as Marketing Asset

### The Two-Layer Structure

The grief narrative is not a spoiler; it is a discovery. This is the game's greatest marketing advantage. It enables two separate conversion moments:

1. **Pre-purchase**: "This looks like a charming idle game I'd enjoy." (Tier 1 conversion)
2. **Post-play word of mouth**: "This game has something going on underneath. You should play it." (Tier 1 players converting Tier 2 players, who then convert more Tier 1 players)

### What to Reveal, and When

**Always safe to show**:
- The idle loop (paddles, ball, streaks, FP)
- Partners and their personality (barks, names, visual charm)
- The shop and item system
- The desktop companion mode (as a secondary feature)
- The hand-drawn art style
- The tone (warm, earnest, cozy)

**Never reveal in marketing**:
- The Break sequence
- The real meaning of the world record
- The identity of The Rival
- The grief narrative directly

**Let players and press discover and share on their own terms**: The Break is a moment that will generate organic conversation. Trying to market it directly would undermine it. The goal is to create the conditions for discovery, not to sell the twist.

## Phased Strategy

### Phase 1: Prototype (itch.io)

**Goal**: Validate the core loop with real players. Build initial community.

**Actions**:
- Publish playable demo on itch.io
- Open-source the repository under the Shuck Games org (builds credibility, invites community)
- Share development progress in relevant communities (idle game forums, Godot communities, indie dev spaces)
- Capture early feedback on feel, pacing, and visual appeal
- Begin collecting email addresses or Discord members for future updates

**Key metric**: Do strangers play for an afternoon without being asked to?

**Marketing spend**: Zero. This phase is about the game speaking for itself.

### Phase 2: Alpha/Beta (Community Building)

**Goal**: Build a wishlist base on Steam. Establish Discord community. Get the game in front of creators.

**Actions**:
- Launch Steam store page with trailer and screenshots
- Apply for Steam Next Fest (if timing aligns)
- Establish Discord server as the primary community hub
- Begin reaching out to creators who cover cozy/indie/narrative games
- Share GIFs and short clips on social media (the art style and partner personality are inherently shareable)
- Participate in indie game festivals and showcases where possible

**Key assets to create**:
- **Store page trailer** (30-60 seconds): Show the core loop in fullscreen. Show the art. Show a partner saying something warm. A streak building. The shop. End on a quiet moment. No narration. Let the game speak.
- **Screenshots**: The fullscreen experience: partners mid-bark, the shop, a streak in progress, the hand-drawn art doing its thing. Include one or two shots of the desktop companion mode as a secondary feature.
- **GIFs**: Short loops of gameplay. A partner arriving. A streak building. The ball bouncing in a satisfying rhythm. These are the social media currency.

**Key metric**: Wishlist count at Beta launch. 10,000+ wishlists before v1 is the target for a healthy launch.

### Phase 3: v1 Launch (Steam)

**Goal**: Convert wishlists. Generate reviews. Seed organic word of mouth.

**Actions**:
- Launch at full price ($7.99, no launch discount)
- Send keys to creators who expressed interest during Beta
- Post launch announcement across all community channels
- Monitor reviews closely in the first 48 hours (respond to feedback, fix issues fast)
- Let The Break generate its own conversation; do not explain it

**Key metric**: First-week sales and review sentiment. Positive reviews in the first week feed Steam's algorithm, which feeds visibility, which feeds more sales.

### Phase 4: Post-Launch (Sustained Engagement)

**Goal**: Extend the tail. Pair content updates with visibility moments.

**Actions**:
- Release content updates (new partners, items, features) paired with 20-25% discounts
- Seasonal Steam sales at 30-40% off
- Community events in Discord
- Support creator coverage of The Break and narrative discoveries as they emerge organically
- Never discount below 50% ($3.99 floor)

**Key principle**: Every sale is paired with a reason to come back. A discount alone is not a strategy; a discount plus a new partner is.

## Visual Identity in Marketing

### The Differentiator

Volley! is hand-drawn in a market saturated with pixel art. This is a significant marketing advantage. Every screenshot, GIF, and trailer should lean into this:

- Bold shapes and confident colour
- Animation that breathes (idle animations, partner expressions, ball physics)
- The warm, intentional aesthetic of the constructed world

### Consistency

All marketing materials should follow the same Five Commandments as the game's art:

1. **Intentional**: Every frame in a trailer, every screenshot, every GIF is chosen deliberately
2. **Specific**: Show this game, not "a cozy indie game"
3. **Distinct**: Recognisable at thumbnail size in a Steam browse
4. **Diegetic**: Marketing materials feel like they belong to the game's world
5. **Breathing**: Use animation wherever possible; still images underserve the game's personality

## Risks and Mitigations

### Risk: "Pong game" perception limits audience

**Mitigation**: Never lead with Pong in marketing. The art style, tone, and progression are the first impression. Pong is the mechanic, not the identity.

### Risk: Narrative depth is invisible to casual browsers

**Mitigation**: The game must work as a pure idle game for Tier 1 players. The narrative is a retention and word-of-mouth driver, not a purchase driver. Do not try to sell the twist.

### Risk: Small outreach budget (5 points / ~£440)

**Mitigation**: The hand-drawn art style and partner personality are inherently shareable. The narrative generates organic word of mouth. Invest the budget in a small number of well-chosen creator keys rather than broad advertising.

### Risk: Low wishlist conversion in a tough 2024-2025 market

**Mitigation**: Price point ($7.99) is low enough for impulse conversion. Pair wishlist notifications with content updates. Steam Next Fest participation (if timing works) provides a concentrated visibility window.

## Viability as a First-Time Release

### The Market Reality

The numbers for indie games on Steam are not gentle. In 2025, over 20,000 games launched. The median earned $249 gross. 66% earned under $1,000. 2,200 received zero reviews. The market is enormous, but the attention is concentrated at the top [25][26].

The "first game tax" is real and measurable. A developer's first game averages around $120K gross, but that average is dragged up by outliers like Balatro and Vampire Survivors. The median first game earns far less, likely under $5,000. Successful developers on Steam have typically released 4-5 games; first releases are almost always the lowest performers [27].

### What Works in Volley!'s Favour

Despite the headwinds, Volley! has several structural advantages that most first releases do not:

**The art style and tone sell themselves.** Hand-drawn illustration in a market saturated with pixel art is immediately distinctive. The partners, the barks, the warmth of the visual identity: these are shareable without explanation. A short clip of a rally building with a partner reacting is all the pitch most people need. The desktop companion mode adds a secondary hook for the "runs while I work" crowd [28].

**$7.99 is the validated sweet spot.** Recent analysis identifies $7.99 as the optimal indie price point: it feels close to $5 psychologically while earning 60% more per unit. Content Warning ($7.99) sold 5M+. Rusty's Retirement ($7) sold 550K+. Cozy and idle games at this price show some of the highest wishlist-to-sale conversion rates on Steam [29].

**The itch.io-to-Steam pipeline is proven.** Webfishing went from an 8-day game jam project on itch.io to 3,000+ reviews and 10,000 peak concurrent on Steam. Celeste and Baba Is You followed the same path. The Prototype phase on itch.io is the right first step: validate with real players, build a small community, then bring that momentum to Steam [30].

**Software engineering background.** Higher code quality, faster iteration, and the ability to ship polish that many first-time game devs struggle with. A day job means no financial pressure to ship early, which is consistently cited as one of the most important advantages a first-time dev can have [31].

**The narrative is a word-of-mouth engine.** The dual-layer design (cozy surface, grief underneath) is the kind of thing that generates organic "you need to play this" conversations. Omori, Undertale, and Celeste all rode this wave. You cannot buy this kind of marketing; it has to be built into the game, and it is.

### What Works Against It

**No existing audience.** Starting from zero wishlists. Without a viral moment, festival pickup, or creator coverage, expect 1-5 organic wishlists per day from Steam discovery alone. That is slow [25].

**The cozy idle space is getting crowded.** Post-Rusty's Retirement, there is a wave of idle and companion-style games arriving on Steam. Volley! differentiates through art direction, narrative depth, and a unique mechanical foundation, but the broader niche is no longer undiscovered.

**Marketing takes real effort.** 54% of game developers do no marketing at all. Just being in the other 46% is an advantage, but it requires deliberate, sustained work alongside development [25].

### Revenue Scenarios

| Scenario | Wishlists at Launch | First-Year Sales | Gross Revenue | Take-Home (after Steam 30%) |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| Pessimistic (bottom 66%) | Under 500 | 50-200 copies | $400-$1,600 | $280-$1,120 |
| Realistic (top 20-30%) | 2,000-7,000 | 500-2,000 copies | $4,000-$16,000 | $2,800-$11,200 |
| Optimistic (top 5-10%) | 10,000-30,000 | 3,000-10,000 copies | $24,000-$80,000 | $16,800-$56,000 |
| Breakout (top ~0.5%) | 100,000+ | 100,000+ copies | $700K+ | $490K+ |

At $7.99 with a ~£21K budget, break-even is roughly 3,800 copies after Steam's cut. That sits within the realistic scenario. Everything above that is surplus.

### The Breakout Examples

First-time devs do break through. Every one of these was a debut commercial release:

- **Vampire Survivors** (poncle): Started as a $3 game, sold millions. Made "just for fun" after the developer had "given up on the idea of success" [32].
- **Stardew Valley** (ConcernedApe): 4+ years of solo development. 425,000 copies in the first two weeks. Now over 20M copies [32].
- **Undertale** (Toby Fox): Kickstarter-funded ($50K). Over 3.5M copies sold [32].
- **Balatro** (LocalThunk): Won Best Indie and Best Debut at The Game Awards 2024. Over 7M copies. The creator originally considered a Steam release partly to "help get a game developer job somewhere" [33].

What they share: a novel twist on something familiar, an extremely tight core loop, organic word-of-mouth and creator appeal, extended development focused on polish, and low price points ($3-$15). None relied on paid marketing.

These are survivorship bias examples. For every Balatro there are thousands of equally passionate first-time devs whose games earned under $1,000. But they prove the ceiling is not capped by experience.

### The Honest Assessment

Volley!'s design, format, price point, and niche are all aligned with what works on Steam right now. But the base case for a first game is modest revenue, not a breakout. The strongest framing: this is a game worth making regardless of commercial outcome, with structural advantages that give it a real shot at the realistic-to-optimistic range. The day job makes that sustainable.

The first game is tuition. Build it well, ship it, learn from it, and build an audience for the long term. If Volley! exceeds expectations, that is wonderful. If it finds a modest audience and teaches what game two needs, that is also a success.

### Events and Festivals Beyond Next Fest

Next Fest is the single highest-impact free marketing event, but it is far from the only one. The months between Next Fest and launch should be filled with a stacked calendar of themed fests, showcases, and award submissions.

#### Steam Themed Fests

Steam runs themed festivals throughout the year where **unreleased games with demos can participate**. Registration is free, done through Steamworks, and typically opens 4-8 weeks before each event. These are the most relevant to Volley!:

| Festival | Typical Timing | Expected Wishlist Impact | Fit |
|----------|---------------|------------------------|-----|
| **Steam Cozy Fest** | Late March / Early April | 500-5,000 | Directly targets the audience. Cozy, relaxing, low-stress games. |
| **Steam Narrative Fest** | Late March | 500-5,000 | Story-rich and narrative-driven games. The hidden depth is the hook here. |
| **Steam Indie Fest** | January (varies) | 500-3,000 | Broad indie showcase. Less targeted but good exposure. |
| **Steam Wholesome Games Fest** | Summer (varies) | 500-3,000 | Tied to the Wholesome Games brand. Cozy, positive, feel-good. |

**Strategy**: Participate in every relevant themed fest between February and July 2027. Each one compounds: a player who sees the game in Cozy Fest and then again in Narrative Fest is more likely to wishlist than one who sees it once. The demo built for Next Fest serves all of these.

#### Online Showcases

These are curated, streamed events with large audiences. Selection is competitive; apply with the best trailer and build available.

| Showcase | Typical Timing | How to Apply | Expected Wishlist Impact | Fit |
|----------|---------------|-------------|------------------------|-----|

Being selected here would be the single biggest visibility moment outside Next Fest. |
| **LudoNarraCon** | May | Apply via Fellow Traveller, ~3-4 months before | 1,000-5,000 | A narrative-focused festival that runs directly on Steam. Games get a dedicated Steam page during the event, which means direct wishlist conversion. |
