# Narrative discipline

How the prose, scenes, and dialogue of Volley get made. Not what the story is: that lives in [`outline.md`](outline.md). This is the craft the writing aims for, line by line.

The prose voice lives in [`../../ai/STYLE.md`](../../ai/STYLE.md). The world canon sits in [`../art/bible.md`](../art/bible.md). The protagonist's interior is in [`../characters/protagonist.md`](../characters/protagonist.md). The structural spine is in [`../concept/`](../concept/). Read those first; this doc tells you how to write inside them.

Every section below pairs a principle with a concrete pattern: a worked example drawn from a real or invented Volley scene, and where it sharpens the rule, an anti-example showing the failure mode. Copy the pattern; do not derive your own from the principle alone.

## Scene construction

A Reality scene is a room with several things going on at once. A kettle that has just clicked off. A photo on a side table, half tucked under a magazine. A back at the window, watching the street. The protagonist walks in and the scene does not stop for them; the scene was already running.

The puzzle the player is solving is presence. Not which dialogue option, not which item to pick up. Being in the room long enough to notice what wants to happen. The sister is turning a page; the protagonist can sit, or not. The kettle is cooling; the protagonist can pour, or not. Each interaction is small and contextual and reads as something a person would do without being prompted.

### Pattern: layer first, prune second

A scene that works has more going on than the player will touch in one visit. A scene that feels flat has been written as a list of interactions: tap A, tap B, leave. If three things are happening and the player engages with one, the other two were not wasted; they were the room.

Worked example, the sister's kitchen at the start of Reconstruction:

> The kettle has just clicked off. The sister is at the table with the photo album open, turning a page. The kettle finishes its tick, then settles. A magazine sits half over a photo on the side table; the corner of the photo shows the edge of a hand. Outside, a delivery van pulls away.

Four things: kettle, sister, photo, van. The scene runs whether the protagonist pours tea, sits, lifts the magazine, or watches the van. The first visit might land on the album; a second visit lands on the photo; a third notices the van. The writer authors all four; the player chooses what to carry.

Anti-example, the same scene written as a checklist:

> A kettle. A photo album on the table. The sister sitting. The protagonist enters.

Four nouns, no relations, no motion, no time. The room is a list of props the player has to activate. There is nothing happening for the protagonist to walk into.

### Pattern: place the nouns before the people

The geography is built before the people are. Place the nouns: the table is by the window, the book is on the table, the sister is across the table from where the protagonist will sit. The reader walks in behind the protagonist and finds the room already standing. Voice's place-the-nouns rule applies word for word.

Worked example, the cliff bench at the end of Part 2:

> A bench, dedicated to the friend who died. Behind it, the path back to the gate. In front, the cliff edge and the sea past it. On the bench, the shopkeeper, holding a phone.

Each noun lands somewhere relative to the last. The reader assembles the scene as fast as the prose lays it down.

Anti-example: "A bench. The shopkeeper. The cliff. The sea." Four nouns floating side by side. The reader has to reconcile where the second sits relative to the first; by the third the picture has fallen apart.

## Dialogue

Construction speaks warmer and lighter. Coaches perform a little; the friend at the stall keeps things easy; lines have lift. The voice is the voice of a place that wants to be enjoyable. It is not arch and it is not knowing, but it carries more colour than Reality does.

Reality speaks plainer. Half-finished sentences. The thing said while looking at something else. Long pauses where the prose holds its breath instead of filling. The sister mentions the weather and means the weather; the cashier asks after the protagonist and means it; nothing performs.

The protagonist talks less than they are talked to. Their lines are short. They answer when asked, and the answer is usually one beat shorter than expected. The interior is in the protagonist profile; on the page it shows up as economy, not as introspection.

### Pattern: Reality speaks plainer

Worked example, the sister at the kitchen table:

> "Tea's gone cold."
>
> She turns the page.
>
> "Brother used to do that. Pour one and forget it."

Three short utterances. The first is about the tea. The second is not dialogue at all. The third arrives sideways, looking at the album not the protagonist, and lands the memory.

Anti-example, the same beat in performed register:

> "Oh, the tea's gone cold," she sighed, glancing up from the album. "You know, my brother used to do that all the time. He'd pour himself a cup and just... forget about it. He was always like that."

The performed version explains the memory, performs the sigh, fills the pause, and softens the loss into nostalgia. The plain version trusts the reader.

### Pattern: memory arrives sideways

When characters share memories of the shopkeeper, the memory is the mention. Each character knew the shopkeeper differently, and the prose lets that asymmetry sit; the player assembles the shopkeeper from the asymmetry, the way grief assembles a person from the people who loved them.

Worked example, a coach mid-rally during a Reconstruction round at the canopy venue:

> The coach catches the ball, holds it a beat.
>
> "He used to call this shot the kettle. No idea why. He just did."
>
> The serve goes back out. The rally resumes.

The line names a thing the shopkeeper did, does not explain it, returns to the rally. The player carries the kettle forward.

Anti-example, the memory delivered head-on: "The coach paused, remembering the shopkeeper. He had been such a kind man, always full of strange jokes. The coach felt the loss keenly." The memory has been told instead of arrived at, and the player has been informed of the feeling instead of building it.

### Pattern: canon lands as action

A piece of world that needs to reach the player reaches it as something a character does in front of the protagonist, or as something the world shows. The locked gate is shown, not described. The number sits in the phone, ready when the player finds it.

Worked example: the player can dial the unnamed number from Construction onwards. It rings out. No system message explains that it is the shopkeeper's number; the shopkeeper just does not answer. The recognition is held until the cliff. The canon ("the protagonist has been reaching out, fruitlessly, the whole time") is delivered by the silence on the line, in the player's own action.

Anti-example: a flavour-text popup on first dial reading "this is the shopkeeper's number, but they are not answering you." The canon has been told. Every rally afterwards is poorer for it.

## Tone modulation

Tone moves moment to moment. Construction's default is the coach's grin between rallies, the friend at the stall calling a name across the court, the lift in a line that did not need lift. A meta-crack lands, the player notices and reaches for a way to read it, the rally moves on. The slip is one beat the prose commits to and lets land; the resettle is the rest of the rally. A scene that crackles for two paragraphs has overcommitted.

### Pattern: slip and resettle

Worked example, a meta-crack during a Construction rally:

> The coach's grin holds a beat too long.
>
> "Same time next week?"
>
> The rally resumes. The next ball is bright and the chime lands clean.

One sentence of slip, one line of dialogue carrying the weight, one sentence of resettle. The player notices and reaches for an answer. The rally does not stop to acknowledge what it just did.

Anti-example, the same beat overcommitted:

> The coach's grin held a beat too long, his eyes flickering with something the protagonist couldn't quite name. "Same time next week?" he said, his voice tight. The rally resumed, but the air had changed. The light at the edge of the court seemed cooler now, and the protagonist felt a weight settle in their chest that hadn't been there before.

Three paragraphs of slip and no resettle. The construct has stopped pretending to be the construct. The player has nowhere left to put the beat short of "the game is telling me the world is fake."

### Pattern: the break runs cold and short

The break runs cold and short. Sentences shrink. Adjectives drop. Light becomes light, sound becomes sound, the prose stops reaching for warmth because the construct has stopped supplying it.

Worked example, the moment after the championship win:

> The crowd. The chime. The number on the wall.
>
> The protagonist holds the racquet.
>
> Nothing happens.

Three short paragraphs. No adjectives. The third sentence is the one the reader carries.

### Pattern: dread as floor, not adjective

Part 2 defaults to dread. The temperature is low. Warm surfacings are rare and earned: a memory at a rally's apex, a small mercy from the sister, a cup of tea poured without comment. The contrast is the point. Warmth on a dread floor reads brighter than warmth on a warm floor.

Worked example, the sister puts a cup of tea down beside the protagonist without comment, three Reconstruction scenes after the album has been half-filled. No dialogue. No reaction shot. The cup is there. The next paragraph returns to the album.

Anti-example, narrating the dread: "The world felt heavy and oppressive, a weight of dread sitting on every surface as the protagonist searched for any sign of the shopkeeper." The dread has become a paint coat. There is nowhere left for warmth to land.

## Memory surfacings

In Reconstruction rallies, memory shows up as a half-second image. Present tense. The image does the work a flashback would do, faster, and leaves the rally intact.

### Pattern: present-tense surfacing in the rally

Worked example, mid-rally at the underwater venue:

> The ball returns, slower under water, the light bending around it. An elbow on the counter. Eyes on the rally. The next hit lands.

Five sentences. The middle two are the surfacing. Present tense ("an elbow", "eyes on the rally") puts the image in the room rather than reporting it from a past. The image fades on the next hit.

Anti-example, past-tense framing:

> The ball returned, slower under water. The protagonist remembered how the shopkeeper used to lean on the counter, watching the rally with quiet attention.

The memory has been narrated instead of surfaced. The rally has stopped to host the memory; the present-tense version keeps both alive at once.

### Pattern: specificity over warmth

Surfacings come from a specific moment with the friend at the counter. The reader builds the friend out of the particulars across many surfacings: a sleeve, a laugh, the way they handed back a racquet. Specificity is the engine; generalities like "the friend's warmth" or "the old days" reach for the meaning before the picture has earned it.

Worked example: "A sleeve, the cuff folded twice. A laugh through the window. The racquet handed back, grip first."

Anti-example: "A sense of the shopkeeper's warmth and presence, the comfort of the old days at the stall."

The specific version puts a picture in the reader. The general version reaches for the meaning the pictures should produce.

## Cracks at the line level

A crack is a visible, authored beat. The player notices. The discipline is not in hiding the crack; the discipline is in the crack earning its place and giving the player something to reach for when they try to explain it.

### Pattern: a meta-crack in dialogue

A meta-crack in dialogue is a line that almost-acknowledges the construct. The line stays in-fiction; the construct stays unnamed; the weight is on the page.

Worked examples:

> The coach: "We'll do this again next week, won't we."
>
> The friend at the stall, ending a goodbye: "See you tomorrow, same as always."

The weight sits on "won't we" and on "same as always". The player notices. The player reaches: the coach is being warm, the friend is being a friend, the line was meant kindly. The reach is the deniability.

Anti-example, the meta-crack made explicit:

> The coach said, "We'll do this again next week, won't we?", and there was something strange in his voice, as though he wasn't quite sure he believed it himself.

The narration has named the strangeness. The line no longer lets the player do the noticing.

### Pattern: a tonal crack in description

A tonal crack in description is a flicker named with weight, not buried. Two sentences, plain prose, no character noticing. The player notices. The player reaches: a lighting cue, a sound bug, an atmospheric flourish.

Worked examples:

> The light at the corner of the court goes blue for a beat, then back.
>
> Somewhere behind the stall, a kettle clicks off in a kitchen that is not there.

Plain prose. Two sentences each. No character pauses to react.

Anti-example: "The light at the corner of the court goes blue for a beat, an eerie reminder that all is not what it seems in this carefully constructed world." The narration has explained what it should let the player carry.

### Pattern: the cumulative load

One crack is a flourish; a dozen across an act is the world failing to reconcile. The protagonist's construct is failing to reconcile for them in the same shape and at the same pace. Player and protagonist drift out of alignment with Construction together. By the time the break arrives, the player has been collecting evidence the protagonist has been collecting too, and the recognition is shared rather than delivered.

Write the crack, then read it back and ask two questions. Does the player notice? Sharpen until the beat lands. Does the player have somewhere in-fiction to put it, somewhere short of "the game is telling me the world is fake"? Pull back until the in-fiction reach is available again. The target is a beat the player sees clearly and explains away.

## Pacing rhythms

A hit lands. The ball arcs back, slower, carrying the rally across the net before the next hit lands. Short sentences hit; longer sentences carry the rally between them. Voice's long-long-short pattern is the prose-level version of the same shape.

### Pattern: push when the moment pushes

The break wants short sentences in a stack. The cliff walk wants one breath, then the next. The recognition is a single sentence with no qualifier.

Worked example, the cliff walk:

> The path from the gate is narrow. The wind off the sea. The bench ahead.

Three sentences. No adjectives beyond "narrow". Each clause is one breath.

Worked example, the recognition at the cliff: "The number is the shopkeeper's." No qualifier, no explanation, no reaction shot. The next paragraph picks up the dial.

### Pattern: settle when the moment settles

A scene with the sister at the table runs in longer paragraphs that breathe. A coach's quiet line gets a paragraph break before and after. The reader's breath is part of the pacing budget.

Worked example, the credits rally:

> The court holds the late afternoon light Construction always had. The bench from the cliff is visible past the open gate, a long way off, small. The protagonist serves. The shopkeeper returns it. The chime lands clean, and the next ball is already in the air.

One longer paragraph. No short sentence to land on; the close is a continuation. The credits keep going.

The Construction warmth at credits runs longer paragraphs again, the prose unhurried, light returning. Pacing is not a global setting; it is a per-beat decision, and the beat decides.

## What the writing reaches for

### Pattern: endings land on a particular

A scene that closes on the loaded image lets the reader carry the meaning out the door with them; the scene trusts the reader to do that work, and the reader does.

Worked example, the close of a Reconstruction visit to the closed shop:

> The door is locked. A delivery slip is wedged into the frame, the date a week old. The protagonist walks back the way they came.

The slip with the date carries the scene's information without naming the worry. The reader carries it.

Anti-example, the same close summarised: "The protagonist felt a deep concern as they realised the shopkeeper had been gone for at least a week, and turned to leave with a heavy heart."

The summarised version names the feeling, names the conclusion, and closes on the heart. The particular version closes on the slip and trusts the reader.

### Pattern: themes live in what happens

The themes live in what happens. Volley is the rally, the friend at the stall, the cliff and the call. Whatever the story is about arrives through those particulars, and the prose lets it arrive without naming it. Characters and narration alike stay inside the world rather than describing it.

Worked example: the credits rally. The visual and the audio carry the synthesis the game has been working toward. No line of dialogue names the synthesis. The rally itself is the proof.

Anti-example: a credits voice-over that says "and so the protagonist learned that what mattered was being present with the people they loved." The theme has been spoken aloud. The rally does less work for it.

### Pattern: dialogue plays oblique to the theme

Dialogue plays oblique to the theme it carries. Characters talk about the weather, the toast, the score, the bus that arrived late. The themes ride underneath those conversations, surfacing through what is left in the room rather than through what is said. The reader hears both registers at once and feels the second one most because the first one carried it.

Worked example, the sister at the kitchen table on a bad day in Reconstruction:

> "The bus was late again."
>
> She turns a page.
>
> "He'd have walked."

Three lines about a bus and a brother who walked. The theme rides underneath and the reader feels it most because the surface stayed plain.
