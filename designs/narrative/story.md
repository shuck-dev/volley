# The story

The story Volley tells. Setting, cast, and structural canon are in [`outline.md`](outline.md). World rendering and tone are in [`../art/bible.md`](../art/bible.md). The protagonist's interior is in [`../characters/protagonist.md`](../characters/protagonist.md).

---

The protagonist is up before the sun is good. The walk to the garden is a walk they have been doing for years, and their feet do it without consulting the rest of them. The garden is at the end of a path that smells of rain and citrus. The court is at the far end of the garden. A racquet is leaning against the post where the protagonist left it the night before, and they pick it up by the grip that has worn to fit their hand.

The friend behind the counter looks up when the protagonist comes through. "Nine hundred and twelve," they say. The protagonist lifts the racquet in a small acknowledgement and walks on. "Try for a thousand today." The protagonist does not turn. Across the court, a coach is warming up against the wall.

The rally starts. The racquet finds the ball, sends it back, finds it again. The count climbs. From the stall, when the count rolls over the next round number, the friend says it like a small celebration. The protagonist nods once in the rally without missing a stroke.

At the back of the garden, behind the stall and behind the rally, there is a gate set into the wall. It is locked. The protagonist's eyes do not go to it. Their feet have a route through the garden that does not pass within twenty feet of it, and they do not know they have a route.

The world volley record sits on a board nobody quite shows the protagonist, and the protagonist knows the number anyway. It is in their phone. It is in the call log, fifteen rows down, with no contact name. Sometimes, after the rally, the protagonist takes the phone out and looks at the number. Their thumb hovers above it. The screen times out. The protagonist puts the phone away. They have been doing this for years.

---

Then small things start arriving wrong. A light at the corner of the court cools and does not warm again. A coach's tilt is held a beat too long. The score on the board shows the right number and the wrong colour. The friend behind the counter holds the look on the protagonist's name a moment longer than usual, and the protagonist almost asks what is the matter, and does not.

The bracket clears. The protagonist climbs every round. At the top of it is the champ, who has never lost and never showed up for a rally the protagonist could see, and the protagonist has been thinking about the champ the way a person thinks about someone they want to become. The match arrives.

The match is good. They play it well. Across the court, the champ moves the way the protagonist has wanted to move for a long time, and when the rally goes long, the champ holds the line. The protagonist beats the champ at four-eighty-seven to four-eighty-six.

The score lands. The protagonist waits for the inside of them to lift. It does not lift. They wait longer. The chime is bright. The crowd is bright. The friend at the counter is watching, and the friend has stopped saying numbers.

---

The light at the corner of the court goes blue and stays blue. The colour at the edge of the score is a colour the score does not have. The crowd thins without leaving. A coach across the court is looking at the protagonist the way a stage actor looks at someone who has wandered onto the stage by mistake.

The protagonist puts down the racquet. They are still in the garden when they take the first step, and they are not in the garden when they take the second. The path under their feet shifts. The air decides differently about being warm. They are walking, and the walking is the walking they have been not doing for years.

---

The protagonist is in their actual hometown. The streets are the streets they grew up on, and the air smells of rain and citrus, and the rain is colder and the citrus is fainter and the protagonist walks them like someone late to a funeral by a decade.

The shop is closed.

The slips wedged into the door are a week old. The protagonist stands on the step. After a minute the door opens; the sister is on the other side. She has been waiting for the protagonist for a long time without being able to say so. "Come in," she says. The protagonist comes in.

The kitchen is the kitchen the protagonist remembers, mostly. There is an album on the table, half-full. A kettle that has just clicked off. The sister puts a cup in front of the protagonist and does not pour. "He's not here," she says. "He hasn't been here for a week."

The protagonist sits down.

---

The protagonist starts going to the garden again, and the rallies go differently. The racquet finds the ball, and somewhere in the middle of a swing, the protagonist sees an elbow on a counter. The next swing, a sleeve folded twice at the cuff. The next, a racquet handed back to them grip first, by the friend who died, who never let the protagonist carry the bag home.

The memories become photographs and the photographs come out of the album and out of the world. Each one leads to a room in the hometown the protagonist has not entered in years. A back kitchen. A garage with the door rolled half-up. A bench by the harbour. Each room holds something the protagonist had stopped letting themselves remember.

The friend died on the cliff. The friend group had been jumping from the cliff for years; on the day the friend went, the shopkeeper was jumping with them. The protagonist was at home. They had been saying no to going for some months. The last thing the protagonist said to the friend was something on the phone that morning. The protagonist has tried to remember what it was. They cannot.

The shopkeeper came down from the cliff. The protagonist did not come for weeks. When they came, it was to the funeral; they stood at the back; they left without finding the shopkeeper, who was at the front and was looking for them.

The protagonist built a place to be in that did not run into any of it. The place is the garden.

---

The album fills, slow. Each photograph the protagonist puts in is a photograph they did not know was theirs. The compartment in the back of the album opens after, the way a thing opens when the right amount of weight has been put into the part you can see. The sister is at the table. She slides the key across the wood without looking up.

The protagonist walks back to the garden. The friend at the counter is at the counter still; the look the friend has been holding has shifted again, and the air the protagonist has been moving through is thinner. The rally is going on without them. The protagonist walks past the rally and past the stall, and for the first time in years, their feet take them along a path that ends at the gate.

They put the key in the lock. They turn it. The gate opens.

---

On the other side, the path is short. Grass and rosemary, a low wall, a bench against the wall. The bench has a dedication plaque on the back of it; the name on the plaque is the friend's. The shopkeeper is on the bench, alive, smaller than the protagonist remembers.

The shopkeeper is wearing a shirt the protagonist does not recognise. They are holding a phone. They are not looking at the path. The wind is from the sea.

The protagonist takes out their own phone. The number, fifteen rows down, with no contact name. Their thumb has hovered over it for years. They press it.

The phone in the shopkeeper's hand rings.

The shopkeeper looks at the screen. They have been letting calls ring for months. They could let this one ring. They could put the phone face down on the bench and let it be one more thing to be done with the air. The shopkeeper looks up.

The protagonist is on the path. Their phone is at their ear.

The shopkeeper looks at the protagonist for a long time, and they pick up.

The line opens. Neither of them speaks. The line stays open.

---

The credits play over the two of them on the same court. The rally between them is uneven; neither of them has rallied like this in a long time. The light the garden has had all this time has something else in it now, the texture the actual hometown has worn into it over months of going back and forth.

The cliff is behind them, returnable. The bench is there.

The gate stays open.
