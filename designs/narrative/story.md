# The story

The story Volley tells. Setting, cast, and structural canon are in [`outline.md`](outline.md). World rendering and tone are in [`../art/bible.md`](../art/bible.md). The protagonist's interior is in [`../characters/protagonist.md`](../characters/protagonist.md).

---

The protagonist is up before the sun. They walk through the garden by habit, past the wooden stall at the corner where the friend behind the counter is already in place, already easy. The court is at the far end of the garden, where the morning light hits first. The protagonist picks up the racquet they left leaning against the post the night before. Across the court, a coach is warming up against the wall.

The rally starts. The racquet finds the ball, sends it back, finds it again. The count climbs by one each time the rally crosses the net. From the stall, when the count rolls over a hundred, the friend behind the counter calls the protagonist's name. The friend says it the way you say the name of someone you have known for years, soft and offhand, almost dropped.

At the back of the garden, behind the stall and behind the rally, there is a gate set into the wall. It is locked. The protagonist does not look at it. They have not looked at it for a long time.

Coaches arrive over the months. Each one teaches another part of the rally, and the protagonist learns. Their racquet learns. The count climbs. The world volley record sits at the top of a board nobody quite shows them, and the protagonist knows the number anyway. It is in their phone. It is in the call log, in a row of digits with no contact name. The protagonist looks at it sometimes the way you look at something you are not ready to look at, and then they put the phone away and they go to the garden.

There are days when the friend behind the counter looks at the protagonist for a beat longer than the friend usually does. The protagonist does not notice. The rally is bright. The chime is bright. Something underneath the brightness is trying to get the protagonist's attention, and the protagonist is not giving it.

---

Then small things start arriving wrong.

A light at the corner of the court cools and does not warm. A coach's tilt is held a beat too long. A colour at the edge of a UI element is off, and the protagonist looks at it and looks away. The friend behind the counter holds the look on the protagonist's name, and this time the protagonist almost notices.

The championship arrives. The protagonist climbs every round to the final, and the final is the champ, and they have been waiting for this match for as long as they can remember waiting for anything. The match is good. The rally is the rally. The protagonist beats the champ.

They wait for what was supposed to happen next. They wait the way you wait for the inside of you to catch up to what you just did. It does not come.

The construct breaks.

---

The protagonist is in the actual hometown, walking. They have not been here in years. The streets are the streets they grew up on, and the air smells of rain and citrus the way it has always smelled, and the protagonist walks them like someone late to a funeral.

The shop is closed.

The slips wedged into the door are a week old. The protagonist stands on the step and looks at the slips and does not move. Inside, in another room of the house behind the shop, there is a sister who will eventually open the door and ask them in. On the kitchen table behind her is an album, half-full, with a compartment in the back of it that nobody can open yet.

The protagonist starts looking.

---

Each rally now surfaces a memory. The rally happens, the racquet finds the ball, and somewhere in the middle of a swing the protagonist sees an elbow on a counter, or a sleeve folded twice at the cuff, or the way a racquet was once handed back to them, grip first, by someone who never let them carry the bag home.

The memories become photographs. The photographs come out of the album and out of the world, and each one leads to a room in the hometown the protagonist has not entered in years. A back kitchen. A garage with the door rolled half-up. A bench by the harbour. Each room holds something the protagonist had stopped letting themselves remember.

A close friend died on the cliff a long time ago. Cliff jumping was a thing the friend group did, dangerous in the way the dangerous things you do in your twenties are dangerous, routine until it wasn't. The protagonist was not there that day. The shopkeeper was. The shopkeeper had been jumping with the friend, and the protagonist had been somewhere else, and the asymmetry of that day has sat between them ever since.

The protagonist did not know they had been living around it. They thought they were chasing the world record. They thought the rally was the thing they were doing because the rally was the thing.

---

The album fills. The protagonist puts the photographs in one by one, and on the last page a compartment in the back of the album opens.

The sister hands the protagonist the key.

She does not ask what it is for. She has been watching the album fill, watching the protagonist put each photograph in, and she knows what comes next without having to know.

The protagonist walks back to the garden. The stall is in its corner. The friend behind the counter is looking at them with the long look the friend has been holding for months, and the protagonist gives a look back that the friend has been waiting for. The court is going on without them. The protagonist walks past the court without stopping. They stand at the gate at the back of the garden, the gate they have not looked at for years.

For the first time, they put the key in the lock and turn it.

The gate opens.

---

On the other side, the path is short. It is just a path through low grass and rosemary. At the end of it, the cliff is just the cliff, the way places are just places when you finally arrive at them. There is a low wall set back from the edge, and a bench against the wall. The bench has a dedication plaque on the back of it.

The shopkeeper is on the bench, alive, smaller than the protagonist remembers.

They have been here for a while. The phone in their hand has been ringing for months, and they have been letting it ring. They have not been picking up calls. They have not been picking up because the people calling are people who would want to talk about how they are, and they are not in a place to be talked about.

The protagonist stops a few feet from the bench. They take out their own phone. The number they have looked at for years, the number with no contact name, is on the screen. They press it.

The phone in the shopkeeper's hand rings.

The shopkeeper has been letting calls ring like this for the whole search. They could let this one ring. They look at the phone in their hand. They look up.

They see the protagonist standing there.

They pick up.

---

The number the protagonist was chasing and the number on the bench are the same number.

The world volley record was the shopkeeper's phone number. Every rally the protagonist has had for years was a daily call to the friend they had pushed away and could not call. Every dial that went silent was the same dial. Every count that climbed was the protagonist getting closer to knowing what they had not let themselves know.

Now the call goes through.

The credits play over the two of them on the same court, the rally going on between them as the names roll past. The cliff is behind them, returnable. The bench is still there. The shop will open again.

The gate stays open.
