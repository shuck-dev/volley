# Funding and Grants: Volley!

Research into available grants, tax relief, and funding opportunities for a UK-based solo indie developer. Covers what exists, what is realistic, and when to apply relative to the development schedule.

---

## The Starting Position

- UK-based solo developer with a day job
- First commercial game
- Budget: ~£21K (see `costing.md`)
- Engine: Godot
- Target release: July 2027 on Steam (full release, not Early Access)
- No limited company currently set up (assumed; see below)

---

## Step Zero: Form a Limited Company

Nearly every meaningful funding source requires a UK limited company. The UK Games Fund requires PAYE registration. VGEC tax relief requires Corporation Tax. Arts Council England is the only major exception (individuals can apply).

Setting up a Ltd is £12 at Companies House, same-day online. Registering for PAYE is free. This is the single highest-return action for unlocking funding, and there is no reason to delay it.

Once the company exists, pay yourself a small salary via PAYE (even nominal). This satisfies the UK Games Fund eligibility requirement.

## Tax Relief: VGEC (Video Games Expenditure Credit)

This is the most reliable financial benefit available. It is not a grant; it is a tax credit. If you qualify, you will get money back.

### How It Works

VGEC replaced the old Video Games Tax Relief on 1 January 2024. You claim a credit at **34% of qualifying expenditure**. This appears as income in your accounts and is subject to 25% Corporation Tax, resulting in a **net benefit of 25.5%** of qualifying spend [1][2].

On a £21K qualifying spend, that is approximately **£5,355 back**.

### What Qualifies

- Your own salary as a director doing development work
- Freelancer and contractor fees (art, sound, writing)
- Software tools used directly for production
- Animation, audio, and art costs
- Must be "core costs": designing, producing, and testing the game

Does **not** include: initial concept development, debugging, post-completion maintenance, marketing [1].

### Requirements

- Must be a **UK limited company** paying Corporation Tax [1]
- Must pass the **BFI Cultural Test**: score at least 16 out of 31 points on content, cultural contribution, and development criteria [3]
- At least 10% of core costs must be UK expenditure [1]
- No minimum spend threshold; even small projects can qualify [2]

### Cultural Test Fit

The BFI cultural test awards points for British setting/characters, English language, British cultural contribution, use of UK facilities, and UK-based developers. A narrative-rich game by a UK solo developer, written in English, with British cultural references should score comfortably [3].

### When to Apply

Apply to the BFI for cultural certification once the game is in production (prototype or later). The certification can take several weeks. You then claim VGEC through your annual Corporation Tax return. This means the actual money comes back after your financial year-end, but it is predictable and near-guaranteed if you pass the cultural test [1].

### Specialist Advice

VGEC has specific rules around payments to "connected parties" (i.e. paying yourself as a sole director). Get specialist accountancy advice. Firms that specialise in this: Myriad Associates, VGEC Specialists, Friend Partnership [2][4].

## UK Games Fund

The most directly relevant grant source. Run by UK Games Talent and Finance CIC, funded by DCMS. The UK government announced a **£30 million Games Growth Package** across 2026/27 to 2028/29 (£10M/year), which will significantly expand what the fund can offer [5][6].

### Prototype Fund

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| Amount | Up to **£30,000** (monthly claims typically capped at £6,000) |
| Stage | Early-stage: the grant funds building a playable prototype |
| Eligibility | Registered UK SME with PAYE registration and at least one employee |
| Requires | 2-minute pitch video (no talking heads, no trailers), written application, interview if shortlisted |
| Revenue share | None; this is a grant, not an investment |
| Timeline | Expressions of Interest open periodically (most recent: June 2025). Full applications late June/July, interviews September, funded work starts October |
| Current status | Funded until March 2026; expansion under the £30M Games Growth Package expected from April 2026 [5][6] |

**What panels look for**: Team experience in professional game development; business acumen (at least one person who can speak commercially); a clear plan for what happens after the grant; inclusivity and diversity; potential for the studio to grow beyond one project [5].

### Content Fund

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| Amount | **£50,000 to £150,000** |
| Stage | Post-prototype, pre-release. **A working prototype must already exist.** |
| Eligibility | Registered UK gaming company. Must own the IP. Total project costs must be at least double the grant requested. |
| Requires | Working prototype, commercial viability evidence, clear path to market |
| Revenue share | None |
| Timeline | Rolling applications; portal closes for 10-14 day review periods between batches |
| Current status | Active, funded until March 2026; expected to expand [6][7] |

**Key note**: A £50K Content Fund grant requires at least £100K in total project costs. For a solo developer with a £21K budget, this would only become relevant if the project grows significantly in scope or if the Prototype Fund grant expands the budget [7].

### Fit for Volley!

The Prototype Fund is the natural fit. Apply when the next round opens (likely mid-2026 under the expanded fund). By that point, the Prototype phase will be underway with a playable build to demonstrate in the pitch video. The £30K grant would more than cover the £21K budget.

## Arts Council England

### National Lottery Project Grants

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| Amount | **£1,000 to £100,000** |
| Stage | Any stage; flexible |
| Eligibility | Individuals or organisations based in England. **No limited company required.** |
| Decision time | 6-12 weeks depending on amount |
| Current status | Always open [8] |

Games are not explicitly listed as an eligible discipline, but they are not excluded either. The fund covers "arts, libraries and museums projects." If the project is framed as interactive narrative art or digital art with emotional depth, it could qualify. The grief narrative, dual-world structure, and hand-drawn art direction all strengthen a case for cultural and artistic merit [8].

This is the only major UK fund where an individual can apply without a limited company. Worth trying even if the outcome is uncertain.

## Regional Bodies

These are only relevant if you are based in the corresponding region.

### Creative Scotland

Open Fund and Multi-Year Funding available. Scotland has acknowledged a funding gap in video game development and is working to address it under the "Level Up Scotland" plan targeting £1B sector turnover by 2030 [9].

### Creative Wales

Partners with the UK Games Fund for a Wales-specific stream covering staff and contractor costs for development activity in Wales [10].

### Northern Ireland Screen

Has an "Interactive" funding strand specifically for video games, immersive, and web projects. Offers prototype funding and skills development [11].

### Accessible from the UK

| Fund | Amount | Relevance | Notes |
|------|--------|-----------|-------|
| **Indie Fund** | Varies per project | Medium | Investment model, not a grant: revenue share until recouped + multiplier, then it ends. Highly selective (2-3 games/year). They tend to find you through the indie scene rather than cold applications. Worth being on their radar but not a reliable plan [12]. |
| **Xsolla Funding Accelerator** | Up to $100,000 | Low-Medium | Bridge funding plus mentorship. Typically wants games with commercial traction potential. Investment model [13]. |

### Not Accessible

| Fund | Why |
|------|-----|
| **Epic MegaGrants** | Requires Unreal Engine or UEFN. Godot projects are not eligible [15]. |
| **Creative Europe MEDIA** | UK excluded post-Brexit. The government created the UK Global Screen Fund (via BFI) as a partial replacement [16]. |
| **Nordic Game Program** | Restricted to developers in Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and Sweden [17]. |
| **Godot-specific grants** | Do not exist. The Godot Foundation funds engine development, not games made with Godot [18]. |

## What Grant Panels Want to See

Based on UK Games Fund guidance and general grant application advice [5][6][7]:

1. **Commercial viability**: Not just "I want to make this game" but "here is the audience, here is the market, here is the path to revenue." The marketing strategy doc (`marketing-strategy.md`) is this.
2. **Sustainability**: Panels want to fund studios that will continue, not one-off projects. Frame Volley! as the first release of a studio with a longer-term plan.
3. **Business plan over game design doc**: A budget breakdown, revenue projections, and market positioning matter more to panels than mechanics documentation.
4. **A playable demo**: Not always required (Prototype Fund does not need one), but having something people can play dramatically strengthens any application.
5. **Inclusivity and diversity**: Explicitly mentioned by the UK Games Fund as a factor.
6. **UK economic impact**: Job creation, skills development, growing the UK games sector. Even as a solo developer, paying UK contractors (artists, musicians) counts.

## Practical Notes

### Can you apply to multiple grants simultaneously?

Yes, with caveats:
- UK subsidy control: total grants must not exceed £315,000 over three years [7]
- Declare all other funding in every application
- Grants cannot fund the same costs twice (no double-dipping)
- VGEC is separate from grants, but VGEC only applies to costs you actually bore (grant-funded costs may not qualify for VGEC) [1]

### How long do applications take?

- UK Games Fund Prototype Fund: EOI to funding decision is roughly 3-4 months [5]
- UK Games Fund Content Fund: rolling, batched review periods of 10-14 days [7]
- Arts Council England: 6-12 weeks depending on amount [8]
- VGEC cultural certification: several weeks via BFI [3]

### Documentation typically required

- Company registration details and PAYE reference
- Project description and game design document
- Business plan with commercial strategy and post-grant sustainability
- Budget breakdown
- Team CVs showing relevant experience
- 2-minute pitch video (UK Games Fund specifically)
- Prototype or demo (for Content Fund; not required for Prototype Fund)
- Evidence of IP ownership

## The Big Question: £100K and Going Full-Time

If the realistic-to-optimistic funding scenario plays out (Prototype Fund + VGEC + potentially Arts Council or Content Fund), the total budget could reach £75K-£100K+. At that point the question shifts from "can I fund this?" to "should I quit my job?"

### How Long Does £100K Last?

Running through a Ltd company with a solo developer paying themselves a modest salary:

| Category | Monthly | Annual |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Salary (at personal allowance) | £1,050 | £12,570 |
| Dividends top-up | £1,000-1,500 | £12,000-18,000 |
| Employer's NI | ~£185 | ~£2,220 |
| Corporation Tax reserve | ~£350 | ~£4,200 |
| Accountant | ~£125 | £1,500 |
| Contractors (art, sound, music) | £1,500-2,500 | £18,000-30,000 |
| Software, tools, hardware | ~£130 | ~£1,560 |
| Insurance, workspace, misc | ~£180 | ~£2,160 |

**Conservative (minimal contractors)**: ~£4,700/month. £100K lasts ~21 months.
**Moderate (regular contractor work)**: ~£6,500/month. £100K lasts ~15 months.
**Aggressive (significant outsourcing)**: ~£8,000/month. £100K lasts ~12 months.

For a 13-month remaining timeline, the moderate scenario is tight but feasible. The conservative scenario gives a comfortable buffer.

### Pros of Going Full-Time

**Development speed.** Full-time means 35-40 productive hours/week versus perhaps 15-20 hours evenings and weekends. That is a 2-2.5x multiplier on raw output, plus the elimination of context-switching costs between a day job and a creative project. A 13-month part-time timeline is really 6-7 months of equivalent full-time work.

**Polish and quality.** More time means more iteration: playtesting, polish, accessibility, localisation, controller support, Steam Deck verification. These are the things that get cut when time is short but that meaningfully affect reviews and sales.

**Marketing and events.** Attending WASD, EGX, game dev meetups, and publisher pitch events during business hours. Maintaining a consistent social media and devlog presence. Responding to community feedback on a reasonable schedule.

**Grant compliance.** UKGF grants do not explicitly mandate full-time commitment, but milestone schedules are calibrated around full-time development. Being full-time removes the risk of falling behind because the day job had a crunch period.

### Cons of Going Full-Time

**Financial risk if the game underperforms.** The median indie game on Steam earns under $5,000 lifetime. At $7.99 with Steam's 30% cut, you net ~$5.59 per sale. To recoup £100K (~$125K) from sales alone, you would need approximately 22,000 copies. That is in the optimistic scenario for a first game, not the base case [20][21].

**Finite runway creates pressure.** Every month that slips is a month of salary burning. This creates tension between "ship it polished" and "ship it before the money runs out." Scope creep becomes genuinely dangerous rather than just inconvenient.

**Loss of steady income.** UK employment benefits disappear: employer pension contributions, sick pay, holiday pay, potential share schemes. The psychological safety net of a steady paycheck is significant, and its absence can create anxiety that undermines the creative benefits of going full-time.

**The first game tax.** Most experienced indie developers (Jake Birkett, Jeff Vogel, Rami Ismail) emphasise that the first game is a learning experience, not a financial return [22]. Mike Bithell made Thomas Was Alone while working full-time as a web developer in the UK; the game's success allowed him to go full-time for subsequent projects, not the other way around.

**Isolation.** Solo full-time development from home is isolating. Multiple post-mortems cite this as a significant factor in burnout and depression. The day job, whatever its flaws, provides social structure and routine.

**What happens after?** If the game ships on time and sells poorly (statistically the most likely outcome for a first game), you face returning to employment with depleted savings and a CV gap. In software engineering this gap is generally forgivable, but the stress of job hunting under financial pressure is real.

### The Hybrid Option: Part-Time

Negotiate reduced hours at the day job (3-4 days/week). Use the freed time for game development. The grant money covers contractors, tools, and marketing rather than your salary.

**Why this works well:**
- Financial safety net remains intact
- Grant money goes further (not paying yourself from it)
- If the game shows traction (wishlists, demo feedback), you can quit with more confidence later
- If it does not, you still have a career and income
- Lower psychological pressure means better creative decisions

**Why it might not work:**
- Slower development; the 13-month timeline may need to extend
- Context-switching still exists, just less severely
- The employer may say no
- Some grant milestones may be difficult to hit at reduced pace

### The Pragmatic Sequence

Many experienced devs recommend this approach for a first game:

1. Go part-time at the day job (3-4 days/week)
2. Use freed time plus grant money to reach a playable demo
3. Publish the demo on Steam, measure wishlists and feedback
4. If traction is strong (5,000+ wishlists, positive reception), consider going full-time for the final push to launch
5. If traction is weak, finish the game part-time, learn from the experience, keep financial security

This is the lower-risk path. It defers the biggest decision (quitting entirely) until there is real data rather than projections.

### Grant Reporting and Obligations

UKGF grants typically require quarterly milestone reports and financial documentation (receipts, invoices, salary records). The reporting is manageable (1-2 days per report) but adds overhead [5][7].

If milestones slip, grant bodies generally work with you if you communicate early. They would rather you deliver late than not at all. However, clawback provisions do exist contractually: if you fundamentally fail to deliver, some or all of the grant money may need to be returned. This is rare in practice but it is a real obligation [7].

UKGF grants do not take IP or equity. The IP remains yours. This is a significant advantage over publisher deals.

### The Honest Assessment

Going full-time on £100K of grant money for a first game is feasible but high-risk. The money is enough for 13-15 months of modest living and moderate contractor spend. The risk is not the development period; it is what comes after if the game underperforms.

The hybrid path (part-time day job + grant-funded development) is the approach most commonly recommended by experienced indie developers for a first commercial release. It preserves optionality: if the game takes off, you can quit with confidence. If it does not, you have learned everything you need for game two without financial damage.

The worst-case scenario to avoid: quitting the job, spending the grant money, shipping the game, earning $2,000 in the first year, and needing to find a new job under financial pressure. The hybrid path makes this scenario impossible.
