# Volley! Art Style Workshop

A working document for establishing the visual language of Volley! This is not a brief; it is a reference analysis and style exploration that will inform the brief.

---

## The Core Question

Volley! is a game about someone who built a world to hide from grief. The art must support two worlds:

1. **The Constructed World**: warm, curated, safe. A small pong arena that someone made to feel okay.
2. **The Real World**: ordinary, lived-in, grounded. A place that feels like someone actually grew up there.

These are not two art styles. They share line work, shapes, and proportions, but they differ in how much comfort they offer. The constructed world is a simplification of the real one: cleaner, softer edges, more saturated colour, more deliberate composition. The real world has more texture, more weight, more life that was not arranged for anyone's comfort. The shift between them should feel like waking up, not like changing the channel.

## References

Grouped by what they teach, not by medium. Each entry names the key people responsible for the visual work and what specifically to study.

### Warmth as Construction

**Porco Rosso** (1992)
Director: Hayao Miyazaki. Art Director: Kazuo Oga. Animation Director: Toshio Kawaguchi.

Oga's watercolour backgrounds feel like late afternoon in a place you used to go: warm without being saccharine, nostalgic without being sentimental. Miyazaki's film is also literally about a man hiding behind a different form to avoid dealing with his past. The warmth is real, but it is also a shield.

Study: Oga's quality of light. The feeling of warmth that is both genuine and protecting you from something.

**Bo Burnham: Inside** (2021)
Creator: Bo Burnham (writer, director, cinematographer, editor, performer, composer).

One person, one room, elaborate creative lighting to make a confined space feel like an entire world. Burnham built something obsessively detailed inside a box because the alternative was confronting what was outside it.

Study: making a small space feel like everything. The constructed world lives in a small desktop window, and it should feel like someone has arranged every part of it with care. Also: the emotional arc of someone building something elaborate to cope.

**La La Land** (2016)
Director: Damien Chazelle. Cinematographer: Linus Sandgren. Production Designer: David Wasco.

The fantasy sequences feel different from reality not because the style changes but because the feeling changes. Sandgren shot on CinemaScope film stock; Wasco built a hyper-romanticised Los Angeles. The musical numbers are vivid, saturated, choreographed. The quiet scenes between them are muted, still, natural. Same world, but the colours cool, the light flattens, and the framing loosens. You feel the shift before you notice it.

Study: how saturation, lighting, and composition shift together to change how a scene feels without changing the underlying style.

### Ordinary as Beautiful

**Good Will Hunting** (1997)
Director: Gus Van Sant. Cinematographer: Jean-Yves Escoffier.

Escoffier gave working-class Boston a warm, muted quality with soft natural lighting. The beauty comes from paying attention: the way light comes through a window, the texture of a particular bar, the feeling of a place that has been lived in for a long time. The camera stays close and quiet.

Study: finding beauty in honest, unglamorous settings. Not pretty, but specific.

**Odd Taxi** (2021)
Director: Baku Kinoshita. Character Designer: Kazuya Konomoto. Studios: OLM / P.I.C.S.

Deliberately muted, low-key visuals. Characters who feel like real people living ordinary lives, hiding enormous things beneath the surface. The visual restraint is a conscious choice to let the writing carry. Simple, flat character designs mask a complex mystery.

Study: visual restraint as narrative strategy. Art that looks simple and warm on the surface but rewards attention.

### Expression from Simplicity

**Ranking of Kings / Ousama Ranking** (2021)
Director: Yousuke Hatta. Character Designer: Atsuko Nozaki. Studio: WIT Studio.

Childlike, storybook character designs with enormous emotional range. Bojji is a simple shape, but his body language communicates everything. The gap between how simple the characters look and how deeply the show makes you feel is the entire point.

Study: the production model. Simple designs, expressive through animation, not through detail. This approach solves the production efficiency problem while giving enormous emotional range.

**Keep Your Hands Off Eizouken!** (2020)
Director: Masaaki Yuasa. Character Designer: Naoyuki Asano. Studio: Science SARU.

Loose, sketchy, energetic character designs that prioritise movement over model consistency. Science SARU's house style emphasises expressive, fluid, sometimes abstract animation. The joy is in the movement, not the rendering.

Study: the animation philosophy. Fewer frames, better chosen. Each animation state should feel like a different emotion, not a different sprite.

**Star Fetchers** (Svavelstickan)
Developer/Artist: LK (Svavelstickan).

Kinetic, anime-influenced animation with fluid character movement. Personality comes from how characters move, not from how much detail they have.

Study: fluid, characterful animation achieved by focusing effort on movement rather than rendering detail.

**Cuphead** (Studio MDHR, 2017)
Directors: Chad and Jared Moldenhauer. Lead Animator: Jake Clark.

Hand-drawn frames, bold shapes, confident colour, enormous personality from animation. Every enemy, every background, every UI element feels like it was drawn by the same hand. The commitment to a specific visual identity is total: nothing breaks the fiction, including the UI. A small-ish team producing characterful hand-drawn art at full game scale.

Study: production commitment to a unified hand-drawn identity. Every element belonging to the same world. Diegetic UI that feels like part of the fiction. Not the retro pastiche framing, but the discipline of carrying a single visual voice through every asset in the game.

### Sports as Feeling

The game riffs off sports anime and sports competition. These references show how a single moment of contact can carry emotional weight through animation and visual style.

**Haikyuu!!** (2014-2020)
Director: Susumu Mitsunaka. Studio: Production I.G.

Volleyball impacts, reaction shots, the emotional weight of a single point. Time stretches at the moment of contact. The build-up, the hit, the team reaction: a rally becomes a story. Probably the most directly relevant reference for how a volley should feel.

Study: how anticipation, contact, and follow-through turn a simple action into an emotional beat. The reaction shots that give a point meaning beyond the score.

**Ping Pong: The Animation** (2014)
Director: Masaaki Yuasa. Studio: TATSUNOKO.

Yuasa again. Abstracted, expressive sports animation where the style bends to match the emotion of the moment. A rally is not just a rally; it is a conversation between two people expressed through movement. The rendering shifts during intense exchanges: lines loosen, perspectives warp, the ball becomes a feeling rather than an object.

Study: how the visual style itself responds to emotional intensity. Abstraction at peak moments. The proof that a pong-like exchange between two paddles can carry real weight.

### Style Shifts Without Breaking Coherence

**Omori** (2020)
Developer: OMOCAT.

The most direct structural parallel. Headspace is a bright, pastel, hand-drawn safe world the protagonist built to avoid confronting trauma. Reality is muted, grainier, heavier. The two worlds share character designs but differ in colour, rendering, and how much they are willing to show. The shift between them is not a surprise twist; it is a slow reveal that recontextualises everything the player has already seen.

Study: the closest existing example of what Volley! is doing with its two worlds. How the constructed world's warmth and the real world's weight coexist in the same game. How character designs carry across both without needing to be redrawn. How the shift lands because the player has spent time in the safe version first.

**To Be Hero X** (2025)
Director: Li Haoling. Studios: BeDream / PB Animation / Studio LAN / Paper Plane.

Different visual styles and media within a single work, used as emotional punctuation. When the world shifts, you feel it before you understand it.

Study: shifting palette, line weight, and rendering approach within a single work without losing coherence, as long as the emotional core holds.

**Everything Everywhere All at Once** (2022)
Directors: Daniel Kwan, Daniel Scheinert (the Daniels). Cinematographer: Larkin Seiple. Production Designer: Jason Kisvarday.

Coherence across wildly different visual modes because the emotional core is consistent. Seiple used varied lighting and colour grading to distinguish each universe. Kisvarday built mundane-to-fantastical environments on a limited budget.

Study: different visual worlds can coexist as long as they feel like they belong to the same person. The emotional throughline is what holds it together.

### Deep Worlds from Considered Details

**Rain World** (Videocult)
Artist/Designer/Programmer: Joar Jakobsson.

One person created the procedural animation system, creature designs, and painterly environmental art by hand. Every element feels interconnected. The environment tells its own story without dialogue.

Study: environmental storytelling through care. Nothing generic, everything placed by someone who was thinking about something. A single artist building a world that feels vast through specificity.

**Oddworld: Abe's Oddysee / Exodus** (1997/1998)
Director: Lorne Lanning. Production Designers: Farzad Varahramyan, Steven Olds.

A world that rewards looking closely. Varahramyan and Lanning co-created the "Oddworld Signature": six artistic commandments that governed every piece of art produced. Over 1,000 unique screens with visual consistency, because the team worked from principles rather than just taste.

Their six commandments state that all images must:

1. **Be realistic**: ground the fantastical in believable visual principles
2. **Have solidity**: possess weight and structural integrity
3. **Be original**: avoid derivative or recycled concepts
4. **Be cool**: maintain visual appeal and style
5. **Evoke emotion**: connect players to the atmosphere; generate feeling
6. **Look alive**: convey vitality and living presence

Study: the power of governing principles. Commandments give an artist a framework for making decisions independently. A style guide shows what things look like; commandments define what things must feel like.

### Diegetic Design and Grounded Worlds

**Black Hole Fishing** (SDG Games, 2026)
Developer: SDG Games (solo). Publisher: Playsaurus.

The in-game shop is called "Fishy Business Magazine" and is designed to look and feel like an actual fishing catalogue. Players buy fish by browsing a magazine, not a menu. The entire purchasing interaction is framed as a physical object within the world.

Study: diegetic UI at its most committed. The shop is not a screen; it is a thing. Every player-facing interaction framed as something that exists within the fiction.

**Cast n Chill** (Wombat Brawler, 2025)
Developer: Wombat Brawler. Lead Background Artist: Ashley Rose Smith.

Multiple layered backgrounds creating significant depth. The background artists came from landscape painting, which gives the environments a painterly quality: trees reflecting in water, birds taking flight, light shifting across layers. "The longer you look, the better it looks."

Study: backgrounds as environments, not backdrops. Foreground, middle, and back, with layers that move and breathe. This grounds the player in the world rather than reminding them they are looking at a screen.

### Readable at Small Scale

**Enter the Gungeon** (Dodge Roll Games)
Artist: Joe Harty.

Chunky, readable sprite work that makes bullet-hell chaos legible. Every enemy, gun, and room communicates at a glance. Personality packed into small frames through silhouette and colour, not detail.

Study: readability at small scale. If you squint and it is still clear, it works.

**Saltsea Chronicles** (Die Gute Fabrik)
Art Director: Nils Deneken.

Risograph-print-inspired, storybook illustration style. Solid colours doing enormous work. No complex rendering; palette and composition carry everything.

Study: solid colour as a tool, not a limitation. Confident colour choices read as intentional, not under-produced.

**Poco** (Whalefall)
2D/3D hybrid with hand-painted backgrounds and cartoony 3D animation. Vibrant, varied colour palette applied to cluttered environments filled with forgotten objects.

Study: vibrant does not mean noisy. A wide palette that is still controlled, where each environment has its own colour identity within the broader range.

### Rhythm

**Baby Driver** (2017)
Director: Edgar Wright. Cinematographer: Bill Pope. Production Designer: Marcus Rowland.

Every cut, movement, and colour choice serves the rhythm. Pope's kinetic camerawork tracks musical choreography. Rowland designed stylised, colour-coded settings.

Study: rhythm as visual principle. A game loop has a rhythm. Reactions that feel like beats, breaks that feel like rests. Visual rhythm is felt before it is noticed.

## Primary References

Five works that between them define the territory Volley! lives in. The other references sharpen specific qualities; these five set the direction.

**Ranking of Kings** is the character and animation north star. Simple designs with enormous emotional range, expressive through body language rather than detail. This is how characters should be built: clean enough to read at any size, animated well enough to carry feeling without dialogue. If the art had to look like one thing, it would look closest to this.

**Porco Rosso** is the north star for the constructed world's feeling. Oga's light, Miyazaki's nostalgia, the sense of a place someone loved rendered from memory. The warmth of the constructed world should feel like Porco Rosso's Adriatic: genuine, beautiful, and quietly protecting someone from something.

**Omori** is the north star for the dual-world structure. Headspace and reality coexisting in the same game, the same characters carrying across both, the shift recontextualising everything. The closest existing example of what Volley! is doing. How a constructed safe world and a grounded real world can share character designs and visual DNA while feeling fundamentally different through colour, rendering, and how much they are willing to show.

**Saltsea Chronicles** is the north star for colour and rendering approach. Solid colours, confident and intentional, carrying emotion without complex rendering. This is the proof that restraint reads as a choice, not a limitation. The colour confidence that makes simple shapes feel considered rather than unfinished.

**Odd Taxi** is the north star for narrative-visual strategy. Simple surface, enormous depth underneath. Art that looks approachable and warm at first glance, and only reveals what it is carrying when you pay attention. The visual restraint is not a constraint; it is the point.

## The Volley! Commandments

Five principles that govern every piece of art in the game. These are not style rules; they are qualities. Any asset, any screen, either world. Inspired by Oddworld's approach of defining non-negotiable qualities and trusting artists to find their way there.

### Intentional

Every mark, colour, and placement is a choice. Nothing is filler, nothing is accidental, nothing is "just a background." If it is in the frame, someone decided it should be there.

Burnham's obsessively lit room. Oga's watercolour afternoons. Rain World's hand-placed ecosystems. The feeling that every element was considered.

### Specific

This object, this space, this person. Never generic. Every element should feel like it belongs to this world and no other.

Good Will Hunting's sense of place. Odd Taxi's ordinary lives. Oddworld's commandments demanding originality. The feeling that you could not swap any element out without it being wrong.

### Distinct

Communicates through shape and colour. Unmistakable at any size. If two things could be confused at a glance, one of them needs to change.

Enter the Gungeon's readability. Saltsea's confident solid colours. Ranking of Kings' simple silhouettes carrying enormous emotion. The feeling that everything announces itself clearly.

### Diegetic

Part of the world, not on top of it. Every interaction, every surface, every piece of information the player sees should feel like it exists within the fiction.

Black Hole Fishing's catalogue-as-shop. Cast n Chill's layered environments that feel like places rather than screens. The feeling that you are inside something, not looking at something.

### Breathing

Feels alive. Movement carries personality. Still frames should feel like they are about to move.

Eizouken's animation philosophy. Star Fetchers' fluid characters. Ranking of Kings' emotional body language. Poco's vibrant clutter. The feeling that the world is not waiting for the player; it is already going.

## What This Means for the Style

**Hand-drawn illustration with bold shapes, limited but confident colour, and expression through animation rather than detail.**

Not pixel art. Not painterly realism. Not vector-clean. A style that puts effort into animation and colour rather than rendering complexity.

### Resolving the Tensions

The references pull in two directions that are not conflicts; they are the two ends of the spectrum between worlds.

**Vibrancy vs. restraint.** Porco Rosso and Poco are vivid and saturated. Odd Taxi and Saltsea are muted and restrained. Both are right, because the game has two worlds. The constructed world leans toward vibrancy: saturated colour, soft lighting, deliberate composition. The real world leans toward restraint: quieter palette, natural light, less arrangement. The underlying style is the same; the saturation, edge quality, and composition shift between them.

**Clean vs. loose.** Ranking of Kings has clean, consistent character designs. Eizouken prioritises sketchy energy over model consistency. The answer is both: clean enough to be distinct at small scale, loose enough to breathe. Lines should have life in them, not be mechanically precise, but silhouettes must hold at any size. Ranking of Kings is the anchor; Eizouken is the permission to let things move.

**Simple characters vs. rich worlds.** Saltsea and Ranking of Kings point toward simplicity. Cast n Chill and Rain World point toward layered, detailed environments. If characters are too simple and backgrounds too rich, characters float on top of the world instead of living in it. But Ranking of Kings and Porco Rosso both solve this: simple characters against rich, painted backgrounds, connected through shared light, colour, and grounding. Characters belong to a space when the light hits them the same way it hits everything else. The resolution is not uniform complexity; it is that characters are simple, environments have depth, and they share the same light and palette so they feel like the same place. This also maps to the registers: the constructed world's environments are more composed and curated (closer to Saltsea), while the real world's have more texture and life (closer to Cast n Chill). Characters stay at the same level of simplicity across both; the world around them shifts.

### The Two Registers

The constructed world uses this style with saturated colour, clean edges, soft lighting, and considered composition: every element placed with care. The real world shares the same shapes and proportions but loosens the lines, desaturates the palette, lets the light fall where it falls, and leaves things where they actually are rather than where someone arranged them.

The Break is the moment the player sees both worlds and understands that the first was always a simplification of the second.
