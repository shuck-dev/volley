# Artist World Bible: Re-architecture Outline

Working doc for the bible's restructure. Captures the architecture Josh and I worked out 2026-04-25 after the diagnosis that the bible was texture without thesis and conflated the bright-world protagonist with the reality protagonist.

## The architecture in one paragraph

Volley is a game with three registers: Construction, Reality, and Reconstruction. Construction is the bright world, an over-the-top Nintendo aesthetic the protagonist actively maintains to chase a constructed goal: beat the world volley record. Cracks form during construction; they are visible but easy to dismiss, more obvious as the game continues. The break is the moment the maintenance fails. Reality is what is there when the construction stops: a completely different render, real characters at their real ages, less vibrant, less full, less fun in a deliberate way. Reality is story-driven (dialogue, descriptive memory beats), with volleyball as an occasional mini-game rather than the driver. Reconstruction is the synthesis: the construction returns, but matured, less fake, less Nintendo, more Nausicaä. Same fundamental rendering choice as construction, more weight.

Everyone in the game is real. The bright-world cast is the real-world cast rendered young, vibrant, helping. Two different asset sets for the characters who appear in both registers.

The bible's job is to make this clear to the artist so every painting, every gesture, every venue choice fits the register it belongs to.

## Section list

Fourteen sections. One outline minion per section in the next dispatch wave. After outlines are reviewed and revised, content fills in a second wave.

### A. What the game is

The hook. The protagonist's stated goal in the bright world: beat the world volley record. Friends help along the way. The count climbs. This is the engine of construction.

The hook is presented in-game (probably via dialogue). The artist needs to know it because every bright-world rendering serves it.

### B. Construction

The bright world. Nintendo aesthetic. Over-the-top. Vibrant, young, full. The protagonist is happy-go-lucky; the friends are bright and helping; the rally is the game. The garden as the first venue, improvised. The light is warm, the count climbs, the racquet is in the protagonist's hand because they are pursuing the goal.

What construction LOOKS like: saturation, youth, motion, generosity of light, fullness of detail, characters present and active.

What construction IS: the pretense the protagonist actively maintains. It takes energy to keep.

### C. Cracks during construction

Cracks form as the maintenance strains. They are visible but easy to dismiss; the player can keep playing and not notice. They get more obvious as the game continues.

The artist's job here is the hardest in the bible: paint cracks the viewer can dismiss but cannot deny. Examples might include a flicker in a character's brightness, a line of dialogue that does not quite fit, a moment of light that comes from the wrong direction, a friend's smile that holds a beat too long or not long enough.

The cracks rhyme with reality. They are reality leaking through the construction's seams.

### D. The break

The rupture. Both a cumulative collapse (the cracks pile up) and a single big moment (the construction fails at one point). The artist's job is to render the moment when the construction's maintenance can no longer be sustained.

The break is the bridge to reality. Visually it is where the asset sets switch. Tonally it is where the player gives up the energy of construction.

### E. Reality

The completely different register. Real characters at their real ages. Less vibrant, less saturated, less full. Boring in the deliberate sense: not adventure, not climbing, not chasing. The everyday underneath.

Reality is story-driven: dialogue, descriptive memory beats. The player spends time with the people the protagonist actually knew, in the rooms they actually shared. Volleyball can appear as a mini-game (a flashback rally, a moment of play with a real friend) but is not the driver.

Visually a complete second asset set. Same characters, rendered as they actually are.

### F. Reconstruction

The matured construction. The protagonist returns to the bright world after reality, and the bright world is different. Less Nintendo, more Nausicaä. Same fundamental rendering choice as construction (it is still a constructed register) but with weight, with knowledge, with what reality showed.

This may be more thematic than visual: the construction does not look entirely different, it looks less fake. The brightness has matured.

The trajectory is from Nintendo to Nausicaä. The register's saturation drops a notch; the line work gains weight; the world feels lived in rather than performed.

### G. What the artist holds

The three registers as craft direction. How each is rendered. What the artist needs to know to paint a moment that belongs to construction vs cracks vs reality vs reconstruction.

Two asset sets per character (construction-form and reality-form). One register-shift between cracks and break. One tonal maturation between reality and reconstruction.

The seam between registers is itself a craft challenge.

### H. Constants across venues

The racquet. The ball. The friend. The stall. The spirit of the volley (collapsed friendship + spirit; physically present in the bright world; gives the ball its arc, lifts the protagonist).

These travel with the game across every venue. They are construction-side constants. Reality has its own constants (the kitchen, the photograph, the back walking down a street) that do not need to mirror the construction's.

### I. The cast (everyone is real)

The protagonist. The friend at the stall. The tinkerer. Martha and the partners. Each character has a real-world identity that the construction renders as the bright-world version.

Brief sketches of each. Pointer to the MC profile (PR #427) for the protagonist's depth. The shop and tinkerer characters need the same MC-profile treatment eventually; for now this section names them as real-anchored and gives the artist the bright-world version.

The lineage rule (every partner named for a character from a science-fiction author's work) stays.

### J. Diegetic surfaces

The shop as Argos catalogue. The workshop. The racks (ball feeder, equipment rack). The plaque, the sign, the counter that reads zero. These are the world's surfaces that carry information without HUD.

This section is craft-direction for diegetic UI. It applies primarily to construction (where most of the rendering happens); reality has its own diegetic surfaces (kitchen objects, photographs, paper, real shops).

### K. Venues

First venue: the improvised garden. No built court; a stretch of grass walled in by stone and vines. The prototype delivers this venue and one or two cracks.

The leap to the second venue (likely underwater, V2 tentative). The trajectory from there: meteor in nebula light, tropical island, jungle, library, ice cave, all sketches.

Per SH-275 (define prototype venue scope), the bible can sketch the trajectory but the binding minimum is the first venue plus a hint of the next.

### L. The Six Marks

The rendering rules: Intentional, Specific, Distinct, Diegetic, Breathing, Shifting. Each applies across all three registers, but their expression varies by register.

This section stays largely as-is from the previous bible iteration; the architecture above is what it serves.

### M. References and named hands

The reference register: films and animation, games, named hands. Linked to canonical sources (Steam, Wikipedia, etc) per the existing section. Stays as-is.

Reconstruction's Nausicaä framing means Studio Ghibli references should be doubled-down on here, especially the matured-Nausicaä-aesthetic touchstones (Mononoke, Spirited Away rendering after the bathhouse).

### N. Tone rules and what to avoid

The do-nots. The voice rules in summary form. Stays largely as-is.

## Process

The essay-style swarm-write workflow Josh wants:

1. (You are here.) Outline the architecture as a section list with one-paragraph notes per section. Doc lives at this scratchpad.
2. Dispatch one minion per section to OUTLINE that section in more depth (what beats, what canon, what the artist needs).
3. Outlines come back to scratchpads under `ai/scratchpads/bible-section-N-outline.md`.
4. Josh reviews the outlines.
5. Dispatch a fresh minion per section to FILL the content based on the approved outline.
6. Section content lands in the bible (or in a fresh draft) in coherent waves.

Each minion preloads `ai/skills/voice.md` (six rules: cadence, sentence shapes, show-don't-tell, place-the-nouns, integrate-the-sentences, literary-devices-sparingly) and reads the surrounding bible context. Cross-register canon (the three registers, the hook, everyone is real, Nintendo→Nausicaä) is in this outline doc.
