# Item Design

A few things we look for in a good item. Treat these as guidelines, not gates. They describe what has tended to work for us, and a great idea that bends one is still worth bringing.

- Change the game, not the numbers. The items we love best add a new behaviour, rule, or bit of causality rather than a usage multiplier.
- Make it implicitly understandable. Aim for a mechanic that reads in play without a tooltip, and a reward you feel without having to track it.
- Make it fun in the doing. Let the interaction itself be the appeal, more than the figure it outputs.
- Start from a real object with a twist. Grounding the mechanic in fiction tends to help: goop from under the floorboards, a helmet that does not fit.
- Build across levels, then subvert. Each upgrade can play on the one before, and a top level is often strongest when it turns a learned instinct against the player rather than simply amplifying it.
- Match the reward to the difficulty. A harder item earns a payoff worth the effort; ideally it is not also the easier way to play.
- Design the mechanic, not the feeling. Reach for the actual rules, not the player experience you hope they will produce.
