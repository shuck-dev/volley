# Inspirations

A running list of visual, tonal, and design inspirations for Volley!

---

## Animation and Film

**Porco Rosso** (1992, Hayao Miyazaki / Kazuo Oga)
Watercolour backgrounds, warm nostalgic light, the feeling of a place remembered fondly. A man hiding behind a different form to avoid dealing with his past. The warmth is real but it is also a shield.

**Ranking of Kings / Ousama Ranking** (2021, WIT Studio)
Childlike storybook character designs with enormous emotional range. Simple shapes, expressive through body language rather than detail. The gap between how simple the characters look and how deeply they make you feel.

**Ping Pong: The Animation** (2014, Masaaki Yuasa / TATSUNOKO)
Abstracted, expressive sports animation where the style bends to match the emotion. A rally is a conversation between two people expressed through movement. The rendering shifts during intense exchanges: lines loosen, perspectives warp, the ball becomes a feeling.

**Haikyuu!!** (2014-2020, Production I.G.)
The emotional weight of a single point. Anticipation, contact, follow-through. Time stretches at the moment of impact. A rally becomes a story.

**Keep Your Hands Off Eizouken!** (2020, Masaaki Yuasa / Science SARU)
Loose, sketchy, energetic character designs that prioritise movement over model consistency. Fewer frames, better chosen. Each animation state feels like a different emotion, not a different sprite.

**Star Fetchers** (Svavelstickan)
Kinetic, anime-influenced animation with fluid character movement. Personality comes from how characters move, not from how much detail they have.

**Bo Burnham: Inside** (2021)
One person, one room, elaborate creative lighting to make a confined space feel like an entire world. The emotional arc of someone building something elaborate to cope.

**La La Land** (2016, Damien Chazelle / Linus Sandgren)
Saturation and lighting shifts without changing the underlying style. The fantasy sequences feel different from reality because the feeling changes, not the rules. Colour as emotion.

**Good Will Hunting** (1997, Gus Van Sant / Jean-Yves Escoffier)
Finding beauty in honest, unglamorous settings. Warm, muted quality with soft natural lighting. The beauty comes from paying attention.

**Odd Taxi** (2021, Baku Kinoshita / OLM / P.I.C.S.)
Deliberately muted, low-key visuals. Characters who feel like real people living ordinary lives, hiding enormous things beneath the surface. Visual restraint as narrative strategy.

**Everything Everywhere All at Once** (2022, the Daniels / Larkin Seiple)
Coherence across wildly different visual modes because the emotional core is consistent. Different visual worlds can coexist as long as they feel like they belong to the same person.

**To Be Hero X** (2025, Li Haoling)
Different visual styles and media within a single work, used as emotional punctuation. When the world shifts, you feel it before you understand it.

**Baby Driver** (2017, Edgar Wright / Bill Pope)
Rhythm as visual principle. Every cut, movement, and colour choice serves the rhythm. A game loop has a rhythm too: reactions that feel like beats, breaks that feel like rests.

## Games

**Omori** (2020, OMOCAT)
The most direct structural parallel. A bright, hand-drawn safe world built to avoid confronting trauma, and a muted, heavier reality underneath. Same characters carrying across both worlds. The shift recontextualises everything.

**Cuphead** (2017, Studio MDHR)
Hand-drawn frames, bold shapes, confident colour. Total commitment to a specific visual identity: nothing breaks the fiction, including the UI. Every element belongs to the same world.

**Rain World** (Videocult / Joar Jakobsson)
A single artist building a world that feels vast through specificity. Painterly environmental art, procedural animation, interconnected ecosystems. Nothing generic, everything placed by someone who was thinking about something.

**Oddworld: Abe's Oddysee / Exodus** (1997/1998, Lorne Lanning / Farzad Varahramyan)
A world that rewards looking closely. Over 1,000 unique screens with visual consistency, built from governing principles rather than just taste. The "Oddworld Signature": six artistic commandments that governed every piece of art produced.

**Black Hole Fishing** (2026, SDG Games)
Diegetic UI at its most committed. The in-game shop is a physical fishing magazine, not a menu. Every player-facing interaction framed as something that exists within the fiction.

**Cast n Chill** (2025, Wombat Brawler)
Multiple layered backgrounds creating depth. Background artists from landscape painting, giving environments a painterly quality. "The longer you look, the better it looks."

**Enter the Gungeon** (Dodge Roll Games / Joe Harty)
Chunky, readable sprite work. Personality packed into small frames through silhouette and colour, not detail. Readability at small scale.

**Poco** (Whalefall)
Vibrant, varied colour palette applied to cluttered environments. Vibrant does not mean noisy: a wide palette that is still controlled, where each environment has its own colour identity.

**Rusty's Retirement** (2024)
The closest format comparison. Small persistent window, plays while you work. Warm, earthy pixel palette, stays out of the way. Volley! layers considered art direction and narrative on top of the same "play while you work" format.

**Neko Atsume**
Simple, minimal art with a check-in-occasionally play pattern and hidden emotional warmth.
