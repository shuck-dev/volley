# Volley! World Bible: Artist Background

A companion to `artist-brief.md`. The brief is the deal. This is the world. Read the brief first; come back here for the texture.

The bible is dense on purpose. Pick the bits that resonate and let the rest be ambient context. Every section earns its weight; nothing here is a homework list.

---

## The shape of the game, in one breath

Volley! is an idle pong game played in a window on someone's screen. A partner on the left, a partner on the right, a ball in the air between them, a counter ticking up. That is the surface. (Full-window play is the default; a smaller desktop-companion mode exists as a bonus.)

Most Steam idles default to pixel art; the mobile cosy lineage (Cats and Soup, Tsuki, Neko Atsume) defaults to soft pastel illustration. Hand-drawn illustration as the visual centre of an idle game is unclaimed territory, and that is where Volley sits.

Underneath sits a quieter story. The protagonist is androgynous, agender, mid-30s, less athletic than they used to be. They lost someone close, and built a place to keep playing while they figured out how to live with it. The bright world the player meets first is that place. There is also a quieter world underneath it: a small town the protagonist grew up in, where the loss happened. Both are visited. The bright world holds the rally and the friend's voice. The quieter world holds the ordinary, familiar, difficult moments the protagonist keeps coming back to.

The throughline can be hinted visually before it ever speaks. A vision through a gate. A photo glimpsed across a table. A reflection that holds for a beat. The art's job is to let those moments land.

Players who never go looking for the story still enjoy the volley, the items, the partners, the climbing record. Players who pay attention notice the names, the descriptions, the way certain lines and objects sit a beat longer than a pong game has earned. Eventually the seam shows. The cosy surface holds. It was real all along.

---

## How a session feels

To anchor everything else: the player opens Volley! and a rally is already happening, or about to. They serve, they hit, the counter climbs. Items sit on a rack at the side of the court. A friend behind a stall calls out occasionally. The player picks up new items, drops things off at a workshop, brings new partners into the rally, and chases a higher count. The rally has tiers it climbs through, each one slightly hotter than the last. A miss ends the rally; the ball rolls out of the court and waits to be picked up again. The game keeps going whether the player is watching or not.

That is the loop. Everything below dresses it.

## The court (and what Volley actually looks like)

A correction to anything else you might have read first: Volley is **Pong-shape**, not table-tennis. The rally happens on a court bounded on three sides:

- **Top edge.** The screen edge. A hard ceiling; the ball bounces.
- **Bottom edge.** The ground. Pong-style floor; the ball bounces. Hitting the ground does not end the rally.
- **Back wall.** Behind the protagonist. The miss line. Crossing past it ends the rally.
- **The far side.** Where the partner stands. Before the first partner arrives, a wall stands in for them and returns the ball. After the partner arrives, the partner returns it.

The court visibly opens onto the rest of the venue. A missed ball does not despawn. It keeps its velocity, rolls out of the court, loses energy on the venue floor, and comes to rest wherever it stops. Resting balls render in the mid or foreground over the shop and workshop, so they stay visible.

The constant the player carries is the partner's tool: a paddle, the protagonist's. Equipment items attach to it. The artist will shape it, and the shape will travel.

## The protagonist

The person whose game this is. Androgynous, agender, mid-30s, less athletic than they used to be, the kind of person who picked up a paddle long ago and never quite put it down. Gentle with themselves, and we should be too. Not a tragic figure, not melodramatic, not a person in collapse. Someone who built a place where they could keep playing.

In the bright world they are off-camera as a body most of the time. We catch them in glimpses: a hand on the paddle, a reflection in a window, a teacup left on a wall. The world is built around the protagonist's quiet presence at its centre.

In the quieter world they are on screen. The brief asks for a real-world memory beat with them, observed fly-on-the-wall: a small moment with a partner in an ordinary place. That is where they get a body. Mid-30s, a little softer than they once were, gentle in posture. The artist's first quieter-world deliverable will set the rest of their physical specifics.

They lost someone close to them. Recently enough that it is not healed, long enough that they have built a structure around it. The bright world is that structure. They do not hide on purpose; they hide because the structure was the thing they could build that held.

## The friend

The most important supporting character in the bright world. The shopkeeper at the heart of the venue. The voice the player hears most. Their stall sits against one wall of the garden, and their presence is what makes the venue feel like a place rather than a stage.

The friend is one of several people the protagonist holds close. They are the warm centre of the venue. Whether they are the lost one or someone else who matters is left ambiguous; the artist's read is welcome here, and the answer can stay open.

What matters: the friend reads as one particular person the protagonist loves, not a generic shopkeeper. Visual register: warm, grounded, named rather than typed. Gender, age, and presentation are all open to the artist's read. The friend stays behind the stall most of the time, head tilted toward the play, attentive without intruding.

The friend, the stall, and the warmth travel with the game across every venue. When the bright world leaps to underwater or a meteor or a treetop canopy, the stall lashes to whatever holds it and the friend is still there. They are the constant.

In the quieter world they are quieter still. A photograph. A figure across a kitchen table. A back walking down a street. The bright-world version is a bigger, brighter rendering of someone the protagonist remembers very specifically.

## Martha

A partner. She joins the right side of the court once the player has built up enough friendship between them, and from then on she is the one returning the ball from her side.

**Who she is.** A Black woman who was the cashier at the local newsagent in the protagonist's hometown: a small shop on the high street, the kind of shop you pop into most days. She always had a smile waiting. They would chat about nothing: the weather, something in the paper, a thing she saw on telly. Maybe they hung out once or twice outside the shop, but it never became a regular thing. She was a fixture, not a friend exactly, but someone who made the day a little warmer.

**Why she comes early.** She is one of the lowest-risk memories. No history to untangle, no falling out, no guilt. Just someone who was reliably kind. Reaching for her does not yet require facing anything heavier. The partners that come later carry more.

**Voice register.** Warm, slightly sharp, particular, British. The kind of person who would tell you the truth and then make you a cup of tea. She speaks rarely. Her silences are characterisation: she does not cheer on individual hits, she does not commentate. She is not a coach, she is a friend. The idle chatter is the centre of her voice. Small talk about nothing, the way she would have at the newsagent.

**Lineage.** Every partner in Volley is named for a character from a science fiction author's work, with a bias toward authors writing outside the dominant tradition (women, writers of colour, queer writers, writers from outside the Anglo-American centre). Martha shares her name with the protagonist of Octavia Butler's "The Book of Martha" (2003; collected 2005). Butler's Martha is a Black woman writer summoned by God to change humanity, with one rule: people must still find some happiness. The link to Volley's Martha is quieter than narrative; Butler is the patron saint of the character. The lineage informs how each partner carries themselves; it does not need to be visible on the surface of the game.

## Friendship and the spirit of the volley

Two threads of warmth carry across every partner.

**Friendship** is what builds between the protagonist and each partner over time. It deepens with shared rallies and small attentions. The artist will see it land as warmth in the venue, in the way a partner stands a little easier next to the protagonist as friendship grows.

**The spirit of the volley** is the protagonist's own fighting spirit, made into a metaphor. It is internal, not a guest at the table. A long rally is the spirit at full breath. A miss is the spirit catching its breath again. The ball runs hotter when the spirit is up; the venue light leans warmer; the rally feels a little brighter than the count alone explains. None of this needs spelling out for the player. The art carries it.

---

## The tinkerer

The other named character in the venue. She runs the workshop. The player drops items off; she rests, procrastinates, and works at her own pace; finished items land on a done tray with a soft chime.

The tinkerer is the friend's younger sister. Different rhythm, different relationship to the protagonist, same family warmth. She talks less than the friend. She hands things back when they are done. The rhythm of the workshop is part of her personality: she makes tea, stares out the window, reads, tidies the bench, settles into the work in her own time. She unlocks later than the friend.

The tinkerer's age, presentation, and look are open. Her relationship to the friend is what anchors her: family, not a separate thread.

## The cast of partners

Four partners join the protagonist from fairly early in the game. After that, each new venue brings one more.

Each partner is a person the protagonist remembers. Each has a personality, a particular voice, a quieter-world counterpart. Beyond Martha, none are drafted yet.

The lineage rule names candidates: Le Guin, Jemisin, Tiptree / Sheldon, Becky Chambers, Margaret Atwood, Naomi Mitchison, Joanna Russ, Liu Cixin, Nnedi Okorafor. The artist designing Martha sets the bar for the rest.

A note on what partners feel like to play with: having a partner is a gift, and the art carries that. Their presence steadies the rally. Their misses sit at speeds the protagonist is also struggling with. The character truth underneath: partners are people the protagonist is glad to have around. The game is built so the player feels the same.

## The first venue

A garden. The setting is a UK and Mediterranean blend, warm climate; the architecture reads part European cottage, part Mediterranean patio, part a memory the protagonist is reaching for that does not exactly belong anywhere. Stone or terracotta tiles underfoot. A wall or wooden fence around the edge. A tree at the back, an olive or fig or apple, branches already old. Late afternoon light, warm and slant. The garden feels lived-in. Paint chips, vines on the wall, a watering can on its side near the tree.

The friend's stall sits against one inside wall. A small wooden affair with a stripe of awning and one shelf of items. The stall is not part of the architecture; it is a thing the friend put there. It can fold up and travel.

A ball feeder sits on one side of the court, holding the balls that are not currently in play. A smaller rack opposite holds equipment items. Both have visible age and use; they have been here a while.

A locked gate sits at the back beside the tree. The player can see it but not reach it. Past it, just barely visible, is something the player cannot quite resolve: a streak of weirdness, a glimpse of palm fronds, a strip of sky that does not belong here. The garden is closed but not sealed, and the strangeness is meant to flicker rather than linger.

## The trajectory of the bright world

The first venue is small and contained. The game then leaps. Each leap is a wholly different place, and each leap is bigger than the last. Same partner. Same friend's voice. Same warmth. A wildly different world around them.

The first leap, in the prototype, is implied as a cliffhanger rather than visited. The player triggers it and the world fades on the brink of something else. The leading candidate for the second venue is **underwater**: sun above the surface, light filtered green and gold through deep water, the ball drifting through bubble streams, the friend's voice now muffled and warm. Cool, alien, dreamlike. Studio Ghibli's *Ponyo* or the sunken world in *Spirited Away* as register references. Underwater is not formally locked yet, and what comes after is fully open.

Beyond underwater, the leaps are sketches at this stage; throwing ideas down is part of the work. A tropical island with palm shadows on white sand. A meteor in nebula light. A cathedral of trees high in a canopy, golden hour through leaves. A goo dimension where everything jiggles. A jungle full of dinosaurs. A massive stadium with a crowd. A city on clouds. An ice cave with breath visible in the air. A library so vast the shelves disappear into haze.

The mechanic underneath the leaps is the protagonist reaching further and further for a better hiding place. The bigger the wonder, the more elaborate the construction. The break, when it comes, lands on top of all of it.

## The constants

Things that travel with the camera across every bright-world venue.

- **The protagonist's paddle.** Same shape, same colour, same handle, wherever the rally goes. Equipment hangs off it.
- **The opposing returner.** A wall in the smallest first venue, then a partner from the moment the first partner arrives.
- **The ball.** The same ball at the centre. Item skins change its art; the size and presence are the same.
- **The friend's stall.** Lashed differently per venue (against a fence, around a palm, against a meteor edge), but always recognisable. A small awning, one shelf, the friend somewhere behind it.
- **The friend's voice.** Audible across every venue. Muffled underwater, distant on the meteor, but always present.
- **The volley counter.** The same number, dressed for each venue. A wooden plaque in the garden, a coral-rimmed slate underwater, a hovering glyph in space. Diegetic everywhere.
- **The warm light register.** Colour temperature shifts venue by venue (afternoon yellow, submerged green, nebula pink, canopy gold). The warmth holds.

## The quieter world

What the player sees occasionally, and what the protagonist is reaching for. Setting: a town somewhere between southern UK and Mediterranean coast. Warm but not Caribbean warm. Stone houses, small streets, a square with one cafe, a bus stop nobody uses. The kind of place a child grows up in and an adult comes back to.

The visual register here can be anything that rhymes with the bright world: same DNA, different mood. Memory rather than fantasy. Less saturation, perhaps. More texture. The light is just light; the air has weather. People walk by; cars pass; a dog barks somewhere. The town has a pulse the bright world does not.

Quieter-world moments are short and named. A kitchen table at twilight. A walk down a particular street. A bench by a pond. A photograph on a fridge door. They are felt rather than narrated. The protagonist is in them; the friend is there; sometimes Martha or the tinkerer or another partner. Each moment is the protagonist remembering one thing in particular.

The artist's deliverable for the quieter world is a small moment between the protagonist and a partner in an ordinary place. The choice of moment shapes the cast that follows.

How the two worlds relate visually is open. The principle holds: the shift between them is not a style change. It is a change in how much comfort the art offers.

The cast across both worlds is aged down for that youthful feel: 20s and 30s. The protagonist sits at the older end of that band.

## The phases

Four phases. The visual register shifts across them. The prototype lives in phase one.

1. **Pre-break.** Warm, cosy, bright world. The protagonist is in the place they built. Everything has light. The friend is at the stall, the partners arrive, the wonders escalate. Underneath, small moments of oddness or unexpected interest can sit; the player is not lulled, just held. This is the prototype's whole register and the artist's first deliverable.
2. **The break.** A monumental change. The bright world deconstructs. The seam does not just show; it opens. Light, sound, object readings all turn at once. The break is not subtle; it is the hinge of the game.
3. **Post-break.** Reconstruction and recontextualisation. The bright world and the quieter world both come back, both altered, both readable in a new light. The same venues hold different weight. The friend is still there, quieter; Martha's lines acquire weight; the protagonist is still playing, but knowing more.
4. **Peace.** Genuinely peaceful, and still fun and challenging. The warmth that returns knows what it costs. The visual register is the warmth of pre-break with the texture of post-break. The protagonist has integrated the loss without losing the world.

From the north star non-goals: the game is sad, but it earns the sadness and does not wallow in it. The reveal lands and the game moves forward.

## How items feel (form, not specifics)

The roster will change before ship. The form will not. Three things hold across every item the artist will touch.

**Items are physical objects with a small twist.** A whistle that plays a flat note. A roll of grip tape that leaves marks on everything. A locker mirror whose reflections do not stop. Mundane object plus one detail that hints at the effect. The artist's job per item is the object plus the detail, made specific.

**Items have lived in someone's life before the player got them.** Worn elastic, frayed velcro, ends stuck to themselves, paint chipped, edges creased. Nothing is new. Each item carries the patina of having been used.

**Items belong somewhere on the court.** Some are equipment the protagonist clips, ties, or fixes onto the paddle, the way a player tapes a grip or fits a new string to a racket. Some replace the ball itself: a stitched leather one, a glass one, a meteor shard. Some take up a corner of the court as a piece of furniture: a music cabinet against a wall, a training wall set up across the court, a small dock for the bot. The form an item carries gives the artist its home in the world.

A handful of hero illustrations is more useful than a full roster, because the roster is going to move.

## The shop as a travel magazine

A corner of the venue, hidden until the friend unlocks. The shop reads as a small kiosk: a table with items resting on it, a thumbed-through travel magazine the friend brings out across the table, a small counter where boxes briefly sit before they go on their way.

The travel magazine is the charming object at the centre of it. The artist's read on it sets the worldbuilding: 70s travel posters, *National Geographic* spreads, Studio Ghibli "found art", Outer Wilds field-guide pages. Each new venue could read like a travel ad inside its pages. Even with placeholder photography, the aesthetic sells the wider world cheaply. Items reference places that do not exist in the prototype yet, which is one of the load-bearing implication cues.

## The prototype's job: imply the rest

The prototype lives in the first garden venue and ends on the cliffhanger leap. Underwater is not playable in the prototype. The implication does the heavy lifting:

- **Fence edge.** What you glimpse past the fence is not a neighbour's roof. A streak of weirdness, palm fronds, a purple cloud bank, a distant something-massive. One painted backdrop layer that reads "this place borders other places."
- **Items have provenance.** Each item name and description references a place that does not exist in the prototype. "Ankle Weights, picked up on the dock at the tropical island."
- **The friend's stall stocks oddities.** Items the friend brought back from elsewhere: a starfish, a meteor shard, a vial of glow.
- **Diegetic plaque flavour.** "Best volley: 47, set after the storm." Memory in place of HUD.
- **Single offstage character.** The friend mentions someone in passing: "they're off in the cloud city this week." Player meets them later.
- **Sound layer.** Mostly afternoon birds and distant traffic. Occasional outliers: a whale call, an unrecognisable wind, a low rumble.
- **A locked gate.** Visible but unreachable. The world has elsewheres.

Cost is mostly text plus one painted backdrop layer plus a handful of audio clips. The prototype delivers a cosy garden the player can fall into; the cues make the player wonder what is past the fence without rendering it.

## Light register

A constant-warmth principle: every venue, every phase, has some warmth somewhere. Colour temperature changes; intensity changes; mood changes. The warmth holds.

Examples:
- Pre-break garden: late-afternoon yellow, slant shadows, slight haze.
- Underwater: sun above filtered green and gold, with motes of light cutting through.
- Meteor or space: nebula pink, cold star points, the friend's stall has its own glow.
- Treetops: golden hour through leaves; the canopy filtering everything to honey.
- Ice cave: cold blue ground, but a single internal glow in the crystal walls or a lantern on the table.
- Post-break garden: the same garden, but the afternoon yellow has thinned. Light is grey-warm rather than gold-warm.

## Sound and atmosphere

The world has a sound, and knowing the audio register helps the visual one.

Pre-break garden: afternoon birds, distant traffic, the friend's voice drifting in. Occasionally a wind that carries something further: a wave, a wind-chime, a sound that does not quite belong here.

Underwater: muffled, slow, with the warmth of bubbles and the friend's voice as if heard through water. The paddle hits sound differently.

Meteor: very quiet. Sparse. The friend's voice carries because there is nothing else.

Treetops: layered birdcall, leaves moving in wind, distant thunder maybe, golden silence between rallies.

Across all of them: the paddle hit, the volley counter ticking, the ball's quiet whoosh. These are the constants the player anchors on.

## The Six Marks

The art bible's rulebook. Every piece of art carries all six. They are descriptions of what Volley looks like when the work is landing, not commandments.

1. **Intentional.** Every choice carries a reason. Nothing arbitrary, nothing default.
2. **Specific.** Particulars over types. This fence, this stall, this Martha. Generic warmth fails.
3. **Distinct.** The visual register is unmistakably Volley. Not rounded-cosy, not pixel, not painterly-realism.
4. **Diegetic.** The world is something the player is inside, not looking at. UI sits inside the same hand and voice.
5. **Breathing.** Movement, posture, body language carry the personality. Animation breathes; nothing is locked. A simple shape that moves with feeling carries more than a detailed shape that sits still.
6. **Shifting.** The same world holds multiple registers (bright / quieter, pre-break / post-break, biome to biome). Silhouettes hold; light, colour, line quality shift.

## Reference register

Every work below has been cited somewhere in the design corpus as a tonal or visual touchstone. Pick the two or three that resonate most with where you want to push the work; let the rest be ambient context.

### Animation and film

- **Porco Rosso** (1992, Studio Ghibli). The bright world's north star for light and warmth. Late-afternoon watercolour register. Warmth that is genuine and also a shield.
- **Good Will Hunting** (1997). Quieter-world reference for finding beauty in honest, unglamorous places. Bare-bulb light, soft natural tones, a place that has been lived in.
- **La La Land** (2016). How saturation and lighting shift to change a scene's feeling without changing its style. The fantasy sequences move on the same axis Volley moves between its two registers.
- **Baby Driver** (2017). Rhythm as a visual principle. Cuts, movement, and colour serving a felt pulse. Volley has a pulse the art and sound both serve.
- **Ping Pong: The Animation** (2014, dir. Masaaki Yuasa). Sport as conversation. The style itself bends at peak emotion.
- **Haikyuu!!** (2014-2020). The canonical text on volleyball as emotional language. Anticipation, contact, follow-through. Reaction shots that give a single point meaning.
- **Keep Your Hands Off Eizouken!** (2020, dir. Masaaki Yuasa). Fewer frames, better chosen. Movement-first, model-consistency-second.
- **Ranking of Kings** (2021). Storybook simplicity carrying enormous emotional range. The Bojji lesson: a silhouette can carry more feeling than a model sheet.
- **Odd Taxi** (2021). Visual restraint as narrative strategy. Flat, deliberately muted designs hiding enormous things.
- **Bo Burnham: Inside** (2021). One person, one room, obsessive light arrangement. How a small constructed space can feel like everything.
- **Everything Everywhere All at Once** (2022). Wildly different visual modes held by a single emotional core. Permission for register shifts to feel coherent.
- **To Be Hero X** (2025). Multiple visual styles within one work, used as emotional punctuation.
- **Detectorists**. British pastoral cosy. Quiet warmth, gentle people, English summer afternoons. Tonal anchor for the quieter world.
- **Coco**. Warm Mediterranean register, family memory, magic that does not undercut the heart of the story.
- **Wallace and Gromit / Beatrix Potter**. British illustration register. Warm, specific, lived-in.
- **Adventure Time**. Visual variety held together by tonal warmth across wildly different settings.

### Games

- **Pong** (1972). The seed object. The whole rally lineage starts here.
- **Oddworld: Abe's Oddysee / Exodus** (1997 / 1998). The Oddworld Signature: six artistic commandments that governed every screen. Structural inspiration for Volley's Six Marks. Painterly-atmosphere reference at sustainable scale.
- **Machinarium** (2009, Amanita Design). Painterly atmosphere at sustainable scale. Simpler character silhouettes against textured backgrounds.
- **The Stanley Parable** (2013). A game that lets its own scaffolding show without breaking the player's trust.
- **Neko Atsume** (2014). Mobile cosy lineage; Volley sits adjacent to this, not inside it.
- **Enter the Gungeon** (2016). Readability at small scale. Silhouette-led sprites that work when scaled down.
- **Cuphead** (2017). Production discipline. Total commitment to one hand-drawn voice across every asset, including UI.
- **Rain World** (2017). Environmental storytelling through care. Hand-placed ecosystems, painterly decay. One artist building a world that feels vast through specificity.
- **Star Fetchers** (2020). Fluid, anime-influenced character animation. Personality through motion rather than rendering.
- **Omori** (2020). The closest existing structural parallel for Volley's two-world move. Headspace and Faraway Town: same cast, different weight, shared character DNA.
- **Slay the Princess** (2023). Hand-drawn art that responds to narrative. Pencil-on-paper line that shifts as the player's belief shifts.
- **In Stars and Time** (2023). Hand-drawn storybook cast in a time-loop frame. Cosy-warmth-with-weight peer.
- **Rusty's Retirement** (2024). Format peer for the desktop-companion mode. Validated the desktop-resident idle category.
- **Cast n Chill** (2025). Backgrounds as environments rather than backdrops. Painterly layered depth.
- **Poco** (2025). Vibrant-but-controlled palette across cluttered hand-painted worlds.
- **Wanderstop** (2025). Cosy-to-honest register peer. Mechanics that stay pleasant all the way through while carrying real weight.
- **Despelote** (2025). Sport as a frame for emotional material. A kick against a wall as enough.
- **Black Hole Fishing** (2026). Diegetic UI at its most committed. The shape Volley's travel-magazine shop is reaching for.
- **Outer Wilds**. Tiny planets, music drifting across space, the field-guide register the travel magazine reaches for.
- **Mario Galaxy**. Biome variety as cosmic-cosy. Each tiny world with its own character.
- **Wii Sports Resort**. Sport-as-place. Each activity a distinct visual setting.
- **Broken Age**. Hand-drawn painterly with dual settings shared by one story.

### The calibration

Volley aims for the painterly atmosphere of Broken Age, Poco, Machinarium, Oddworld, and Cast n Chill, at the labour scale of Ranking of Kings and Eizouken: simple character shapes that breathe through animation rather than rendering, painterly backgrounds carrying the world's weight, characters readable in silhouette before line. The painterly register is the feel; the simple-shape discipline is how we afford it.

Said as a single line: characters as simple as Ranking of Kings, coloured as confidently as Poco, lit as warmly as Porco Rosso, committed as completely as Cuphead, carrying across two worlds like Omori, with the restraint of Odd Taxi underneath.

## Anti-references

What Volley is consciously not.

- **Pixel art.** Hand-drawn illustration, not pixel.
- **Photoreal.** Hand-drawn warmth, not realism.
- **Vector polish.** Lines have life in them. No mechanical precision.
- **Painterly realism.** The hand-drawn mark stays visible.
- **Generic cosy register.** Volley's cosy is particular, not generic.
- **Anime-stylised faces with big eyes.** The characters are people drawn warmly, not avatars.
- **Period pastiche.** No fixed real-world era reference.
- **Scary or distressing imagery.** The quieter register is quieter, not darker.
- **Outline-and-flat-fill shortcuts.** Line and fill work together; shadows are painted, not rendered.

---

## Tone rules

Three principles the game holds across every register.

1. **The warmth is real.** The cosy surface is not a lie. The narrative reveal does not punish the player for enjoying the rally. Warmth is earned and stays.
2. **Earnest, never ironic.** Cosy, never stressful. Personal, never melodramatic. Characters are warm and particular. Items have flavour. Nothing is a wink.
3. **Trust the player.** No exposition. No signposting. Let the player notice, wonder, and eventually understand.

A line that holds it: a cosy idle game with more going on than it lets on. The art's job is to carry both halves of that line at once.

## Studio identity: Shuck

The studio Volley lives in. The artist is working with Shuck, not just on Volley, and the studio has a posture worth knowing about up front.

**Shuck is a sanctuary for craft.** The industry context is grim: 18,900 games shipped on Steam in 2024 (one every 28 minutes), 80% of releases unplayed, rolling layoff cycles. Shuck's response is open development as long-cadence practice: a public devlog, public design docs, an MIT-licensed source repository, an audience built one post at a time. Peers Shuck draws on: Team Cherry (Hollow Knight), Eric Barone (Stardew), Bay 12 (Dwarf Fortress), Lucas Pope (Papers Please / Obra Dinn), Double Fine (PsychOdyssey), Toby Fox (Undertale / Deltarune).

**Warmth as craft, not as marketing.** The Six Marks above are framed as descriptions, not commandments. An artist works against the Marks rather than under them.

**What this means for the artist:**
- The work will be public from the start. Sketches, redirects, dead ends, breakthroughs may end up in a shuck.dev post, in the repo, or in a roundup. Anything you want to keep private, flag and we hold it.
- You are credited as a contributor, by name. Recognition compounds and you get yours.
- Decisions are explained, not announced. Push back where something feels wrong; the docs move when better arguments arrive.
