# Audio bible

A racquet, a ball, a counter that climbs. Late afternoon light, a friend at a small wooden stall watching the rally, glad to be there. The score sits inside that picture and inside the picture that pulls the protagonist out of it. Two halves, two voices, a long arc where one half learns the other is there.

This document is the why behind the score, written for the composer reading it for the first time. The world this music renders sits in [`../narrative/outline.md`](../narrative/outline.md) and [`../art/bible.md`](../art/bible.md); the setting it shares with both lives at [`../narrative/outline.md` § Setting](../narrative/outline.md#setting) and [`../art/bible.md` § 2](../art/bible.md#2-setting-and-period). When this document and the art or narrative bibles disagree on what a moment is, those bibles are canon and the audio bends to fit.

## Construction

The lulling half. The player is inside a constructed world the protagonist has built and tended, and they do not know it yet. The score is part of how the construct holds. Bright, propulsive, unbothered in the early venues; intense by the late ones; off the chain at the championship. The reference is Koji Kondo in the Mario Kart era: a score that hides what it is doing while it does it.

This is also the multi-genre half of the game. The principle: an under-utilised instrument carries the line and the piano answers it, or the inverse. The principle is settled; the instruments are not. Vibraphone, talk box, harp, a wild synth: prompts thrown across the table to Patrick, not picks. The choice is the composer's, made against what each candidate can actually do for the piece.

Each Construction venue gets a treatment. Same melodic material, different palette, different tempo, different instrument carrying the line. The garden sounds like late afternoon on a stone tile. The underwater venue, if that is the second leap, sounds like the same song heard through deep green water. Whatever the leap is, the score follows the art's leap into it. The bigger the wonder, the more elaborate the arrangement; the construction reaches further every time.

## Cracks

Reality leaks into Construction in two flavours, both authored, both deniable. The art bible at [`../art/bible.md` § 6](../art/bible.md#6-cracks-during-construction) holds the visual canon; audio carries its half of the same accumulation.

Tonal cracks sit inside the fiction. The unusual instrument that has been carrying the line slips a hair out of step with the piano. A held note in the venue's pad cools at its tail. A rest lands a beat late and the piano covers it. The warmth gets a touch too warm for the room. None of these read as a mistake on first encounter; the cumulative shape is what does the work.

Meta-contextual cracks sit outside the fiction. A music cue that skips. A track that loops a measure short. A pause-menu sting that does not match the previous visit. The art bible's UI flicker has an audio sibling: the surfaces around the world misbehaving in small, deniable ways.

The discipline is the same as the visual one. The leak is never literal. The Reality theme does not arrive in Construction during the cracks. What arrives is the Construction music starting to sound like it knows it is the Construction music.

The championship final is the one Construction venue where Reality leaks in early. Not as a quotation of the Reality theme, which the player has not heard yet, but as something in the arrangement that does not belong: a held interval that opens the wrong way, an instrument that arrives too acoustic for the room. The player should feel something is wrong before the prose says so.

## The break

The protagonist beats the champ. Both pulls of becoming-champ satisfy at the same beat and neither was the thing. The construct breaks open.

The Construction theme stops mid-phrase. The first thing the player hears after the break is silence and weather. The art bible at [`../art/bible.md` § 7](../art/bible.md#7-the-break) holds the visual canon for the rupture; the audio version is the absence of music against the air the visual gives it. Wind off the water. The breath of a room that has lost its centre.

The walk through the protagonist's hometown is the player's first sustained Reality. The score does not introduce the Reality theme here. The first Reality the player hears is a room: footsteps on a pavement, a closed shop door, a flap of awning, the sea somewhere behind it. The acoustic palette enters as room before it enters as music.

When the player returns to Construction the music returns too, and it is not the same music. The shopkeeper is gone from the stall and the rally's celebratory beat thins or falls silent in the garden. Other venues, with their coaches still present, keep their wind. The hollow lands in the score the way it lands in the picture.

## Reconstruction

The grounded half. The player moves between Construction and Reality, carrying things across, and the score moves with them.

Reality is acoustic, less intense, more room. The references are Joe Hisaishi and Philip Glass: scores that trust restraint to do what loudness cannot. The setting belongs to narrative; what audio takes from it is the room. The piano gets softer and sparser. Whatever electronic colour Construction had recedes. What carries the room in its place is open, and the room itself, the wind off the water and the bustle of an ordinary high street, is part of the answer.

The two leitmotifs do not work together at first. They are not supposed to. By Reconstruction they begin to find each other, the way a person who has been refusing to think about something starts to think about it. The first crossings are small. One phrase from the Construction theme arrives under a Reality instrumentation, or the inverse. Subsequent crossings get larger. The reconciliation is in the score because it is in the protagonist; the music tracks the acceptance, not the other way around.

Construction across Reconstruction holds its weathered version. Saturation drops a notch in the picture and a notch in the arrangement. The garden's Construction theme plays without the apex chime in the rallies the shopkeeper used to hold. The rally still rallies; the celebratory beat is the part that has gone quiet.

## The cliff and the call

The most loaded musical moment in the game. The art bible at [`../art/bible.md` § 9](../art/bible.md#9-the-cliff-and-the-call) and the narrative outline both hold the canon: a worn ledge, a bench dedicated to the friend who died, the shopkeeper alive on it, the protagonist on the path with their phone.

The approach is thin. Not silence; the cliff has its own room. Wind, sea, the small acoustic presence of a place people came to. The Reality palette at its sparsest. The protagonist sees the shopkeeper. Recognition lands. The dial happens.

This is where the two themes meet for real. The phone rings on the bench. The shopkeeper picks up. The score does what the dial does: holds both intentions in one frame. A phrase from the Construction theme under the Reality palette, or the inverse, played the way two people who have not spoken in a long time finally speak. Restraint is what carries it. The full reconciliation is not yet; the reach is.

The call ends and the protagonist crosses the few steps to the bench. The score sits with them.

## Credits and postgame

Credits play over a rally. The protagonist on the left, the shopkeeper on the right, the first volley between them. The art bible at [`../art/bible.md` § 10](../art/bible.md#10-credits-and-postgame) holds the picture: Construction's saturated light returned, with the texture Reality has worn into it across Reconstruction.

The audio sits in synthesis at exactly the visual moment of synthesis. The synth warmth that opened the game returns layered with the acoustic instruments Reality brought. The two themes that did not know each other in Part 1 share a single arrangement now. The reconciliation the score has been working toward across the arc is the credits' job to land.

Postgame is rallying with the shopkeeper at the championship spot. The cliff stays returnable through the now-unlocked gate, with no new gameplay. The score keeps both palettes available; the player can hear either depending on where they stand. Neither is the substitute any more.

## Form

The piano reduction is the compromise for now. Patrick's natural instrument is piano and the project does not yet have the surface to commission a full ensemble, so the music gets written and shipped at the keyboard first. The reduction has to hold up alone, because for a stretch of the project it is the only version that exists.

Long term the score is multi-genre and the unusual instruments do real work. The reduction is the seed of that work, not the destination. As the project grows, the palette branches out from the keyboard, and the bible's job is to keep the principle steady while the palette changes.

## What we are not

Not orchestral by default. Not "epic." Not Hollywood-trailer. Not generic chiptune for Construction or generic ambient for Reality. The Construction half earns its intensity through unusual instrumentation, not through scale. The Reality half earns its quiet through restraint, not through pad-and-hold. The break is silence and weather, not a stinger. The cliff is small acoustic presence, not a swell. The credits are the synthesis the arc has earned, not a finale that arrives because the credits arrived.

If a cue starts to feel like it belongs in a different game, that is the signal to throw it out and start again.
